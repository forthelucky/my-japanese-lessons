### Example Sentences

**1. technocrat**
> Speer was the ideal Nazi technocrat: technically brilliant, organisationally gifted, politically naive (or so he claimed), and willing to subordinate all ethical considerations to the achievement of measurable production targets.

**2. forced labour**
> Speer's production miracle depended fundamentally on forced labour: by 1944, over half the workers in the German armaments industry were prisoners, slave labourers from occupied territories, or concentration camp inmates.

**3. armaments production**
> Between 1942 and 1944, under Speer's direction, German armaments production tripled — an achievement all the more astonishing given that it occurred under continuous Allied bombing that Speer countered through dispersed production and rapid repair.

**4. moral compartmentalisation**
> Speer's defence at Nuremberg was essentially a plea for moral compartmentalisation: he was a professional architect and production manager, he argued, not a political figure, and could not be held responsible for how the regime used the products he organised.

**5. war crimes**
> The International Military Tribunal at Nuremberg convicted Speer of war crimes and crimes against humanity — specifically, his use of slave labour — and sentenced him to twenty years in prison, rather than death, partly because of his claimed opposition to Hitler's scorched-earth orders at the war's end.

**6. rationalisation**
> Speer's post-war memoir "Inside the Third Reich" was a masterpiece of rationalisation: it presented him as a decent man who had been seduced by Hitler's charisma and had gradually become aware of the regime's crimes, while carefully obscuring the evidence of his direct complicity.

**7. complicity**
> The historical evidence of Speer's complicity in the Holocaust is now well-documented: he attended the Posen conference in 1943 where Himmler explicitly described the extermination of the Jews, attended not as a passive witness but as an active participant in the regime's leadership.

## 📝 Part 2 — Reading Passage

---

### The Man Who Made the Machinery Run
#### Albert Speer and the Morality of Professional Efficiency

Albert Speer was, by any professional measure, a brilliant man. As Hitler's personal architect, he had designed the Nuremberg rally grounds and planned a rebuilt Berlin of monumental proportions. As Reich Minister of Armaments, he took a German war economy that was producing tanks at a rate that could not sustain the Eastern Front and transformed it, in three years, into the most productive armaments system in Europe — despite bombing, material shortages, and the diversion of resources to the Holocaust.

He was also, almost certainly, a war criminal who spent the rest of his life constructing an elaborate lie.

Speer's post-war persona — the good Nazi, the professional who had been seduced by Hitler's vision, who had tried to resist the worst excesses, who had not known about the death camps — was not merely self-serving; it was extraordinarily effective. It made him the most published and most cited source on the Nazi leadership, the man who provided the post-war world with its intimate narrative of the Third Reich. For decades, it was largely believed.

The historical revision began in the 1990s. Documents established that Speer had been at the Posen conference where Himmler described the extermination programme explicitly. His use of forced labour — including from concentration camps — was documented in his own ministry's files. The "I didn't know" defence collapsed under the weight of his own records.

What Speer represents is the most disturbing version of a very common phenomenon: the professional who performs his function with exceptional skill, allowing the institution that employs him to serve terrible ends, while telling himself — and others — that his technical expertise makes him politically and morally neutral. It is a story that did not end at Nuremberg, and has not ended since.

## ✏️ Part 3 — Vocabulary in Use

**1.** Use **"technocrat"** to discuss whether technical expertise ever makes a professional morally neutral — or whether expertise always implies some responsibility for how it is applied.

**2.** Use **"forced labour"** to discuss the relationship between Speer's production "miracle" and the system of exploitation that made it possible.

**3.** Use **"armaments production"** to evaluate Speer's achievement. Is it possible to admire the organisational genius while condemning the moral framework?

**4.** Use **"moral compartmentalisation"** to describe a contemporary situation where professionals separate their work from its ethical implications. Is this psychologically possible, and is it ever justified?

**5.** Use **"war crimes"** to discuss the standard by which Nuremberg judged individual responsibility. Was the standard applied consistently?

**6.** Use **"rationalisation"** to discuss how Speer's memoir served his interests. What does it tell us about the reliability of memoir as a historical source?

**7.** Use **"complicity"** to argue where Speer's responsibility began. Was he complicit because he knew, because he organised labour for the regime, or because he was a visible symbol of the regime's legitimacy?

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** Speer claimed he "didn't know" about the death camps. The historical evidence shows this was false. What motivated the lie, and what enabled it to be believed for so long?

**2.** The text argues Speer represents "the professional who performs his function with exceptional skill, allowing the institution to serve terrible ends." How common is this dynamic, and how does it operate in contemporary professional life?

**3.** Speer was spared execution partly because he claimed to have opposed Hitler's scorched-earth orders at the war's end. Is this a sufficient moral distinction to justify different treatment?

**4.** Speer's memoir became the dominant insider account of the Nazi leadership. What are the consequences for historical understanding when a morally compromised actor becomes the primary source?

**5.** Can brilliant resource management and professional efficiency ever be detached from the morality of the goal they serve?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** The Speer defence — "I was just doing my job" / "I didn't know" — has appeared in multiple post-war contexts. How should institutions and societies evaluate this defence?

**2.** Speer was educated, cultured, and aesthetically sophisticated — an admirer of art and architecture. Does this complicate the common assumption that education and culture provide protection against participation in atrocity?

**3.** The industrial model of mass murder — systematic, logistical, bureaucratic — that Himmler implemented and Speer's labour system supplied has been called uniquely modern. What does it tell us about the relationship between modernity and evil?

**4.** Korean forced labourers — brought to Japan and German-occupied territories during WWII — experienced the system Speer administered from the victim's perspective. How does this historical connection affect the evaluation of Speer?

**5.** Contemporary corporations sometimes discover that their supply chains involve forced labour or serious human rights abuses. How does the Speer case inform our thinking about corporate responsibility for supply chain practices?
