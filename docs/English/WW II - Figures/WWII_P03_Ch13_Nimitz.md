## 📖 Part 1 — Key Vocabulary

| # | Word / Phrase | Part of Speech | Korean | Definition |
|---|--------------|---------------|--------|------------|
| 1 | **intelligence-driven** | adjective | 정보 기반의 | Decisions or operations based primarily on systematic analysis of available intelligence |
| 2 | **calculated risk** | noun | 계산된 위험 | A risk accepted after careful evaluation of the probability of success and the consequences of failure |

### Example Sentences

**1. intelligence-driven**
> Nimitz's decision to concentrate his carriers near Midway in June 1942 was an intelligence-driven gamble of extraordinary boldness — he was staking his entire carrier fleet on the accuracy of Joe Rochefort's code-breaking estimate of Japanese intentions.

**2. calculated risk**
> The calculated risk at Midway — three carriers against four, waiting in ambush — exemplifies the difference between a reckless gamble (ignoring the odds) and a calculated risk (accepting the odds after determining that the alternative is worse).

**3. fleet-in-being**
> After Pearl Harbor, Nimitz had to balance the fleet-in-being strategy — preserving his remaining carriers as a deterrent force — against the need to contest Japanese expansion aggressively enough to prevent further strategic losses.

**4. theatre command**
> The division of Pacific theatre command between Nimitz (Navy) and MacArthur (Army) was a compromise between institutional egos that created genuine strategic inefficiency, requiring constant coordination between two commanders who often disagreed.

**5. servant leadership**
> Nimitz exemplified servant leadership in a military context: he selected excellent commanders and gave them genuine authority to operate, visited ships and units rather than demanding that subordinates come to him, and publicly credited his team for successes while privately addressing failures.

**6. rebuilding capacity**
> The rebuilding capacity of the US Navy after Pearl Harbor was remarkable: within six months of losing eight battleships and suffering 2,000 casualties, Nimitz's forces won the Battle of Midway, the decisive turning point of the Pacific War.

**7. decisive engagement**
> Midway was the decisive engagement of the Pacific War: Japan lost four irreplaceable fleet carriers and most of their best-trained naval aviators, a loss they could never fully recover from, while the US lost one carrier and maintained the strategic initiative.

## 📝 Part 2 — Reading Passage

### The Quiet Indispensable
#### Chester Nimitz and the Leadership of Competence

Chester Nimitz arrived at Pearl Harbor on Christmas Day 1941, three weeks after the attack. What he found was devastation — physically, militarily, and psychologically. The Pacific Fleet's battleships were at the bottom of the harbour. The officers who had commanded them faced potential courts-martial. The men under his command were demoralised, uncertain, and afraid.

Nimitz's first act was characteristically quiet: he walked the harbour, made clear that he found no grounds for the proposed courts-martial, and announced his intention to use what remained of the fleet aggressively rather than defensively. He was not a charismatic speaker. He did not give rousing speeches. He simply communicated, by his presence and his decisions, that he knew what he was doing and that they would go forward.

What made Nimitz exceptional was a combination of qualities that tend to go unrecognised in military history, which tends to favour the dramatic: intellectual rigour, the ability to evaluate intelligence without being seduced by what he wished were true, and a genuine talent for selecting excellent subordinates and giving them the authority to operate. He trusted Joe Rochefort's intelligence assessment about Midway — against the advice of colleagues who doubted it — and staked his entire carrier force on being right.

The Battle of Midway, fought in June 1942, was the most consequential naval battle of the Pacific War. In four days, Japan lost four fleet carriers, 250 aircraft, and the core of its trained aviator corps. The US lost one carrier. The strategic initiative in the Pacific shifted permanently. Nimitz won Midway not by tactical brilliance in the battle itself — the American attack was technically poorly coordinated — but by being in exactly the right position, at exactly the right moment, with intelligence that told him exactly what Yamamoto intended.

Nimitz's post-war years were characterised by the same quiet competence: he refused to take personal credit for Midway, crediting instead the intelligence officers and the pilots. He was, in the words of one biographer, "a man without vanity in a profession that tends to produce vanity in abundance."

## ✏️ Part 3 — Vocabulary in Use

**1.** Use **"intelligence-driven"** to discuss the relationship between intelligence quality and decision-making quality. What happens when intelligence is wrong?

**2.** Use **"calculated risk"** to explain the specific calculation Nimitz made at Midway. What were the odds, and why did he decide they were acceptable?

**3.** Use **"fleet-in-being"** to discuss the strategic dilemma of preserving resources versus using them. When is preservation the right strategy?

**4.** Use **"theatre command"** to discuss the problem of divided command in the Pacific. Could better institutional design have avoided this problem?

**5.** Use **"servant leadership"** to argue whether Nimitz's leadership style — supporting subordinates, distributing credit — can work in all organisational contexts, or whether it requires specific conditions.

**6.** Use **"rebuilding capacity"** to discuss what enables rapid recovery from catastrophic setback. What organisational conditions made the US Navy's post-Pearl Harbor recovery possible?

**7.** Use **"decisive engagement"** to explain why Midway was decisive rather than merely important. What made the losses irreplaceable for Japan?

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** Nimitz trusted Rochefort's intelligence assessment against the advice of colleagues who doubted it. How do decision-makers evaluate the credibility of intelligence sources when the stakes are high and time is limited?

**2.** The text says Nimitz was "a man without vanity in a profession that tends to produce vanity in abundance." Why does military command tend to produce vanity? What counteracts it?

**3.** Nimitz's first act at Pearl Harbor was to indicate there would be no courts-martial for the officers on duty during the attack. Was this the right call? What does it tell us about his leadership priorities?

**4.** The American attack at Midway was "technically poorly coordinated" but won because of positioning based on intelligence. What does this tell us about the relative importance of positioning versus execution?

**5.** Contrast Nimitz's quiet leadership with MacArthur's showmanship. Which style is more effective in a crisis?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** Nimitz has received less historical attention than MacArthur or Patton, despite arguably being more consequential. How does personality and self-promotion affect historical memory?

**2.** The US rebuilt its Pacific Fleet with astonishing speed after Pearl Harbor — a feat of industrial and logistical organisation made possible by specific American economic conditions. What lessons does this hold for contemporary crisis response?

**3.** Korea's security depends on the US Navy's ability to operate in the Pacific. How has the legacy of the Pacific War shaped the strategic relationship between the US Navy and Korea?

**4.** Nimitz trusted his intelligence. The failure to anticipate Pearl Harbor was also an intelligence failure. What distinguishes intelligence success from intelligence failure — luck, method, or institutional culture?

**5.** The Pacific War produced two very different American military heroes — the theatrical MacArthur and the quiet Nimitz. Which model of leadership do you think is more valuable, and why?
