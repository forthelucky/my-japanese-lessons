### Example Sentences

**1. tactical brilliance**
> Rommel's tactical brilliance in North Africa — moving by night, attacking where the enemy least expected, reading terrain instinctively — repeatedly defeated British forces that significantly outnumbered and outgunned him.

**2. logistics**
> Rommel's relationship with logistics was one of deliberate inattention: he was willing to advance until fuel ran out, counting on captured enemy stores to sustain his momentum — a method that worked brilliantly until it didn't.

**3. supply line**
> The fundamental vulnerability of Rommel's North Africa campaign was its supply line: everything had to cross the Mediterranean under Allied air and naval attack, and the further he advanced from Tripoli, the longer and more exposed that line became.

**4. charismatic leadership**
> Rommel led from the front — physically present in the most dangerous places, sharing his soldiers' risks — and this charismatic leadership style generated extraordinary loyalty and willingness to follow him into situations that would have produced refusal under a more distant commander.

**5. strategic overreach**
> Rommel's advance to El Alamein in summer 1942 was a case of strategic overreach: having outrun his supply line and exhausted his forces, he reached a point where he could not advance, could not consolidate, and could not retreat quickly enough.

**6. Auftragstaktik**
> The German Auftragstaktik tradition — tell subordinates what to achieve, not how to achieve it — suited Rommel perfectly: he operated with remarkable autonomy and in return gave his own subordinates the same freedom.

**7. posthumous reputation**
> Rommel's posthumous reputation as the "chivalrous" German general — who allegedly disobeyed orders to kill POWs and Jewish civilians — was partly constructed by the Western Allies after the war to create a usable German military hero for rearmament purposes.

## 📝 Part 2 — Reading Passage

### The Desert Fox and His Limits
#### Erwin Rommel and the Paradox of Tactical Mastery Without Strategic Vision

Erwin Rommel arrived in North Africa in February 1941 with orders to shore up the collapsing Italian position in Libya. Within weeks, ignoring explicit instructions not to advance, he had launched an offensive that drove British forces back to the Egyptian border. He had arrived as a lieutenant-general commanding a handful of German divisions; within a year he was a field marshal whose name was spoken with admiration in British headquarters.

Rommel was a commander of rare gifts. He had extraordinary spatial intuition — the ability to read terrain, anticipate enemy movements, and identify the precise point of vulnerability in an enemy's disposition. He was physically brave to the point of recklessness, habitually present at the decisive point of any action. His soldiers adored him. His opponents feared him. The British public named him the "Desert Fox" — an almost affectionate acknowledgment of a dangerous enemy.

But Rommel had a blind spot that ultimately proved fatal to his campaign. He was, at his core, a brilliant tactician who had been promoted to the level of strategic command for which his particular talents were poorly suited. He could win battles magnificently; he could not manage the logistical system that would have needed to support a genuinely strategic victory. He repeatedly advanced beyond what his supply lines could sustain, counting on captured fuel and captured ammunition to maintain momentum. Sometimes this worked. At El Alamein in October 1942, it did not.

There is also the moral question. Rommel's reputation as a "clean general" — one who fought honourably and refused to implement the regime's criminal orders — has been substantially complicated by post-war research. He knew about the Holocaust. His campaigns depended on the same Nazi war machine whose crimes he is supposed to have deplored. His involvement in the July 1944 plot to assassinate Hitler was marginal and late.

He was given the choice of a show trial or suicide by poison. He chose the poison. He was buried with full military honours.

## ✏️ Part 3 — Vocabulary in Use

**1.** Use **"tactical brilliance"** to explain the distinction between tactical skill and strategic thinking. Why are they so often found separately rather than together?

**2.** Use **"logistics"** to make the argument that logistics — not tactics or strategy — is the actual determinant of most military outcomes.

**3.** Use **"supply line"** to describe a situation in any field (business, politics, sport) where the "supply line" — the infrastructure behind the front line — ultimately determined the outcome.

**4.** Use **"charismatic leadership"** to discuss the risks as well as the benefits of a leadership style based on personal presence and front-line visibility.

**5.** Use **"strategic overreach"** to describe a contemporary or historical situation where an organisation or nation advanced beyond what it could sustain.

**6.** Use **"Auftragstaktik"** to discuss the advantages and risks of delegating objectives without specifying methods. When does this style work, and when does it break down?

**7.** Use **"posthumous reputation"** to discuss how the historical record about a controversial figure can be shaped by political interests after their death.

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** Rommel repeatedly "ignored explicit instructions not to advance." He was initially vindicated by results. How should organisations handle subordinates who disobey orders and succeed?

**2.** The text argues that Rommel was "a brilliant tactician promoted to the level of strategic command for which his particular talents were poorly suited." Is this a failure of Rommel, or of the system that promoted him?

**3.** Rommel's reputation as a "clean general" has been "substantially complicated" by post-war research. What does this tell us about the relationship between military reputation and historical truth?

**4.** Rommel's "charismatic leadership" style — leading from the front — generated exceptional loyalty. What are the costs of a leadership model so dependent on personal presence?

**5.** Why does brilliant tactical execution eventually fail if the underlying supply chain and logistics are ignored?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** Rommel is often discussed as an example of separating military skill from political morality — admiring the general without endorsing the cause. Is this separation intellectually coherent, or does it involve a kind of self-deception?

**2.** Rommel's North Africa campaign was ultimately defeated by British intelligence (Ultra) as much as by British arms. How does this change the moral calculus of his military legacy?

**3.** "Supply chain" has become one of the dominant strategic concerns of the 21st century — in manufacturing, in national security, in pandemic response. What lessons from Rommel's logistical failures apply to these contemporary contexts?

**4.** Rommel was given the choice of execution or suicide. He chose suicide to protect his family. How do we evaluate a man who, facing a monstrous regime, chose personal and family survival over public defiance?

**5.** Some argue that the Western focus on Rommel as the "honourable enemy" reflects a racialised preference for European military opponents over Asian or colonial ones. Do you think this critique is valid?
