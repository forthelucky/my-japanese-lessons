### Example Sentences

**1. government-in-exile**
> De Gaulle's government-in-exile had no territory, no treasury, and initially no army — only a BBC broadcast, a flag, and one general who absolutely refused to accept that France had been defeated.

**2. legitimacy**
> The question of legitimacy ran through everything de Gaulle did: he had to convince the French people, the British, the Americans, and eventually the French Resistance that Free France — not the Vichy government — was the true France.

**3. Resistance**
> The French Resistance was heterogeneous — communist, socialist, Gaullist, Catholic, secular — and required de Gaulle's considerable diplomatic skill to coordinate toward common purpose while preventing any single faction from dominating.

**4. collaborationism**
> The collaborationism of the Vichy government — working with Germany not merely to survive but actively implementing anti-Jewish legislation and providing intelligence to the Gestapo — created a wound in French national identity that has not fully healed.

**5. symbolic leadership**
> De Gaulle understood symbolic leadership with unique clarity: the June 18 radio address from London was not a military command but a symbolic act — the declaration that France still existed as a moral and political entity, regardless of what its government had done.

**6. brinkmanship**
> De Gaulle's brinkmanship with Churchill and Roosevelt was extraordinary given his complete lack of material leverage: he repeatedly threatened to withdraw French cooperation unless his demands were met — and was usually believed, because his commitment to French dignity was clearly genuine.

**7. national narrative**
> De Gaulle's greatest post-war political achievement was creating a national narrative of France as a nation of resisters rather than collaborators — a narrative that was, at best, a selective truth but that served the purpose of rebuilding national cohesion.

### The Voice That Refused to Fall Silent
#### Charles de Gaulle and the Power of Symbolic Leadership

On June 18, 1940, a relatively unknown French general broadcast a three-minute speech on the BBC from London. France had just surrendered. The French government had signed an armistice with Germany. The French army had been defeated. The French political class had, with few exceptions, accepted that the war was over and France's future lay in accommodation with the Nazi new order.

Charles de Gaulle, then a brigadier-general and undersecretary of defence, refused. His June 18 broadcast — "Has the last word been said? Must hope disappear? Is the defeat final? No!" — was heard by very few French people at the time. But its significance was total. De Gaulle had declared that France was not its government, not its territory, not its army — but an idea, which could not be defeated because ideas cannot surrender.

What followed was one of the most remarkable diplomatic and political performances in modern history. De Gaulle had nothing: no troops, no money, no territory, no popular mandate. Against this, he deployed an absolute conviction that he was France, and a willingness to take the brinkmanship required to make powerful men — Churchill, Roosevelt, Stalin — treat him as if he were. He annoyed Churchill so persistently and so productively that the Prime Minister once complained: "The heaviest cross I have to bear is the Cross of Lorraine." He refused to allow American troops to bypass Paris in the Liberation, insisting on French forces entering the capital first. He negotiated a French sector in the German occupation and a permanent seat on the UN Security Council — for a country that had been militarily irrelevant for four years.

The post-war narrative de Gaulle constructed — that France had largely resisted, that the Resistance represented the true France, that collaboration was a deviation — was mythologised history. It was also necessary history: a France that fully confronted the extent of its collaboration in 1945 might not have had the political cohesion to rebuild. De Gaulle understood that national renewal sometimes requires a national story that is more aspirational than accurate.

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** The text says de Gaulle understood that national renewal "requires a national story that is more aspirational than accurate." Is this a pragmatic wisdom or a form of dishonesty? What are the long-term costs of building national identity on myth?

**2.** De Gaulle annoyed Churchill so persistently — and successfully — that Churchill once said the heaviest cross he bore was the Cross of Lorraine. What does this tell us about the strategic value of making yourself indispensably annoying?

**3.** De Gaulle insisted on French forces entering Paris first during the Liberation. Was this petty national vanity, or a legitimate assertion of French political agency?

**4.** De Gaulle had "an absolute conviction that he was France." Is this level of self-identification with a nation a psychological disorder, or a genuine leadership quality?

**5.** How can a leader without any physical resources or leverage still negotiate and maintain power through sheer "legitimacy"?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** De Gaulle's post-war political career — including his creation of the Fifth Republic, his withdrawal from NATO's military command, and his recognition of China — reflected a consistent French commitment to strategic independence. How do you evaluate this posture from a small or medium power's perspective?

**2.** Korea has its own experience of government-in-exile — the Korean Provisional Government was established in Shanghai in 1919 and later operated from Chongqing. How does the Korean experience compare to de Gaulle's Free France?

**3.** The Vichy government's collaboration with Nazi Germany — including the deportation of French Jews — was for decades partially suppressed in French public memory. How should nations process the most uncomfortable aspects of their own history?

**4.** De Gaulle's insistence on French grandeur and independence was partly responsible for the creation of a European alternative to American hegemony. How do you evaluate his legacy in the context of contemporary EU-US relations?

**5.** De Gaulle was a man of the political right who led a coalition that included the communist Resistance. What does this coalition tell us about the nature of national struggle?
