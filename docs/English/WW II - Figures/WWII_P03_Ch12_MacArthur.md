### Example Sentences

**1. island hopping**
> The island hopping strategy MacArthur and Nimitz developed — bypassing Rabaul, Truk, and other heavily fortified positions rather than assaulting them directly — saved potentially hundreds of thousands of lives while advancing the Allied perimeter toward Japan.

**2. showmanship**
> MacArthur's showmanship was legendary: his return to the Philippines was staged as a theatrical event, wading ashore from a landing craft with reporters present, even requiring multiple takes of the wade to achieve the right photographs.

**3. insubordination**
> MacArthur's insubordination during the Korean War — publicly contradicting President Truman's strategic policy and directly communicating with congressional leaders — was so flagrant that even his admirers acknowledged Truman had no choice but to relieve him.

**4. occupation administration**
> MacArthur's occupation administration of Japan was one of the most consequential acts of post-war governance: he imposed a new constitution, broke up the zaibatsu industrial cartels, established land reform, and demilitarised the armed forces — transforming Japan in five years.

**5. strategic bypass**
> The strategic bypass of Rabaul — once considered requiring a direct assault that might cost 50,000 casualties — demonstrated that intelligence-driven selection of where not to fight was as important as tactical skill in where and how to fight.

**6. personal mythology**
> MacArthur curated his personal mythology with meticulous attention: the corncob pipe, the aviator sunglasses, the theatrical pauses before speaking, the famous phrases — all were elements of a carefully maintained image rather than spontaneous expressions of personality.

**7. demilitarisation**
> The demilitarisation of Japan under MacArthur's administration was so thorough — Article 9 of the new constitution renounced war as an instrument of state policy — that it created strategic complications when the Korean War began and the US needed Japan as a base.

## 📝 Part 2 — Reading Passage

---

### The Emperor of the Pacific
#### Douglas MacArthur and the Performance of Greatness

Douglas MacArthur was, in the judgment of most military historians, both a genuine military genius and a profoundly dangerous egomaniac — and the two qualities were so thoroughly intertwined that separating them is essentially impossible.

His military accomplishments were real. The island hopping strategy he developed with Chester Nimitz transformed the Pacific War, advancing the Allied perimeter thousands of miles at a fraction of the cost a conventional island-by-island assault would have required. His return to the Philippines, the liberation of Luzon, and the management of the Pacific campaigns demonstrated operational imagination of a high order. His occupation of Japan — five years of governance that transformed one of history's most militarist states into a peaceful democracy — was an administrative achievement with few parallels in modern history.

But MacArthur's relationship with reality — specifically, with the reality that he served civilian authorities who outranked him — was a lifelong source of institutional damage. He delayed evacuation from the Philippines longer than was wise, in part because it required admitting that the position was untenable. He managed public relations as carefully as he managed military operations, ensuring that his name appeared prominently in all press coverage of the Pacific campaign. He maintained a personal staff that functioned partly as a public relations operation.

The fundamental problem was that MacArthur appeared to believe that he was the war — that the institutional structures he operated within existed to serve his vision rather than the reverse. This worked within the constraints of the Pacific War, where his results were good enough that superiors tolerated his behaviour. It did not survive the Korean War, where his public challenge to civilian authority crossed a line that even his admirers acknowledged could not be permitted.

President Truman relieved him in April 1951. MacArthur returned home to a hero's welcome and a famous speech to Congress: "Old soldiers never die; they just fade away." He faded away.

**1.** Use **"island hopping"** to explain the strategic logic of bypassing strong points rather than assaulting them. What conditions make this approach feasible?

**2.** Use **"showmanship"** to discuss whether theatrical self-presentation in a leader is manipulative, or whether it serves a genuine function for morale and authority.

**3.** Use **"insubordination"** to discuss the conditions under which disobeying orders from superiors is justified, and the conditions under which it is simply self-serving.

**4.** Use **"occupation administration"** to evaluate MacArthur's governance of Japan. Was it a genuine transformation or an imposition?

**5.** Use **"strategic bypass"** to describe a situation in business or personal life where avoiding the strongest opposition was more effective than confronting it directly.

**6.** Use **"personal mythology"** to discuss the role of self-narrative in leadership. How much of leadership is actually the performance of leadership?

**7.** Use **"demilitarisation"** to discuss the long-term effects of Article 9 — Japan's constitutional renunciation of war. Has it served Japan well?

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** The text argues MacArthur believed he was "the war" — that institutions existed to serve his vision. How do organisations handle leaders who genuinely produce results but treat institutional constraints as obstacles?

**2.** MacArthur's occupation of Japan is described as transformative. Was this transformation imposed or negotiated? Does the distinction matter for its legitimacy?

**3.** MacArthur publicly challenged civilian authority in Korea, knowing it might cost him his command. What was he calculating? Was it professional courage, political ambition, or simple hubris?

**4.** Truman's decision to fire MacArthur — a popular hero — at great political cost to himself was a principled defence of civilian control of the military. How important is this principle, and how fragile is it?

**5.** Does extreme arrogance in a leader blind them to risks, or is it a necessary trait to command massive armies?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** Japan's post-war constitution, written under MacArthur's occupation, has shaped the country for eighty years. Korea has significant interest in Japan's pacifist constitution given the history of Japanese militarism. How do you view Article 9 from a Korean perspective?

**2.** MacArthur commanded Allied forces at Inchon in 1950 — one of the most brilliant amphibious operations in military history. His subsequent march to the Yalu provoked Chinese intervention and nearly catastrophic defeat. How do you evaluate his Korean War record in light of the South Korean experience?

**3.** The Korean War is sometimes called the "Forgotten War" in American memory, overshadowed by WWII. Is it forgotten in Korea? How does the Korean memory of this war differ from the American one?

**4.** MacArthur was the effective ruler of Japan for five years. The Japanese constitution he imposed is still in force. Should a defeated people accept a constitution written by the occupying power?

**5.** MacArthur's dismissal was constitutional but politically damaging to Truman. How should democracies balance the principle of civilian control of the military with the political reality that popular generals often have more public support than the presidents who command them?
