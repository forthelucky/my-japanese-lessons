### Example Sentences

**1. carrier-based warfare**
> Yamamoto understood before most of his contemporaries that carrier-based warfare had made the battleship — the traditional symbol of naval power — obsolete as an offensive weapon. Pearl Harbor proved his point; ironically, the American carriers he most needed to destroy were at sea that day.

**2. preemptive strike**
> The Pearl Harbor preemptive strike achieved everything it was designed to achieve in tactical terms — it was one of the most precisely executed surprise attacks in military history — yet it achieved almost nothing it needed to achieve strategically.

**3. industrial capacity**
> Yamamoto had lived in the United States and understood its industrial capacity with a clarity few Japanese leaders shared. He told the naval ministry bluntly that he could "run wild for six months or a year," but could not guarantee the outcome of a longer war.

**4. calculated gamble**
> Midway was Yamamoto's final calculated gamble — an attempt to draw out and destroy the US carrier fleet that he had failed to catch at Pearl Harbor. When American code-breakers revealed his plan in advance, the gamble's odds collapsed.

**5. operational surprise**
> The extraordinary operational surprise of December 7, 1941 — 353 aircraft appearing over Oahu before dawn — was the product of months of meticulous planning, radio silence, and a northern Pacific approach route that US intelligence did not anticipate.

**6. code-breaking**
> The code-breaking that enabled the ambush of Yamamoto's inspection flight in April 1943 — his aircraft was shot down after US code-breakers identified his precise itinerary — was the ultimate intelligence victory of the Pacific War.

**7. fatal overconfidence**
> The Japanese defeat at Midway was largely a product of fatal overconfidence: having defeated the US at Pearl Harbor, at Malaya, at the Philippines, Japanese commanders could not seriously imagine that the Americans were ready to fight effectively.

## 📝 Part 2 — Reading Passage

---

### The Reluctant Gambler
#### Isoroku Yamamoto and the Contradiction of Brilliant Defeat

Isoroku Yamamoto is one of the most paradoxical figures in military history: the man who planned the attack that brought the United States into the war was also the man who most clearly foresaw that the war it started was unwinnable.

Yamamoto was a product of the Imperial Japanese Navy's elite — trained at the Naval Academy, later a student at Harvard and an attaché in Washington, an expert poker player and gambler who understood risk and probability with unusual sophistication. His time in America had given him a precise appreciation of American industrial power. He returned from Washington convinced that Japan could not win a prolonged war with the United States. He said so, repeatedly and bluntly, to anyone who would listen.

No one changed course. Japan was committed to its imperial trajectory in China and Southeast Asia. The oil embargo imposed by the United States in 1941 made war seem inevitable to the military leadership. When Yamamoto could not prevent the war, he decided to shape it — designing an attack so devastating that it would either force the US to negotiate or buy Japan enough time to consolidate its gains.

The attack on Pearl Harbor was a tactical masterpiece. The planning — coordinating six carriers, 353 aircraft, midget submarines, and a nine-carrier screening force across a 4,000-mile voyage in complete radio silence — was an achievement of operational planning that military historians still admire. The execution on December 7, 1941 was nearly flawless.

But the strategic objectives were not achieved. The American aircraft carriers were at sea. The Pearl Harbor oil storage facilities — which would have been far more damaging to destroy than the battleships — were untouched. And the attack produced exactly the national unity that Yamamoto had feared: a sleeping giant woken not into negotiation but into industrial fury.

Yamamoto died in April 1943, his aircraft shot down over Bougainville after American code-breakers decrypted his inspection itinerary. The man who had planned the greatest naval surprise attack in history was killed by the greatest intelligence operation of the Pacific War.

## ✏️ Part 3 — Vocabulary in Use

**1.** Use **"carrier-based warfare"** to discuss how Yamamoto's adoption of a new military paradigm before most of his contemporaries gave Japan early advantages — and how this same paradigm was used against Japan at Midway.

**2.** Use **"preemptive strike"** to discuss whether the Pearl Harbor attack achieved its goals. What would "success" have looked like?

**3.** Use **"industrial capacity"** to explain why Yamamoto believed Japan could not win a long war. Was he right?

**4.** Use **"calculated gamble"** to discuss the ethics of committing a nation to a war you believe is likely to be lost.

**5.** Use **"operational surprise"** to discuss the relationship between tactical success (Pearl Harbor) and strategic failure (the decision to attack).

**6.** Use **"code-breaking"** to discuss the relationship between intelligence superiority and military outcome. How decisive was it in the Pacific War?

**7.** Use **"fatal overconfidence"** to analyse the Japanese military's psychological state after their string of early victories. What cognitive mechanisms drive this kind of overconfidence?

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** Yamamoto knew Japan could not win a long war with the US. He planned Pearl Harbor anyway. Is this contradiction the product of personal courage, institutional loyalty, professional duty, or something else?

**2.** The Pearl Harbor attack was a "tactical masterpiece" but a "strategic failure." How often does this mismatch occur — where the execution is excellent but the goal is misconceived?

**3.** The oil storage facilities at Pearl Harbor were not targeted, despite being far more strategically important than the battleships. Why might excellent planners focus on the wrong targets?

**4.** Yamamoto was killed because his flight plan was decoded. He knew the risks of the inspection flight. What does this tell us about the psychological relationship between leaders and personal risk?

**5.** If you know a venture is statistically likely to fail, how do you justify taking the ultimate high-risk gamble?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** Yamamoto is remembered as a military genius who served an unjust cause. How do we separate professional admiration from moral evaluation when discussing military figures?

**2.** Japan's decision to attack Pearl Harbor was driven partly by the oil embargo — itself a response to Japanese aggression in China. How do we trace the chain of causation in an arms spiral or escalation cycle?

**3.** Korea experienced the Pacific War as a colonial subject of Japan — many Koreans were conscripted into the Imperial forces. How does this complicate the evaluation of Japanese military figures like Yamamoto?

**4.** The Pacific War's beginning and end — Pearl Harbor and the atomic bombs — were both surprise attacks of enormous strategic consequence. What does this symmetry tell us about the nature of the Pacific War?

**5.** Yamamoto's poker skills and his understanding of probability inform his military planning. What disciplines outside of military science should military strategists study?
