### Example Sentences

**1. manifest destiny**
> The phrase "manifest destiny" was coined by journalist John O'Sullivan in 1845 to describe the conviction that Providence had ordained American expansion across the continent — a belief that provided moral cover for what was, in practice, conquest.

**2. displacement**
> The Trail of Tears refers to the forced displacement of roughly sixty thousand Cherokee, Choctaw, and other southeastern Native Americans between 1830 and 1850, during which an estimated fifteen thousand people died of cold, hunger, and disease.

**3. assimilation**
> Federal policy alternated between violent removal and forced assimilation — the latter taking the form of boarding schools where Native American children were stripped of their languages and cultural identities.

**4. treaty**
> The United States made and broke hundreds of treaties with Native American nations over the course of the nineteenth century; by the 1880s, almost every treaty that guaranteed indigenous land rights had been unilaterally violated.

**5. reservation**
> The reservation system concentrated Native American populations on lands typically chosen for their lack of agricultural value, separating peoples from the territories, resources, and sacred sites that defined their identities.

**6. ideology**
> Manifest destiny was an ideology that blended racial hierarchy, Protestant Christianity, and democratic nationalism into a framework that made American expansion seem not merely desirable but morally required.

**7. sovereignty**
> The Supreme Court ruled in 1831 that Native American nations were "domestic dependent nations" — acknowledging a form of sovereignty while denying them the full rights of foreign nations.

### The Myth of Inevitable Expansion
#### Manifest Destiny and the Cost Paid by Others

By the 1840s, a confident ideology had taken hold in American public life: the United States was destined — perhaps divinely — to expand its dominion across the entire North American continent. This belief, called Manifest Destiny, fused Protestant Christianity, racial hierarchy, and democratic nationalism into a framework that made territorial expansion seem not a choice but a moral obligation. Americans were not conquering the continent, in this telling — they were fulfilling it.

The reality behind the ideology was more straightforward: displacement, broken treaties, and the systematic destruction of indigenous civilizations.

The Indian Removal Act of 1830, signed by President Andrew Jackson, authorized the forcible relocation of Native American tribes from the southeastern United States to lands west of the Mississippi. The resulting Trail of Tears — a forced march of tens of thousands of people from homelands they had occupied for millennia — resulted in the deaths of an estimated fifteen thousand people. The Cherokee Nation, which had adopted a written constitution, established courts, and published a newspaper, had made every effort to demonstrate that it could coexist with American civilization. It made no difference.

Throughout the nineteenth century, the pattern repeated itself across the continent. The government negotiated treaties guaranteeing Native American sovereignty over specific territories; then, when those territories proved desirable, it renegotiated or simply violated the treaties. By the 1880s, the indigenous population of North America had been reduced from an estimated ten million at European contact to roughly two hundred and fifty thousand, through a combination of military violence, disease, and deliberate starvation.

Manifest Destiny was not a description of what was happening — it was a justification for it. The ideology absolved Americans of moral responsibility for the costs borne by others by recasting conquest as providence.

## ✏️ Part 3 — Vocabulary in Use

**1.** Use **"manifest destiny"** to discuss a modern ideology that uses the language of destiny or inevitability to justify a policy that serves the interests of the powerful.

**2.** Use **"displacement"** to describe a historical or contemporary situation where people were forced from their homes for political or economic reasons.

**3.** Use **"assimilation"** to discuss the tension between preserving cultural identity and integrating into a dominant culture. When does assimilation become coercion?

**4.** Use **"treaty"** to discuss the significance of treaty obligations. What happens to international trust when treaties are routinely violated?

**5.** Use **"reservation"** to discuss the long-term consequences of concentrating a minority population in restricted areas. What are the economic and cultural effects?

**6.** Use **"ideology"** to describe a belief system that you believe has been used to justify what is, in reality, an exercise of power.

**7.** Use **"sovereignty"** to discuss the concept of "domestic dependent nations." Is this a meaningful form of sovereignty or a contradiction in terms?

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** The text says Manifest Destiny "absolved Americans of moral responsibility by recasting conquest as providence." How do ideologies function to make harmful actions feel virtuous or inevitable to those who carry them out?

**2.** The Cherokee Nation adopted a constitution, published a newspaper, and built courts — and was removed anyway. What does this tell us about the role of assimilation in protecting minority rights?

**3.** The text says the US made and broke hundreds of treaties. What are the long-term consequences for a nation's reputation and international credibility of systematically violating formal commitments?

**4.** The ideology of Manifest Destiny described expansion as divinely ordained. How does religious language function in political discourse to place certain decisions beyond rational debate?

**5.** The text presents two faces of American expansion — the heroic frontier narrative and the brutal displacement narrative. Can both be true simultaneously, or must one be chosen?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** How should modern nations reckon with historical injustices against indigenous peoples? What forms of acknowledgment, restitution, or policy change are appropriate?

**2.** The concept of Manifest Destiny has parallels in other national traditions — the idea that a nation's expansion or dominance is divinely ordained or historically inevitable. Can you identify examples from other contexts?

**3.** The Trail of Tears occurred during the lifetime of the Constitution. How do we reconcile the ideals expressed in the Declaration of Independence with the realities of Indian removal?

**4.** The United States government is still in a complex legal relationship with Native American tribes regarding sovereignty and treaty rights. How should this relationship evolve?

**5.** Korea has its own history of having its sovereignty violated by more powerful neighbours. Does this experience shape how you view the history of American expansion and indigenous dispossession?
