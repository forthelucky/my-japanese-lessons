### Example Sentences

**1. isolationism**
> American isolationism before World War I was not merely a policy preference — it was a deeply held conviction that the Atlantic Ocean provided a natural barrier against European entanglements and that America's republican institutions were best protected by staying out of the Old World's conflicts.

**2. imperialism**
> The Spanish-American War of 1898 marked America's entry into imperial competition: by defeating Spain, the US acquired the Philippines, Guam, and Puerto Rico — and with them, the moral and strategic complications of overseas empire.

**3. neutrality**
> Wilson maintained American neutrality for nearly three years after the war began in Europe, under enormous pressure from both sides; Germany's unrestricted submarine warfare, which threatened American lives and commerce, ultimately made neutrality untenable.

**4. propaganda**
> The Committee on Public Information, established by the Wilson administration in 1917, produced propaganda at industrial scale — posters, films, speeches — to transform a reluctant public into enthusiastic war supporters within months.

**5. armistice**
> The Armistice of November 11, 1918, ended the fighting but not the war itself; the peace treaties that followed — especially Versailles — created conditions that many historians argue made a second world war more likely, not less.

**6. Fourteen Points**
> Wilson's Fourteen Points represented the most ambitious attempt to design a rules-based international order in modern history — open diplomacy, national self-determination, freedom of the seas, and a League of Nations to mediate future disputes.

**7. League of Nations**
> The supreme irony of Wilson's foreign policy was that the League of Nations — his greatest creation — was rejected by the US Senate, leaving the international body Wilson designed without the participation of the world's most powerful nation.

### From Isolation to World Stage
#### The Spanish-American War, World War I, and the End of American Innocence

For most of its first century, the United States followed the foreign policy prescription of its founders: avoid "entangling alliances" with European powers and focus on the development of the Western Hemisphere. The Atlantic and Pacific Oceans were not merely geographical features — they were the physical basis of a strategic doctrine. America was strong enough to defend itself and rich enough to grow without foreign adventure.

That doctrine began to crack in 1898. The Spanish-American War, sparked by the explosion of the USS Maine in Havana Harbour and fuelled by sensationalist journalism and genuine sympathy for Cuban independence, lasted just four months. When it ended, the United States had acquired an overseas empire: Cuba as a protectorate, Puerto Rico as a territory, Guam and the Philippines as possessions. The Philippines in particular would prove troublesome — a three-year insurrection against American rule killed more Americans than the war against Spain had.

When Europe went to war in 1914, Wilson initially maintained strict neutrality. But the nature of the conflict made neutrality increasingly difficult. Germany's declaration of unrestricted submarine warfare — threatening any ship in the war zone, including neutral American vessels — sank American ships and killed American citizens. The Zimmermann Telegram, a German diplomatic cable proposing a military alliance with Mexico against the United States, when published, inflamed American public opinion. In April 1917, Wilson asked Congress for a declaration of war.

American entry transformed the military balance. Two million American troops arrived in France in 1918, providing the fresh manpower that tipped a deadlocked war decisively. But Wilson's ambition was larger than military victory. His Fourteen Points proposed a new international order based on open diplomacy, national self-determination, and a League of Nations to resolve future disputes peacefully. The European allies accepted the framework grudgingly; the US Senate rejected American membership entirely.

## ✏️ Part 3 — Vocabulary in Use

**1.** Use **"isolationism"** to argue for or against the proposition that geographic separation from major conflicts is a realistic basis for foreign policy in the modern world.

**2.** Use **"imperialism"** to discuss the contradictions involved when a nation founded on anti-colonial principles acquires an overseas empire.

**3.** Use **"neutrality"** to discuss the conditions under which neutrality becomes impossible to maintain. What obligations does a powerful nation have when others are at war?

**4.** Use **"propaganda"** to discuss the ethics of government-directed information campaigns during wartime. Is there a meaningful difference between propaganda and legitimate public communication?

**5.** Use **"armistice"** to explain why ending the fighting is different from achieving a durable peace.

**6.** Use **"Fourteen Points"** to discuss what principles should guide the design of a post-conflict international order. Are Wilson's principles still relevant today?

**7.** Use **"League of Nations"** to discuss the consequences of designing an international institution without securing the participation of the most powerful potential member.

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** The text says the Atlantic Ocean was "the physical basis of a strategic doctrine." To what extent is geography still the basis of a nation's strategic choices?

**2.** The US fought a three-year counter-insurgency in the Philippines against people seeking the same independence America claimed to stand for. How do nations manage the gap between their stated principles and their imperial behaviour?

**3.** Wilson's propagandists transformed reluctant public opinion into war enthusiasm within months. What does this reveal about the malleability of public opinion and the power of government-directed media?

**4.** Wilson designed the League of Nations and then watched the US Senate refuse to join. What structural features of American government allowed a minority of senators to block a treaty the president negotiated?

**5.** The Versailles settlement created conditions that many historians blame for World War II. Was Wilson's vision wrong, or was it simply not implemented faithfully?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** America entered World War I partly because of German submarine attacks on neutral shipping. How has the principle of freedom of navigation shaped American foreign policy since then?

**2.** Wilson's League of Nations was the predecessor of the United Nations. What lessons from the League's failure were incorporated into the UN's design? Were they sufficient?

**3.** The United States has periodically oscillated between internationalism and isolationism throughout its history. What drives these cycles?

**4.** Korea's fate was directly affected by World War I and the Paris Peace Conference — the Korean independence movement submitted a petition to Wilson invoking his principle of self-determination, which was ignored. How does this episode illustrate the limits of idealistic international frameworks?

**5.** World War I shattered the European confidence that industrial civilization meant moral progress. Did it — or should it have — produced a similar disillusionment in the United States?
