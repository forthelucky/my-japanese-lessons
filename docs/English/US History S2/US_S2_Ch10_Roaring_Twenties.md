### Example Sentences

**1. speculation**
> The stock market speculation of the late 1920s was fuelled by the reasonable belief that American industrial output would continue to grow — and by the much less reasonable belief that stock prices could rise indefinitely without connection to underlying earnings.

**2. prohibition**
> Prohibition, which outlawed alcohol from 1920 to 1933, demonstrated the limits of law as a tool of social engineering: it reduced alcohol consumption but created a massive illegal market, empowered organized crime, and generated widespread contempt for law enforcement.

**3. consumerism**
> The 1920s was the decade when mass consumerism took root in America — not because people suddenly became more materialistic, but because mass production had lowered prices sufficiently that middle-class families could aspire to own cars, radios, and household appliances.

**4. installment credit**
> The rapid spread of installment credit — the ability to buy a car or refrigerator with a small deposit and monthly payments — allowed consumer spending to outpace income growth during the 1920s, creating hidden fragility in household finances.

**5. Harlem Renaissance**
> The Harlem Renaissance produced Langston Hughes, Zora Neale Hurston, Duke Ellington, and Louis Armstrong — an outpouring of literary, visual, and musical creativity that challenged the racial stereotypes of white America while building a new sense of Black cultural identity.

**6. bubble**
> The stock market bubble of the late 1920s was visible in retrospect — price-to-earnings ratios had reached extraordinary levels — but this is the nature of bubbles: the psychology of the boom makes the warning signs invisible to those inside it.

**7. buying on margin**
> Buying on margin allowed small investors to participate in the stock market boom with borrowed money; it also meant that a relatively modest fall in prices would trigger margin calls, forcing mass liquidation and accelerating the collapse.

### The Party Before the Crash
#### The 1920s and the Hidden Fragility of Prosperity

The 1920s were, in many respects, genuinely remarkable. American industrial output grew at unprecedented rates. The automobile transformed everyday life, enabling suburban development and changing the social geography of every city. The radio created a genuinely national popular culture for the first time. Jazz — born in the Black communities of New Orleans and Harlem — became the defining sound of an era, its improvised energy perfectly matched to a decade that seemed to be constantly improvising.

But the prosperity was unevenly distributed and structurally fragile. Farmers, who had expanded production during World War I, found themselves facing falling prices throughout the decade as European agriculture recovered. Workers' real wages grew more slowly than corporate profits. Wealth concentrated rapidly at the top: by 1929, the top one percent of Americans received roughly twenty-three percent of national income.

The financial architecture of the decade compounded the fragility. Installment credit allowed consumers to purchase goods they could not immediately afford; it sustained demand but created vulnerability to any income shock. Stock market speculation, fuelled partly by buying on margin — borrowing up to ninety percent of a stock's purchase price — drove equity prices far above any defensible valuation. A generation of investors had never experienced a serious market downturn and assumed the good times were permanent.

Prohibition, enacted in 1920, illustrated another contradiction. The legislation was the product of a genuine reform impulse — alcohol abuse was a serious social problem, especially in working-class communities. But outlawing alcohol proved impossibly difficult to enforce. Speakeasies operated openly in every city. Bootleggers — chief among them Al Capone — built criminal enterprises of enormous scale, corrupting police forces and local governments across the country.

By 1929, the surface of prosperity concealed widening cracks. The crash, when it came, was not a random event — it was the settling of accumulated accounts.

## ✏️ Part 3 — Vocabulary in Use

**1.** Use **"speculation"** to discuss the psychology that drives investors to buy assets at prices they would recognize as disconnected from value in a calmer moment.

**2.** Use **"prohibition"** to discuss a contemporary example of legislation designed to suppress a behaviour but that created significant unintended consequences.

**3.** Use **"consumerism"** to argue whether the equation of well-being with consumption is a cultural choice, an economic necessity, or something that can be changed.

**4.** Use **"installment credit"** to discuss how debt-financed consumption sustains short-term demand while creating long-term vulnerability.

**5.** Use **"Harlem Renaissance"** to discuss how cultural creativity can emerge from communities experiencing profound social oppression.

**6.** Use **"bubble"** to explain why rational individuals — investors, homebuyers, entrepreneurs — participate in asset bubbles even when the warning signs are visible.

**7.** Use **"buying on margin"** to explain why leverage amplifies both gains and losses, and why it tends to accelerate market collapses.

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** The text says prosperity was "unevenly distributed and structurally fragile." Which of these problems was more dangerous — the inequality or the fragility? Are the two related?

**2.** Farmers suffered throughout the 1920s while urban consumers prospered. How does uneven distribution of economic gains across sectors create political instability?

**3.** Prohibition reduced alcohol consumption but created organized crime and contempt for law. What principles should govern decisions about what behaviours to criminalize?

**4.** The text calls the crash "the settling of accumulated accounts." What does it mean for a crash to be not a random event but a structural outcome? How does this change how we should think about prevention?

**5.** The Harlem Renaissance flourished amid the deep racial inequality of the 1920s. What does this tell us about the relationship between cultural vitality and political conditions?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** The 1920s is often described as a decade of social liberation — jazz, flappers, loosening Victorian mores. Was this liberation genuine and lasting, or superficial and temporary?

**2.** Financial bubbles recur throughout history despite economists' and regulators' awareness of their dynamics. Why is the knowledge that bubbles exist not sufficient to prevent them?

**3.** Prohibition is often cited as a cautionary tale about government overreach. But it did reduce alcohol consumption. How should policymakers weigh partial success against major unintended consequences?

**4.** The 1920s stock market bubble was visible in retrospect to anyone with basic financial knowledge. What institutional changes might help markets be less prone to this kind of excess?

**5.** The Harlem Renaissance is one of the great cultural flourishings of the twentieth century, emerging from a community facing extreme racial oppression. How do you understand the relationship between adversity and artistic creativity?
