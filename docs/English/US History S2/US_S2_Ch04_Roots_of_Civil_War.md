### Example Sentences

**1. sectionalism**
> Sectionalism intensified throughout the 1840s and 1850s as each new territory acquired from Mexico forced Congress to decide whether slavery would be permitted — transforming every land question into a proxy war over the future of the republic.

**2. abolitionism**
> The abolitionist movement was never simply about opposing slavery on moral grounds; it was also about the political power of slaveholders, who used their dominance in the Senate to block policies that northern farmers and workers wanted.

**3. secession**
> Southern leaders threatened secession so frequently before the Civil War that many northerners initially dismissed it as a bluff; when South Carolina actually seceded in December 1860, the political class had to revise its assumptions rapidly.

**4. slave state**
> The balance between slave states and free states in the Senate was the central political obsession of American politics for three decades before the Civil War; every admission of a new state became a crisis because it threatened to tip that balance.

**5. tariff**
> The South's opposition to protective tariffs was not irrational; as a raw material exporter, the south bought manufactured goods and had no domestic industry to protect — northern tariffs raised costs for southern consumers while benefiting northern manufacturers.

**6. compromise**
> A series of congressional compromises — 1820, 1833, 1850 — managed the slavery question for four decades by deferring the fundamental conflict; each compromise bought time but resolved nothing.

**7. irreconcilable**
> By the mid-1850s, the political systems of north and south had become genuinely irreconcilable: the north was developing an industrial economy that required free labour and free markets; the south was defending an agricultural economy that required enslaved labour and political protection for it.

### Two Nations, One Country
#### The Economic and Moral Fault Lines That Made the Civil War

By 1860, the United States was not one country in any meaningful economic, cultural, or social sense — it was two, held together by a constitution that both sides claimed to interpret correctly and a history of compromise that was running out of road.

The economic division was stark. The northern states had industrialized rapidly. Their economy was based on manufacturing, commerce, and small-scale agriculture worked by free, wage-earning labour. They wanted protective tariffs to shield their industries from European competition, federal investment in infrastructure, and a homestead policy to settle the west with small farmers. The southern states remained agricultural, dependent on cotton, tobacco, and rice cultivated by enslaved labour on large plantations. They wanted low tariffs to keep the cost of manufactured imports down, opposed federal infrastructure spending as a subsidy to the north, and above all, wanted the right to bring their enslaved labour force into new western territories.

These economic differences were inseparable from a deeper moral conflict. Abolitionism had grown from a fringe movement into a significant political force. Harriet Beecher Stowe's novel Uncle Tom's Cabin, published in 1852, brought the realities of slavery before a northern audience that had previously understood it abstractly. The Underground Railroad, the militant abolitionism of John Brown, and the creation of the Republican Party in 1854 — explicitly dedicated to preventing the spread of slavery — all signalled that the political accommodation that had kept the peace for seventy years was failing.

Abraham Lincoln's election in November 1860 — he was not on the ballot in most southern states — was the trigger. South Carolina seceded before Lincoln was inaugurated. Within months, ten more southern states had followed, forming the Confederate States of America. What had been a political crisis became a constitutional one: could a state leave the union?

Lincoln's answer was no. And the question would be settled by four years of the bloodiest war in American history.

## ✏️ Part 3 — Vocabulary in Use

**1.** Use **"sectionalism"** to describe a political context where regional interests dominate national ones. What makes this dynamic so difficult to manage?

**2.** Use **"abolitionism"** to discuss how a moral movement that begins as marginal can become politically transformative. What conditions enable this shift?

**3.** Use **"secession"** to discuss when, if ever, a region or group has a legitimate right to withdraw from a political union.

**4.** Use **"slave state"** to discuss the political mechanics of how a morally illegitimate institution can become embedded in the formal structure of a democratic government.

**5.** Use **"tariff"** to explain how trade policy can intensify regional resentment when different regions have structurally opposed economic interests.

**6.** Use **"compromise"** to discuss the limits of the compromise strategy for managing deep political conflicts. When does deferral become denial?

**7.** Use **"irreconcilable"** to discuss how political leaders should respond when they recognize that two sides in a conflict hold genuinely incompatible positions.

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** The text argues that the US was "two countries held together by a constitution." Is constitutional unity sufficient to sustain political union when economic and social interests have diverged so completely?

**2.** A series of compromises managed the slavery question for forty years. Does this suggest that the conflict was avoidable, or merely postponed an inevitable reckoning?

**3.** The north and south had different economic interests in tariff policy that were entirely separate from slavery. How does the entanglement of economic interest and moral question complicate political resolution?

**4.** Lincoln's election triggered secession even before he had taken any action as president. What does this tell us about the role of symbolic threats in political conflict?

**5.** The text says abolitionism moved from "fringe to political force." What conditions allowed this transformation, and what does it suggest about how moral arguments gain political traction?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** The Civil War is sometimes described as "inevitable" given the incompatibility of the two economic systems. Do you think major historical conflicts are ever truly inevitable, or always the product of specific decisions?

**2.** The United States held together through compromise for seventy years before the system failed. What does this suggest about the capacity of political institutions to manage deep divisions?

**3.** Moral arguments — about the wrongness of slavery — ultimately proved more powerful than political arguments. When do moral claims change political outcomes, and when do they fail to?

**4.** Contemporary America is described by many observers as deeply divided along cultural, economic, and political lines. Does the antebellum period offer any useful parallels or lessons?

**5.** How should a democracy respond when a significant minority of its population holds a position — like the defence of slavery — that the majority considers morally illegitimate?
