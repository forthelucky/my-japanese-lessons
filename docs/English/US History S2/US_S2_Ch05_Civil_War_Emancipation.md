### Example Sentences

**1. emancipation**
> Lincoln framed emancipation first as a war measure rather than a moral revolution — the Emancipation Proclamation freed enslaved people only in Confederate states, where the Union could not yet enforce it — but its symbolic and political effects were immediate and transformative.

**2. proclamation**
> The Emancipation Proclamation, issued January 1, 1863, changed the character of the war: what had been a conflict about union became, inescapably, a conflict about slavery — making European recognition of the Confederacy politically impossible.

**3. attrition**
> Grant's strategy in 1864 was one of ruthless attrition: the Union could replace its losses; the Confederacy could not. The horror of the Overland Campaign — fifty thousand Union casualties in six weeks — was the price of that strategy.

**4. conscription**
> The Union's conscription law of 1863 allowed wealthy men to pay three hundred dollars to avoid service, sparking the New York City Draft Riots — four days of violence in which working-class men, many of them Irish immigrants, attacked symbols of the war and Black neighbourhoods alike.

**5. blockade**
> The Union naval blockade of southern ports, maintained throughout the war, progressively strangled the Confederate economy — preventing the export of cotton and the import of manufactured goods and weapons.

**6. Confederacy**
> The Confederacy's fundamental strategic problem was that it needed to survive long enough to exhaust the Union's will to fight; the Union needed only to maintain the political will to continue until its material advantages became decisive.

**7. address**
> The Gettysburg Address, delivered in November 1863 at the dedication of a military cemetery, was two minutes long — shorter than the main speaker's two-hour oration — and is now considered one of the greatest speeches in American history.

### A War Becomes a Revolution
#### The Civil War, Emancipation, and the Redefinition of America

When the Civil War began in April 1861, Lincoln insisted it was a war to preserve the Union — not to end slavery. This was partly genuine conviction and partly political calculation: the border states that had not seceded (Maryland, Kentucky, Missouri, Delaware) permitted slavery, and Lincoln could not afford to lose them. He was president of a divided country, fighting a war with a divided coalition.

The war's character changed as it lengthened and deepened. Early Union commanders moved cautiously, reluctant to take decisive action. Lincoln cycled through generals until he found Ulysses Grant, who understood that the Union's material superiority could only be decisive if it was applied relentlessly. Meanwhile, the political calculus around slavery shifted. Hundreds of thousands of enslaved people were already voting with their feet — fleeing to Union lines, volunteering to serve as labourers, scouts, and eventually soldiers. By 1863, over 180,000 Black men had served in the Union Army.

The Emancipation Proclamation of January 1, 1863, was a watershed. Lincoln issued it as a war measure under his authority as commander-in-chief: enslaved people in Confederate states were declared free. In the states where it applied, Union forces could not enforce it. But its effects were profound: it made the war explicitly about slavery, destroying any possibility that Britain or France — where antislavery sentiment was strong — would recognize the Confederacy. It also transformed the Union Army into an instrument of emancipation.

The Gettysburg Address, delivered in November 1863, went further than the Proclamation. Lincoln did not merely claim that the Union was fighting to preserve itself — he reinterpreted the nation's founding. The war, he said, was a test of whether a nation "conceived in Liberty, and dedicated to the proposition that all men are created equal" could endure. He was reaching back to the Declaration of Independence — over the Constitution and its compromises — to define what America was supposed to be.

## ✏️ Part 3 — Vocabulary in Use

**1.** Use **"emancipation"** to discuss the difference between legal freedom and genuine liberation. Does formal emancipation automatically produce equality?

**2.** Use **"proclamation"** to explain how official announcements can change political realities even when they cannot be immediately enforced.

**3.** Use **"attrition"** to discuss Grant's strategy. Is a strategy of deliberately accepting heavy casualties in order to exhaust the enemy morally defensible?

**4.** Use **"conscription"** to discuss the equity problems that arise when wealthy citizens can buy their way out of military service.

**5.** Use **"blockade"** to explain how economic warfare — cutting off trade — can be as decisive as military operations.

**6.** Use **"Confederacy"** to discuss the strategic challenge of a side that needs only to survive rather than to win outright.

**7.** Use **"address"** to analyse what makes the Gettysburg Address so rhetorically powerful. What techniques does Lincoln use?

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** Lincoln initially framed the war as about union, not slavery. Was this honest? Was it strategically necessary? Does the distinction matter morally?

**2.** The Emancipation Proclamation freed enslaved people only in Confederate states where the Union could not enforce it. Is a declaration of freedom that cannot be immediately enforced meaningful, or is it merely symbolic?

**3.** The text says Lincoln "reached back to the Declaration of Independence — over the Constitution." What does it mean to choose one founding document over another as the authoritative statement of national values?

**4.** Over 180,000 Black men served in the Union Army. How did their participation change the meaning of what they were fighting for?

**5.** Grant's attrition strategy cost fifty thousand Union casualties in six weeks. How should military leaders weigh the cost in lives against the strategic benefits of a successful strategy?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** The Civil War transformed what had been a political conflict over union into a social revolution over slavery. Can wars produce social transformations that would have been impossible through peaceful politics?

**2.** The Emancipation Proclamation was a political document as much as a moral one. Does the impure motivation of a moral action affect its moral value?

**3.** The Gettysburg Address is two minutes long but has lasted 160 years. What makes political rhetoric endure? What distinguishes genuinely great political speech from merely effective propaganda?

**4.** The Civil War killed approximately 620,000 soldiers — more than any other American war. How do nations process and memorialize losses of this scale?

**5.** The war ended slavery but not the racial hierarchy that slavery had produced. What does this tell us about the limits of military and legal solutions to deeply embedded social structures?
