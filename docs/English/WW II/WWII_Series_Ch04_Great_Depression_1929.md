### Example Sentences

**1. recession**
> The recession of the early 1930s was unlike any that living memory could supply; it was not a dip in the business cycle but a systemic collapse that erased the economic gains of a decade.

**2. unemployment**
> By 1932, unemployment in Germany had reached thirty percent — meaning roughly one in three workers had no income, no prospects, and every reason to blame the system that had failed them.

**3. protectionism**
> The Smoot-Hawley Tariff of 1930, a dramatic act of American protectionism, triggered retaliatory tariffs across the world and transformed a financial crisis into a full-scale collapse of global trade.

**4. deflation**
> Deflation sounds harmless — prices falling sounds like good news — but it causes consumers to delay spending and businesses to cut wages, creating a downward spiral that is extraordinarily difficult to reverse.

**5. authoritarian**
> Voters in economically devastated countries did not become authoritarian because they stopped valuing freedom; they became authoritarian because they stopped believing that freedom and prosperity could be delivered simultaneously.

**6. contagion**
> The financial contagion that began with the collapse of American banks spread to Europe within months, demonstrating that the global economy had become deeply interconnected long before policymakers understood the risks.

**7. populism**
> Populism thrives in the space between what mainstream politics promises and what it delivers; when that gap becomes too wide, voters look for candidates who promise to burn the whole structure down.

### The Crash That Fed Fascism
#### How the Great Depression Destroyed the Political Centre

On October 24, 1929, the New York Stock Exchange collapsed. Within weeks, the financial panic had spread across the Atlantic. Banks failed across Europe. Credit dried up. Factories closed. Within three years, global industrial production had fallen by more than a third, and international trade had contracted by nearly two-thirds. For millions of ordinary people, the experience was not an abstract economic statistic — it was hunger, eviction, and humiliation.

The political consequences were as catastrophic as the economic ones, and they operated through a specific psychological mechanism. People do not abandon moderate, democratic politics simply because they are poor. They abandon it when they believe — correctly or not — that moderate politics is incapable of helping them. In Germany, where the trauma of hyperinflation was still vivid, a second economic catastrophe within a decade confirmed the darkest fears: the Weimar Republic could not protect its citizens.

The numbers tell the story with brutal clarity. In the German federal elections of 1928, before the crash, the Nazi Party received 2.6 percent of the vote — a fringe movement. In the elections of 1932, at the depths of the Depression, they received 37.4 percent, making them the largest single party in the Reichstag. Unemployment had by then reached six million — nearly one in three workers. That electoral transformation did not happen because German voters suddenly became morally bankrupt. It happened because mainstream parties offered incremental solutions to a catastrophic problem, and a demagogue offered a compelling villain and a radical promise.

Japan's trajectory followed a parallel logic. The Depression devastated Japanese silk exports — the country's primary source of foreign currency — and rural poverty became severe. Military officers watched the liberal politicians and business elites who had championed international trade preside over national humiliation. The conclusion drawn in army barracks was the same one drawn in Munich beer halls: the parliamentary system had failed, and only national strength — meaning military expansion — could secure the country's future.

The Depression was a global event, but its political consequences were felt most acutely in societies that had entered it already weakened — by war debts, by previous crises, by institutional distrust. In those societies, it did not just create poverty. It created the conditions in which millions of people were willing to trade political freedom for the promise of economic security.

## ✏️ Part 3 — Vocabulary in Use

**1.** Use **"recession"** to describe an economic downturn you know about. How did the government respond, and was the response effective?

**2.** Use **"unemployment"** to discuss what you think a government's primary obligation is toward people who cannot find work.

**3.** Use **"protectionism"** to argue either for or against trade barriers during an economic crisis.

**4.** Use **"deflation"** to explain why falling prices are not always good news for an economy.

**5.** Use **"authoritarian"** to describe how economic suffering can change what voters are willing to accept from their governments.

**6.** Use **"contagion"** (in its economic or social sense) to describe a modern situation where a problem spread quickly from one country or sector to others.

**7.** Use **"populism"** to describe a political figure or movement — historical or contemporary — that you would classify as populist.

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** The text says people abandon moderate politics not because they are poor, but because they believe moderate politics cannot help them. Is that distinction important? What does it suggest about how democracies should respond to crises?

**2.** Nazi support rose from 2.6% to 37.4% in four years of economic depression. Does this mean that democracy is fundamentally fragile under economic stress, or does it mean that particular democracies were fragile?

**3.** The text argues that the Depression hit hardest in societies "already weakened." What does this suggest about the importance of institutional resilience *before* a crisis hits?

**4.** Both Germany and Japan drew the conclusion that their parliamentary systems had failed. Were they wrong? Was the parliamentary system genuinely failing, or were there other choices available?

**5.** The text describes people "trading political freedom for economic security." Is this always irrational? Under what conditions might you make the same trade?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** The 2008 global financial crisis produced significant political radicalization — but different outcomes in different countries. What factors determined whether a country moved toward authoritarianism or remained stable?

**2.** The text suggests that global economic interdependence enabled the Depression to spread as contagion. Does greater economic integration make the world more or less vulnerable to financial crises today?

**3.** What do you think governments today should learn from the 1930s about managing economic crises — both the economic tools and the political communication?

**4.** Is economic populism — promising radical redistribution or trade protectionism — inherently dangerous, or can it sometimes be a healthy corrective to elite-dominated politics?

**5.** If you were a young person in Germany in 1932, with no job and no prospect of one, how do you imagine you would have voted? Does thinking about that change how you judge the German electorate of that era?
