### Example Sentences

**1. Manhattan Project**
> The Manhattan Project employed one hundred and thirty thousand people and cost approximately two billion dollars — more than any previous scientific or military programme in history — yet it remained largely secret from the public until the moment the weapon was used.

**2. unconditional surrender**
> The Allied demand for unconditional surrender, reiterated at Potsdam in July 1945, left Japan's leadership with no diplomatic route out of the war that preserved any element of the imperial system they had been fighting to protect.

**3. deterrence**
> The atomic bombing of Hiroshima was presented publicly as a measure to shorten the war, but its long-term strategic significance was as the founding event of nuclear deterrence — the age in which the threat of annihilation became the primary guarantor of great-power peace.

**4. war crime**
> The question of whether the atomic bombings constituted a war crime under the laws of the time — given their indiscriminate effects on civilians — has been debated continuously since 1945 and remains unresolved in international law.

**5. imperial**
> Japan's imperial system — built on the divine authority of the emperor — was left formally intact by the American occupation, a calculated decision to use Hirohito as an instrument of orderly demobilization.

**6. legacy**
> The legacy of the Second World War was not only the creation of the United Nations and the modern international order — it was also the nuclear arms race, the Cold War, and the permanent transformation of state sovereignty by international human rights law.

**7. nuclear deterrence**
> Nuclear deterrence rests on a logical paradox: to prevent nuclear war, each side must credibly threaten to start one — which means maintaining weapons that, if actually used, would accomplish nothing except mutual annihilation.

### The Weapon That Ended Two Empires
#### Hiroshima, Berlin, and the Collapse of the World That Had Made the War

By the spring of 1945, both Germany and Japan were militarily defeated in every meaningful sense. Yet neither had surrendered. Germany fought street by street in the ruins of its cities. Japan prepared its civilian population for a homeland invasion that military planners estimated could cost a million Allied casualties and potentially ten million Japanese lives. The war that had begun in the economic despair of the 1930s and the ambitions of two expansionist regimes was approaching its conclusion — but the ending remained catastrophic.

In Europe, the conclusion came first. On April 30, 1945, with Soviet forces fighting inside Berlin, Adolf Hitler killed himself in his underground bunker. Germany surrendered unconditionally on May 8. The Nazi state, which had promised a Thousand-Year Reich, had lasted twelve years and destroyed tens of millions of lives in the process. Its end was not negotiated; it was annihilated.

In the Pacific, the final blow came from science. The Manhattan Project — a secret programme employing over a hundred thousand people at sites across the United States and Canada — had produced a functional atomic weapon. On August 6, 1945, a single American aircraft dropped an atomic bomb on Hiroshima. The explosion killed approximately eighty thousand people instantly and destroyed four square kilometres of the city. Three days later, a second bomb fell on Nagasaki.

Between the two bombings, on August 8, the Soviet Union declared war on Japan and launched a massive invasion of Manchuria, destroying the Japanese army that had been occupying that territory since 1931. Japan was now fighting two nuclear powers simultaneously with no army, no navy, and no functional air force.

On August 15, Emperor Hirohito addressed the Japanese people by radio — the first time they had ever heard his voice — and announced that Japan would accept the terms of the Potsdam Declaration. The surrender was formally signed on September 2, 1945. The war that had begun with Germany's invasion of Poland in September 1939 — and in its Asian dimension, with Japan's invasion of Manchuria in 1931 — was over.

The two empires that had launched it were gone. The world they left behind — shaped by sixty million dead, by nuclear weapons, by the Nuremberg principles, and by the United Nations — was the world we still inhabit.

## ✏️ Part 3 — Vocabulary in Use

**1.** Use **"Manhattan Project"** to discuss the ethical responsibilities of scientists and engineers who develop technologies with devastating potential applications.

**2.** Use **"unconditional surrender"** to argue for or against the principle that a defeated enemy should always be offered a diplomatic exit that might shorten a war.

**3.** Use **"deterrence"** to discuss whether the threat of mutually assured destruction is a stable basis for long-term peace.

**4.** Use **"war crime"** to discuss how the laws of war are defined, enforced, and applied — or fail to be applied — in practice.

**5.** Use **"imperial"** to reflect on how the collapse of an imperial system shapes the society it leaves behind.

**6.** Use **"legacy"** to describe what you consider the most important inheritance — positive or negative — that the Second World War passed to the modern world.

**7.** Use **"nuclear deterrence"** to explain the logical paradox at the heart of this strategy and whether you find it reassuring or alarming.

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** The text presents the atomic bombings as the event that ended the war in the Pacific. Historians debate whether they were necessary. What considerations do you think should weigh most heavily in that judgment?

**2.** Hitler died by suicide as Soviet forces entered Berlin, and Germany fought until literally no state apparatus remained. What does this tell us about the nature of the Nazi regime — and about the risks of political systems built entirely around a single leader?

**3.** The Soviet declaration of war against Japan on August 8 — between the two bombings — may have been as decisive as the bombs themselves in triggering surrender. How does this complicate the standard narrative that the atomic bombs "ended the war"?

**4.** The text notes that Japan's imperial system was preserved by the American occupation decision. What were the arguments for and against this decision, and how has the ambiguity about wartime responsibility shaped modern Japanese politics?

**5.** The text ends by saying the world the war left behind "is the world we still inhabit." Which aspects of the postwar order do you think have most durably shaped modern international relations?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** The atomic bomb ended the Second World War but inaugurated the nuclear age. Taking both effects together — the war it ended and the world it created — do you think its use was justified?

**2.** The Nuremberg trials established that "following orders" is not a sufficient defence for war crimes. How effectively has this principle been applied in the eight decades since 1945?

**3.** The UN was created specifically to prevent another world war. Eighty years later, what is your assessment of its success? Has it fulfilled its core purpose?

**4.** The text describes a world "shaped by sixty million dead." Major war between great powers has not occurred since 1945. To what do you attribute this "long peace" — nuclear deterrence, economic interdependence, international institutions, or something else?

**5.** Looking at the full arc of this series — from the Versailles Treaty to the atomic bomb — what do you think is the single most important lesson that the history of the Second World War offers to the world today?
