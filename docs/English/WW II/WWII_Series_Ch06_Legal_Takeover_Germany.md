### Example Sentences

**1. enabling act**
> The Enabling Act of March 1933 was passed by a two-thirds majority of the Reichstag — the constitutionally required threshold — giving Hitler the legal authority to govern without parliamentary approval for four years.

**2. arson**
> Whether the Reichstag fire was deliberate arson by the Nazis or the act of a lone Dutch communist remains debated, but the political use made of it — to justify emergency powers — was entirely deliberate.

**3. emergency decree**
> The emergency decree issued the morning after the Reichstag fire suspended civil liberties across Germany, making it a criminal offence to criticize the government or organize political opposition.

**4. consolidate**
> Within six months of becoming chancellor, Hitler had consolidated power to a degree that took Mussolini years to achieve in Italy, moving with a speed that left potential opponents no time to coordinate resistance.

**5. totalitarian**
> A totalitarian state differs from an ordinary dictatorship in its ambition: it does not merely suppress opposition — it seeks to eliminate the private sphere entirely, colonizing thought, family life, and personal identity.

**6. chancellor**
> Hitler was appointed chancellor on January 30, 1933, not by election but by presidential decree — invited into power by conservative elites who believed they could use and then discard him.

**7. dissolution**
> The dissolution of all political parties except the Nazi Party, completed by July 1933, meant that Germany had moved from a multi-party democracy to a one-party state in under six months.

### Democracy's Suicide Vote
#### How the Weimar Republic Voted Itself Out of Existence

On January 30, 1933, President Paul von Hindenburg appointed Adolf Hitler as Chancellor of Germany. Hitler had never won a national majority; in the most recent election, the Nazis had received 33 percent of the vote, and support was actually declining. But conservative politicians — men like Franz von Papen, who had engineered the appointment — believed they had made a shrewd calculation. Hitler, they thought, would be a useful instrument: popular enough to mobilize the masses, but controllable enough to govern in their interests. They were catastrophically wrong.

The instrument they believed they were wielding moved with stunning speed. On February 27, the Reichstag parliament building burned. A Dutch communist was arrested at the scene, and the Nazis immediately blamed an international communist conspiracy. The following morning, Hitler persuaded President Hindenburg to sign an emergency decree suspending civil liberties — free speech, free press, freedom of assembly, the right to privacy — indefinitely. Over four thousand political opponents were arrested in days.

The final legal step came in March. The Enabling Act, passed by a Reichstag that had been purged of Communist deputies and intimidated by SS men stationed in the galleries, granted Hitler the right to pass laws without parliamentary approval for four years. It passed by 441 votes to 94. The Social Democrats voted against; everyone else approved. The constitution had not been overthrown. It had been amended into irrelevance by the votes of legislators who were either complicit, coerced, or deluded about what they were enabling.

By July 1933, all political parties except the Nazi Party had been dissolved or had dissolved themselves. Trade unions were abolished. The press was brought under state control. The process that historians call *Gleichschaltung* — "coordination" or "bringing into line" — systematically replaced the leadership of every institution in German society: universities, courts, local governments, professional associations, churches. In six months, the Weimar Republic had been replaced by a totalitarian state. No foreign invasion had been required. The democratic system had provided the legal machinery for its own destruction.

## ✏️ Part 3 — Vocabulary in Use

**1.** Use **"enabling act"** to discuss whether a legislature can ever legitimately vote to suspend its own authority. What safeguards, if any, should exist?

**2.** Use **"arson"** (literally or metaphorically) to describe a situation where a crisis — real or manufactured — was used to justify exceptional measures.

**3.** Use **"emergency decree"** to discuss the risks of giving governments expanded powers during a crisis.

**4.** Use **"consolidate"** to describe how a leader or organization might strengthen its control after coming to power.

**5.** Use **"totalitarian"** to distinguish between a government that is authoritarian and one that is truly totalitarian. Where is the line?

**6.** Use **"chancellor"** in a sentence about the political system in Germany — either historical or contemporary.

**7.** Use **"dissolution"** to discuss what it means for a political party, institution, or assembly to be formally disbanded.

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** Conservative politicians believed they could control Hitler. Why do established elites so consistently miscalculate the danger of leaders they help bring to power?

**2.** The Enabling Act was passed legally, by the required constitutional majority. Does legal procedure legitimize an action, even if that action destroys the legal system itself?

**3.** The text says the democratic system "provided the legal machinery for its own destruction." Is this a flaw in the design of democracy, or an unavoidable feature of any system that respects majority rule?

**4.** Most legislators who voted for the Enabling Act were not Nazis — yet they voted for it. What combination of fear, self-interest, and misjudgment could produce that outcome?

**5.** The entire transformation from democracy to one-party state took approximately six months. What does this speed tell us about how quickly institutions can collapse once the process begins?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** Many constitutional scholars argue that democracies need "militant democracy" provisions — rules that allow the state to ban parties or politicians who seek to undermine democracy itself. Do you support such provisions?

**2.** The Reichstag fire was used to justify the suspension of civil liberties. How should citizens evaluate government claims that a crisis requires the suspension of rights? What level of scepticism is healthy?

**3.** The Nazi consolidation of power relied heavily on conservative elites who thought they could manage Hitler. Does this pattern — of established elites empowering dangerous outsiders — recur in modern politics?

**4.** *Gleichschaltung* — the systematic replacement of all institutional leadership — was a key mechanism of Nazi control. In what modern contexts do you see institutions or organizations being brought into political alignment with ruling power?

**5.** If you had been a German legislator in March 1933, what would you have done? What information would you have needed, and what courage would it have required, to vote against the Enabling Act?
