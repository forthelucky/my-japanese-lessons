### Example Sentences

**1. assassination**
> The assassination of Prime Minister Inukai in 1932 sent an unmistakable message to every subsequent Japanese politician: attempting to restrain the military could cost you your life.

**2. faction**
> Two rival factions within the Japanese army — the Imperial Way and the Control factions — disagreed sharply on strategy, but both agreed that military interests should take precedence over civilian governance.

**3. coup attempt**
> The February 26 Incident of 1936 was a coup attempt by young ultranationalist officers who occupied central Tokyo for four days before being suppressed — yet even in failure, it permanently shifted political power toward the military.

**4. intimidation**
> Political intimidation does not require violence to be effective; the credible threat of violence is enough to change the calculations of every official who must decide whether to resist or comply.

**5. moderate**
> Japan's moderates — the finance ministers, diplomats, and liberal politicians who favoured international cooperation over military expansion — were systematically removed from influence through assassination, forced resignation, or simple fear.

**6. impunity**
> When the officers who killed Prime Minister Inukai received light sentences, or were quietly promoted, the military establishment signalled that political assassination would be tolerated — an act of institutional impunity with long-lasting consequences.

**7. veto**
> Japan's military effectively acquired an informal veto over cabinet formation: any prime minister who could not secure the army's approval for his defence minister could not form a government at all.

### Governance by Assassination
#### How Political Murder Reshaped Japanese Democracy

On May 15, 1932, a group of young naval officers entered the official residence of Prime Minister Tsuyoshi Inukai and shot him dead. Inukai had attempted to open negotiations with China to end the undeclared war in Manchuria and had reportedly tried to restrain the Kwantung Army's unauthorized expansion. His murder was the punctuation mark at the end of an era: the era when Japanese civilian politicians genuinely controlled national policy.

The assassins were tried and convicted — but sentenced to prison terms of between four and fifteen years, far lighter than the death penalty their crimes merited. The leniency was not accidental. It reflected the genuine popularity of the officers among sections of the public and, more critically, a military establishment unwilling to condemn the ambitions that motivated the killers, even while formally objecting to the method.

The message was received clearly throughout the political class. Ministers who proposed budget cuts for the military found themselves receiving death threats. Finance Minister Korekiyo Takahashi, who had the temerity to argue that military spending was becoming economically unsustainable, was murdered in his bed during the February 26 Incident of 1936 — a coup attempt that saw fifteen hundred soldiers occupy central Tokyo for four days. Takahashi was one of four senior officials killed that morning.

The February coup ultimately failed — the emperor refused to legitimize it — but its political consequences were a paradox. The army's official leadership used the incident to suppress the radical factions responsible, but in doing so consolidated its own control over the government. Subsequent prime ministers were effectively chosen with military approval. The budget process became militarized. Foreign policy was set less by the foreign ministry than by army and navy staff officers. Japan was not, technically, a military dictatorship. But governance through the threat of violence had produced outcomes indistinguishable from one.

By the late 1930s, the politicians who had once argued for fiscal prudence, diplomatic engagement, and international cooperation were dead, retired in fear, or had learned to silence themselves. The field had been cleared.

## ✏️ Part 3 — Vocabulary in Use

**1.** Use **"assassination"** to discuss whether political violence ever achieves its intended political goals, or whether it typically produces the opposite effect.

**2.** Use **"faction"** to describe an internal conflict within an organization — a political party, a company, or an institution — that you are familiar with.

**3.** Use **"coup attempt"** to describe a historical or contemporary case. What conditions made it possible, and what determined whether it succeeded or failed?

**4.** Use **"intimidation"** to discuss how the threat of violence, even without actual violence, can reshape political behaviour.

**5.** Use **"moderate"** to argue for or against the idea that moderate political positions are inherently more stable than extreme ones.

**6.** Use **"impunity"** to explain why the failure to punish wrongdoing within powerful institutions typically leads to further wrongdoing.

**7.** Use **"veto"** to discuss what it means when an institution gains an informal veto over decisions it is not supposed to control.

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** The assassins received light sentences and were treated sympathetically by sections of the public. What does this reveal about Japanese political culture in the 1930s, and what made that culture possible?

**2.** The text says the February coup "failed" but produced outcomes resembling a military dictatorship anyway. How can a failed power grab still succeed in its fundamental goals?

**3.** Finance Minister Takahashi was murdered for arguing that military spending was economically unsustainable. How does the elimination of people who tell uncomfortable truths affect a government's long-term decision-making?

**4.** Japan was technically not a military dictatorship but achieved outcomes "indistinguishable from one." What is the practical difference between formal and informal control of a political system?

**5.** The emperor refused to legitimize the February coup. What role did the symbolic authority of the emperor play in setting limits on military action, even in a highly militarized Japan?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** Political violence — threats, attacks on institutions, or killings — has occurred in many democracies. What distinguishes a democracy that survives such violence from one that is permanently damaged by it?

**2.** The Japanese military acquired influence over government budgets by threatening the officials responsible for setting them. Are there modern institutional equivalents — ways that powerful actors can effectively veto budget decisions they dislike?

**3.** The officers who carried out assassinations believed they were acting in the national interest. How should legal systems handle politically motivated violence committed by people who believe they are patriots?

**4.** Japan's political moderates were gradually silenced through fear rather than formal prohibition. What institutional structures are most effective at protecting moderate voices during periods of political extremism?

**5.** If you were a Japanese politician in 1933 who believed that military expansion would lead to catastrophe, what options would you have had? What would courage have required, and what would it have cost?
