### Example Sentences

**1. appeasement**
> The Munich Agreement is the defining case study in the failure of appeasement: each territorial concession made Hitler more confident, not more satisfied, that further demands would be met.

**2. annexation**
> Germany's annexation of Austria in March 1938 — the *Anschluss* — was accomplished without military resistance and without meaningful international protest, signalling to Hitler that borders in Europe were negotiable.

**3. ultimatum**
> When Hitler's ultimatum demanded that Czechoslovakia surrender the Sudetenland or face war, the British and French governments chose to pressure the Czechs to comply rather than to challenge the ultimatum themselves.

**4. capitulate**
> Chamberlain capitulated not out of cowardice but out of genuine conviction that Europe could not survive another war — a belief that made him unable to recognize that refusing to fight now would make a larger war inevitable.

**5. deterrence**
> Effective deterrence requires the adversary to believe that aggression will be met with costs that outweigh the gains; at Munich, the Western powers demonstrated that no such costs would be imposed.

**6. sovereignty**
> Czechoslovakia's sovereignty was effectively terminated at Munich without a Czech representative present at the table — a fact that exposed the gap between the language of international law and its actual enforcement.

**7. concession**
> Each concession made to Hitler's demands was justified as resolving the current crisis; none of the diplomats asked whether each resolution was simply creating the conditions for the next demand.

### The Price of Peace at Any Cost
#### Munich, Appeasement, and the Lessons of Negotiating with Dictators

In September 1938, the leaders of Britain, France, Italy, and Germany met in Munich. Conspicuously absent from the conference was a representative of Czechoslovakia — whose territory was the subject of the negotiations. Hitler had demanded the Sudetenland, the German-speaking border regions of the Czech state, claiming the population there was being persecuted. British Prime Minister Neville Chamberlain and French Premier Édouard Daladier agreed to the transfer. Chamberlain returned to London to cheering crowds and declared the agreement meant "peace for our time."

Within six months, Germany had occupied the rest of Czechoslovakia.

The Munich Agreement has become the most studied case of diplomatic failure in modern history, and the word "appeasement" — the policy it embodied — has been permanently tainted. But it is worth understanding why appeasement seemed rational, even admirable, to the men who practised it. Britain and France in 1938 were not cowards; they were scarred. The First World War had killed a generation. The idea of another continental war, less than twenty years later, was genuinely horrifying. Chamberlain believed he was trading a small, strategically exposed territory for a real and durable peace.

He had made a fundamental analytical error: he had treated Hitler as a rational actor whose demands, once met, would diminish. Hitler was something else — an ideologically driven expansionist whose appetite grew with what it fed on. When the Sudetenland was conceded, he did not feel satisfied; he felt emboldened. Munich demonstrated that the Western powers would retreat before a sufficiently loud threat. The conclusion he drew was that the threat of force was a negotiating tool without significant cost.

The agreement also destroyed what remained of collective security in Europe. Czechoslovakia, abandoned by its nominal allies, was absorbed. The Soviet Union, watching from the east, concluded that Britain and France would not fight for other nations' sovereignty and began its own parallel calculations. And Germany's generals, who had privately doubted that Hitler's gambles could succeed, saw him proved right again. The military opposition to his increasingly reckless strategy evaporated.

Churchill, who had opposed appeasement from the beginning, delivered his verdict in the Commons: "We have sustained a defeat without a war." He was right in one sense — but the war came anyway, less than a year later, on far worse terms.

## ✏️ Part 3 — Vocabulary in Use

**1.** Use **"appeasement"** to discuss whether making concessions to an aggressive party ever successfully prevents further aggression, or always encourages it.

**2.** Use **"annexation"** to describe a historical or contemporary case where one country absorbed another's territory. How was it justified?

**3.** Use **"ultimatum"** to discuss the strategic risks of issuing one. What happens if the other side calls your bluff?

**4.** Use **"capitulate"** in a sentence that distinguishes between a reasonable compromise and a capitulation.

**5.** Use **"deterrence"** to argue why credibility — the belief that you will actually follow through on a threat — is essential for deterrence to work.

**6.** Use **"sovereignty"** to discuss whether small nations can genuinely exercise sovereignty when their security depends entirely on more powerful states.

**7.** Use **"concession"** to describe a negotiation — personal, professional, or political — where you gave something up. Was it the right decision?

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** The text says Chamberlain was not a coward but made an "analytical error" about Hitler's nature. Is it possible to be a competent, well-intentioned leader and still make a catastrophic strategic mistake?

**2.** The text argues that Munich emboldened Hitler rather than satisfying him. Is this a general principle — that concessions to aggressive actors always increase their demands — or does it depend on the specific actor?

**3.** Czechoslovakia was not represented at Munich. What does this reveal about the moral framework within which European great powers operated in 1938?

**4.** The text says Germany's internal military opposition to Hitler evaporated after Munich. Why do successful gambits tend to silence internal critics even when the underlying strategy remains reckless?

**5.** Churchill said the West "sustained a defeat without a war." Is it better to fight a war at a time of your own choosing, even if the cause of conflict is not overwhelming, than to wait until conditions are worse?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** "Appeasement" is now a term of political abuse. But is there a form of diplomatic accommodation that is genuinely wise rather than cowardly? How do you distinguish between them?

**2.** The Munich example is regularly invoked to argue against negotiating with aggressive states. Does this historical analogy get applied appropriately in modern foreign policy debates, or is it often misused?

**3.** Chamberlain was trying to avoid a repeat of the First World War. How does the trauma of a previous catastrophe shape — and sometimes distort — decision-making in a later crisis?

**4.** Small and medium-sized states today often depend on security guarantees from larger powers. Based on what happened to Czechoslovakia, how much trust should smaller states place in such guarantees?

**5.** If you were a British foreign policy adviser in September 1938, what alternative to the Munich Agreement would you have proposed? What were the realistic options, and what would each have risked?
