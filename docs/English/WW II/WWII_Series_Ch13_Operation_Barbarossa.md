## 📖 Part 1 — Key Vocabulary

| # | Word / Phrase | Part of Speech | Korean | Definition |
|---|--------------|---------------|--------|------------|
| 1 | **logistics** | noun | 병참, 군수 | The planning and coordination of the supply, movement, and maintenance of military forces |
| 2 | **supply line** | noun | 보급선 | The route along which supplies — food, fuel, ammunition — are transported to military forces in the field |
| 3 | **attrition** | noun | 소모전, 지구전 | A strategy of wearing down an opponent by continuous pressure and resource depletion |
| 4 | **overreach** | noun / verb | 과도한 팽창; 지나치게 뻗다 | Extending one's power or ambition beyond what can be realistically sustained |
| 5 | **partisan** | noun | 파르티잔, 게릴라 | A member of an irregular resistance force operating behind enemy lines |
| 6 | **scorched earth** | noun | 초토화 전술 | A military strategy of destroying everything useful in a retreating area to deny its use to the enemy |
| 7 | **encircle** | verb | 포위하다 | To surround an enemy force entirely, cutting off its lines of supply and retreat |

### Example Sentences

**1. logistics**
> Napoleon said that an army marches on its stomach; by the time German forces reached the outskirts of Moscow in late 1941, their logistics had collapsed so badly that some frontline units were going days without food or fuel.

**2. supply line**
> As Germany advanced deeper into the Soviet Union, its supply lines stretched across thousands of kilometres of hostile territory, vulnerable to partisan attacks and overwhelmed by the scale of consumption.

**3. attrition**
> Stalin's strategy was, at its core, one of attrition: trade space for time, absorb German advances, and rely on the industrial output of factories relocated east of the Urals to replace losses that would have broken any other nation.

**4. overreach**
> Barbarossa was the supreme military overreach of the Second World War: Germany committed itself to defeating the largest country on earth while simultaneously fighting Britain in the west and supplying forces in North Africa.

**5. partisan**
> Behind the German front lines, Soviet partisans sabotaged railways, ambushed supply convoys, and tied down hundreds of thousands of troops who were needed at the front, gradually bleeding the occupation forces white.

**6. scorched earth**
> The Soviet scorched earth retreat of 1941 — burning crops, destroying infrastructure, and evacuating entire factories — denied Germany the resources it had assumed would be available for self-sustaining its advance.

**7. encircle**
> German forces encircled enormous Soviet armies in the early months of Barbarossa, capturing over three million soldiers — yet the Soviet Union kept generating new armies faster than Germany could destroy them.

### Hitler's Logistical Suicide
#### Operation Barbarossa and the Two-Front War

On June 22, 1941, Germany launched the largest military operation in history. Operation Barbarossa committed three million soldiers, three thousand tanks, and two thousand aircraft against the Soviet Union along a front stretching nearly three thousand kilometres. Hitler had told his generals that the campaign would be over in eight weeks. The Soviet state, he declared, was a rotten structure — "kick in the door and the whole building will come crashing down."

Britain had not yet been defeated. The war in North Africa was ongoing. Germany was about to fight on two fronts simultaneously.

The initial results were spectacular. German armoured units advanced hundreds of kilometres in weeks, encircling Soviet armies numbering in the hundreds of thousands. By October, German troops could see the church spires of Moscow through their binoculars. By any conventional measure of operational success, Barbarossa was working.

Beneath those operational successes, however, the structural problems were accumulating with lethal momentum. Germany's supply system was built for short campaigns across the road networks of Western Europe. The Soviet Union had a different railway gauge — meaning German trains could not run on Soviet tracks — vast distances, minimal roads, and autumn rains that turned dirt tracks into impassable mud. By November 1941, German tank units that had begun the campaign with a hundred vehicles were reporting operational strengths of fifteen or twenty. Replacement parts, fuel, and winter clothing were not arriving. The blitzkrieg formula that had worked in France was breaking down against the sheer scale of the Russian interior.

Meanwhile, the Soviet state proved far more resilient than German intelligence had imagined. The initial encirclements were catastrophic, but new armies kept forming. Soviet industry, much of it physically relocated to the east of the Ural mountains in a logistical feat of extraordinary scale, continued producing tanks and artillery at a rate German planners had declared impossible. And behind German lines, partisan units sabotaged railways and supply convoys, multiplying the logistical crisis.

The campaign that was supposed to last eight weeks lasted four years and consumed the Eastern Front in a war of annihilation from which Germany could not withdraw, could not win, and from which no other theatre could be safely stripped to reinforce it. It was the decision that determined the outcome of the war.

## ✏️ Part 3 — Vocabulary in Use

**1.** Use **"logistics"** to explain why the ability to sustain an operation over time is often more decisive than initial tactical success.

**2.** Use **"supply line"** to discuss how dependence on a long, vulnerable supply chain affects strategic decision-making.

**3.** Use **"attrition"** to describe a strategy — military, commercial, or personal — based on outlasting rather than outfighting an opponent.

**4.** Use **"overreach"** to describe a situation — historical or personal — where someone extended their ambitions beyond what their resources could support.

**5.** Use **"partisan"** to discuss the role of irregular resistance forces in undermining a militarily superior occupying power.

**6.** Use **"scorched earth"** to discuss the ethics of a strategy that destroys one's own infrastructure to deny it to an enemy.

**7.** Use **"encircle"** (literally or metaphorically) to describe a situation where someone or something was trapped without options.

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** Hitler's generals had doubts about Barbarossa but proceeded anyway. How does an institution prevent a leader's certainty from overriding the legitimate concerns of his advisers?

**2.** Germany's supply system was designed for short campaigns and broke down over long distances. What does this reveal about the risks of assuming that a strategy that worked in one context will work in a fundamentally different one?

**3.** The text says Germany "could not withdraw" from the Eastern Front. Why is retreat often harder, politically and psychologically, than initial commitment?

**4.** Soviet industry relocated thousands of factories east of the Urals under fire. How does this example of institutional resilience compare to Germany's assumption that the Soviet state would collapse?

**5.** The text calls Barbarossa "the decision that determined the outcome of the war." Do you agree? Could Germany have won if it had not invaded the Soviet Union, or were its structural weaknesses already fatal?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** The two-front war has been considered a cardinal strategic error since ancient times. Why do military leaders repeatedly fall into it despite knowing this?

**2.** The Soviet Union's resilience depended on its ability to absorb enormous losses that would have broken most nations. What factors — cultural, political, geographical — determined that resilience? Are those factors reproducible?

**3.** Logistics is often called the most important factor in military success but receives far less attention than tactics or leadership. Why do you think logistics tends to be undervalued in historical and strategic narratives?

**4.** Germany's intelligence about Soviet industrial capacity was catastrophically wrong. What does this reveal about the limits of intelligence gathering and analysis, and how should decision-makers account for uncertainty?

**5.** Hitler made the Barbarossa decision largely against the advice of his professional military staff. What structures exist today to prevent national leaders from making unilateral strategic decisions with potentially catastrophic consequences?
