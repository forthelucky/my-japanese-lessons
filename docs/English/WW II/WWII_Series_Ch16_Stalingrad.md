### Example Sentences

**1. attrition**
> Stalingrad became a battle of pure attrition: each side fed men and machines into the ruins of the city, and the question became not who would win tactically but whose reserves would exhaust first.

**2. encirclement**
> Operation Uranus, the Soviet counter-offensive of November 1942, executed a classic double encirclement, trapping the German Sixth Army of three hundred thousand men inside the ruins of Stalingrad.

**3. morale**
> The morale effects of Stalingrad extended far beyond the armies that fought there: Germany's civilian population, which had been told the city was almost captured for months, could no longer trust official communiqués.

**4. capitulate**
> Field Marshal Paulus capitulated with approximately ninety thousand surviving soldiers on February 2, 1943 — the first German field marshal ever to surrender — and the symbolism was devastating.

**5. momentum**
> After Stalingrad, the strategic momentum of the Eastern Front shifted decisively to the Soviet Union and never returned to Germany. Every German operation thereafter was reactive rather than initiative-driven.

**6. catastrophic**
> The loss of the Sixth Army was catastrophic not only in material terms — three hundred thousand men, their equipment, and their irreplaceable experienced officers — but in psychological terms, destroying the myth of German military invincibility.

**7. reversal**
> Stalingrad represented the definitive reversal of the war's trajectory on the Eastern Front: what had been a German advance became a German retreat that would continue, with brief interruptions, until Berlin fell in 1945.

### The City That Broke an Army
#### Stalingrad and the Turning of the Eastern Front

In the summer of 1942, Germany launched its second great offensive in the Soviet Union, driving southeast toward the oil fields of the Caucasus. Hitler split his forces: one army group pushed toward the Caucasus oilfields, another toward a city on the Volga River that bore Stalin's name. The symbolic importance of Stalingrad became, for both sides, a reason not to yield when military logic might have suggested otherwise.

The battle that followed, from August 1942 to February 1943, became the most costly in human history. The city was methodically destroyed from the air and then fought over, block by block and floor by floor, in a kind of combat that rendered German advantages in mobility and coordination useless. The Soviets, under General Vasily Chuikov, deliberately fought at arm's length — keeping German and Soviet positions so close together that German air and artillery support would risk hitting their own men. They called it "hugging the enemy."

By November 1942, the German Sixth Army under Field Marshal Friedrich Paulus had fought its way into the city, at enormous cost, but had not conquered it. On November 19, the Soviets launched Operation Uranus — a massive flanking counter-offensive that targeted the weaker Romanian and Italian armies holding the German flanks. Within three days, the two Soviet pincers had met, encircling the Sixth Army inside the ruins of Stalingrad. Three hundred thousand men were trapped.

Hitler refused to permit a breakout, insisting the encirclement could be relieved from outside. Göring promised to supply the pocket by air — a promise that proved impossible to fulfil. Through December and January, the encircled army slowly starved and froze. On February 2, 1943, the remnants of the Sixth Army — some ninety thousand men — surrendered. Paulus had by then been promoted to Field Marshal by a message from Hitler; the promotion contained an implicit suggestion that he should kill himself. He chose not to.

The physical losses were immense. The psychological and symbolic damage was greater still. For the first time, the German public was officially informed of a catastrophic defeat. The myth of the invincible Wehrmacht was broken. On the Eastern Front, Soviet forces would continue advancing — with enormous losses, but advancing — for the next two years, until they reached Berlin.

## ✏️ Part 3 — Vocabulary in Use

**1.** Use **"attrition"** to describe a situation — in sport, business, or war — where the winning strategy was simply to outlast the opponent.

**2.** Use **"encirclement"** (literally or metaphorically) to describe a situation where someone was trapped with no good exits.

**3.** Use **"morale"** to discuss how the psychological state of a group affects its ability to perform under pressure.

**4.** Use **"capitulate"** to distinguish between a surrender that was honourable or inevitable and one that represented a failure of will or judgement.

**5.** Use **"momentum"** to describe how a series of successes — or failures — can build its own logic, making further success or failure more likely.

**6.** Use **"catastrophic"** to describe a failure — historical, organisational, or personal — and identify what single decision or factor made it so severe.

**7.** Use **"reversal"** to describe a moment in history or in your personal experience when the direction of events fundamentally changed.

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** Hitler refused a breakout order even when a breakout was still possible. What combination of psychological factors — pride, ideology, strategic miscalculation — might lead a commander to sacrifice an entire army rather than retreat?

**2.** The Soviet tactic of "hugging the enemy" — staying so close that German air and artillery support became impossible — deliberately nullified German technological advantages. What does this tell us about adapting to an opponent's strengths?

**3.** The text says the symbolic importance of the city made both sides unwilling to yield for non-military reasons. How does symbolism affect strategic decision-making, and is it ever justified?

**4.** Göring's promise to supply the pocket by air was impossible to fulfil, yet it was accepted. How do leaders evaluate extravagant promises made under pressure, and why do they so often believe what they want to hear?

**5.** The battle destroyed the "myth of German invincibility." How important is the psychological dimension of military reputation — and what happens to an army when that reputation collapses?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** Wars of attrition — from the trenches of the First World War to Stalingrad to modern conflicts — tend to be won by the side with the deeper reserves. Is there a lesson here for how nations should think about the sustainability of their commitments before entering conflicts?

**2.** Stalingrad is taught in military academies worldwide as a case study in encirclement, logistics failure, and command psychology. What do you think is the single most important lesson it offers?

**3.** The Soviet Union accepted losses at Stalingrad that would have broken most nations — perhaps a million casualties in the battle itself. What does this tell us about the relationship between the scale of a perceived threat and a nation's willingness to absorb costs?

**4.** Hitler's refusal to allow retreat at Stalingrad is a classic example of escalation of commitment — doubling down on a losing position rather than accepting the loss. Do you see parallels to this psychological pattern in modern institutional or personal decision-making?

**5.** Paulus refused Hitler's implicit suggestion to commit suicide. Do you think he made the right decision? What obligations does a military commander have to their own soldiers, to their state, and to their own conscience when the two conflict?
