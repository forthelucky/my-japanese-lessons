### Example Sentences

**1. reparations**
> After the First World War, Germany was ordered to pay reparations so enormous that economists warned the terms would cripple the European economy for decades.

**2. hyperinflation**
> During the hyperinflation of 1923, the price of a loaf of bread in Germany rose so fast that workers demanded to be paid twice a day so they could spend their wages before the money lost its value.

**3. austerity**
> The government's austerity measures — cutting public services and raising taxes simultaneously — deepened the recession instead of resolving it, fuelling widespread public anger.

**4. demagogue**
> A skilled demagogue does not need to offer real solutions; it is enough to identify a villain and promise that removing that villain will restore greatness.

**5. scapegoat**
> Throughout history, minority communities have been scapegoated during times of economic crisis, serving as convenient targets for the frustrations of a suffering majority.

**6. sovereign**
> The treaty stripped Germany of sovereign control over key industrial regions, meaning that other nations could dictate the output of German factories and mines.

**7. destabilize**
> Few forces destabilize a democracy as effectively as sustained economic despair, because it erodes the public's belief that the system is capable of protecting them.

### The Versailles Trap
#### Humiliation, Hyperinflation, and the Death of Democratic Trust

The First World War ended in November 1918, but for Germany, the real ordeal had just begun. The Treaty of Versailles, signed in June 1919, imposed crushing terms: Germany lost thirteen percent of its territory, its army was capped at a hundred thousand men, and Article 231 — the "war guilt clause" — forced the country to accept full legal responsibility for the war. On that basis, the Allied powers demanded reparations of 132 billion gold marks, roughly three times Germany's annual economic output.

The psychological damage was immediate. The military high command, which bore actual responsibility for the defeat, successfully promoted the myth that Germany had been "stabbed in the back" by traitors at home — socialists, pacifists, and Jews. This *Dolchstoßlegende* was a fabrication, but a politically potent one. It redirected public rage toward vulnerable minorities and toward the democratic politicians who had signed the armistice. From its very birth, the Weimar Republic was associated in many German minds not with liberation, but with shame.

The reparations burden then collided with a financial system already hollowed out by wartime money-printing. Unable to raise enough tax revenue to meet payments, the government simply printed more currency. The result was catastrophic. In January 1921, one US dollar bought 60 German marks. By November 1923, that same dollar bought four trillion marks.

For ordinary citizens, the consequences were ruinous. A week's salary could become worthless before it was spent. Lifetime savings evaporated overnight. The middle-class war bonds bought out of patriotic duty were now worth less than the paper they were printed on. The wealthy, with access to foreign currency and hard assets, could protect themselves. A schoolteacher, a pensioner, a small shopkeeper had no such refuge.

This was more than an economic crisis. Money is a system of trust, and when a government allows its currency to collapse, it destroys not just purchasing power but the credibility of every institution meant to protect ordinary people. The German middle class — the social backbone of democratic values — emerged from the hyperinflation shattered. They had saved, worked, and obeyed the law. The reward was ruin.

A currency reform in late 1923 halted the spiral, and a brief stability followed. But the wounds never fully healed. When the Great Depression arrived in 1929, mass unemployment landed on top of a society still scarred by inflationary trauma. The conditions were perfect for a demagogue who promised to tear the system down entirely. Versailles and hyperinflation did not make Hitler's rise inevitable — but together, they built the psychological preconditions for it.

## ✏️ Part 3 — Vocabulary in Use

Use the vocabulary from Part 1 to construct your own sentences. Try to discuss real or hypothetical situations.

**1.** Use **"reparations"** to describe a situation — historical or contemporary — in which one party demands compensation from another. Do you think the demand was fair?

**2.** Use **"hyperinflation"** to explain what you think would happen to society if all your savings suddenly lost 90% of their value overnight.

**3.** Use **"austerity"** in a sentence about a government policy. Do you think austerity is ever a justified response to an economic crisis?

**4.** Use **"demagogue"** to describe either a historical or a fictional character who fits the definition.

**5.** Use **"scapegoat"** (as a verb) to describe a situation where a group was unfairly blamed for a larger social problem.

**6.** Use **"sovereign"** to make a point about a country's right to make its own economic decisions, even if those decisions are harmful.

**7.** Use **"destabilize"** to describe how one event — economic, political, or social — can trigger a chain reaction of instability.

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

Discuss the following questions with your teacher. There are no right or wrong answers — the goal is to build your argument clearly and support it with reasoning.

**1.** The text argues that hyperinflation destroyed not just savings, but social trust. Do you agree that a currency crisis can be as politically dangerous as a military defeat? Why or why not?

**2.** The middle class, which had "done everything right," was the group most devastated by the inflation. How does this kind of outcome — where responsible behaviour is punished — affect the way people view democratic institutions?

**3.** The "stab-in-the-back legend" was a fabrication, yet millions of people believed it. What psychological needs does a conspiracy theory like this fulfill for people who are suffering?

**4.** The text mentions that a brief period of stability in the mid-1920s temporarily reduced support for extremist movements. What does this suggest about the relationship between economic conditions and political radicalism?

**5.** The chapter's central argument is that the Treaty of Versailles created the *preconditions* for fascism, but did not make it *inevitable*. At what point do preconditions become so severe that a catastrophic outcome starts to feel almost unavoidable?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

These questions go beyond the text. Use the themes of this chapter as a springboard for broader thinking.

**1.** When a government makes decisions that destroy ordinary people's savings — whether through hyperinflation, financial mismanagement, or corrupt privatization — who should be held accountable? Is accountability even possible in such situations?

**2.** Many historians argue that the Versailles Treaty was so punitive that it made a second war almost unavoidable. Others argue that the real problem was that it was not enforced strictly enough. Which perspective do you find more convincing, and why?

**3.** Consider a modern parallel: a country experiencing rapid currency collapse (such as Venezuela or Zimbabwe). To what extent do you think the political dynamics mirror what happened in Weimar Germany? What is different?

**4.** The text suggests that people who feel economically betrayed become receptive to extreme political messages. How should moderate democratic leaders respond when a large portion of the population begins to distrust the entire system?

**5.** Is it ever justifiable for a victorious nation to demand massive reparations from a defeated one? What principles should guide post-war settlements, based on what you have learned from the Versailles example?
