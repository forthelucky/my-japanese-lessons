### Example Sentences

**1. industrialization**
> Japan's rapid industrialization after the Meiji Restoration was remarkable, but it exposed a structural vulnerability: the country lacked the domestic raw materials to sustain the factories it had built.

**2. raw materials**
> A nation that must import virtually all of its raw materials — iron, coal, rubber, and oil — is perpetually one trade disruption away from economic paralysis.

**3. self-sufficiency**
> The pursuit of agricultural self-sufficiency led many interwar governments to introduce tariffs that, while protecting farmers, raised food costs for urban workers.

**4. expansionism**
> Expansionism in the early twentieth century was often justified domestically as a civilizing mission, even as it destroyed the sovereignty of the peoples being absorbed.

**5. protectorate**
> Japan established a protectorate over Korea in 1905, meaning that Korean foreign policy was now decided in Tokyo, not Seoul.

**6. geopolitics**
> The geopolitics of the Pacific in the 1930s were shaped less by ideology than by the simple question of who controlled access to oil and rubber.

**7. mandate**
> After the First World War, Japan received a League of Nations mandate over former German islands in the Pacific, quietly turning them into fortified military bases.

### Japan's Resource Dilemma
#### The Structural Trap Behind Imperial Expansion

In 1868, a small group of samurai reformers overthrew the feudal Tokugawa shogunate and launched one of the most dramatic national transformations in modern history. The Meiji Restoration, as it came to be known, set Japan on a deliberate course of westernization. Within a single generation, the country built railways, established a modern army and navy, created a constitutional government, and developed a substantial industrial base. By 1905, Japan had defeated Russia in open warfare — the first time an Asian nation had beaten a European power in modern combat — and announced itself as a major force in world affairs.

But the Meiji miracle contained a structural flaw that its architects could not resolve: Japan was an island nation with almost no domestic deposits of the raw materials its new industrial economy required. There was insufficient iron ore for its steel mills, insufficient coal for its power plants, and — critically — almost no domestic oil supply for the naval fleet and motorized army on which its great-power status depended. Cotton for its textile industry had to be imported. Rubber for its vehicles came entirely from Southeast Asia. The more successful Japan's industrialization became, the deeper its dependence on foreign supply chains grew.

This was not merely an economic inconvenience. In an era of rising nationalism and trade barriers, dependence on imported resources meant dependence on the goodwill of other powers — primarily Britain, the United States, and the Netherlands, which controlled the resource-rich territories of Southeast Asia. Japanese strategists drew a stark conclusion: a nation that could not feed its industries from its own soil was not truly sovereign. Economic security, they argued, required territorial control over resource-producing regions.

This logic drove Japan's imperial expansion onto the Asian continent. Korea was annexed in 1910. Influence in Manchuria was steadily expanded throughout the 1920s. Each acquisition was justified partly in strategic terms — buffer zones, access routes — and partly in the language of pan-Asian solidarity. But underneath the rhetoric, the driving calculation was resource arithmetic: more territory meant more coal, more iron, more agricultural land for a population that was growing faster than its island home could feed.

The tragedy was that expansion did not resolve the dilemma — it deepened it. Each new territory required military garrisons and administrative infrastructure, consuming resources rather than simply producing them. Each aggressive move alarmed the Western powers that controlled the oil Japan desperately needed, bringing economic sanctions closer. Japan had built an industrial machine that required constant external fuel, and its attempts to secure that fuel by force would ultimately bring it into direct collision with the United States — a nation whose industrial capacity dwarfed its own.

## ✏️ Part 3 — Vocabulary in Use

**1.** Use **"industrialization"** to describe how rapid economic development can create new vulnerabilities alongside new strengths.

**2.** Use **"raw materials"** to discuss whether a modern country today faces similar supply-chain risks to Japan in the 1930s.

**3.** Use **"self-sufficiency"** to argue either for or against a country's efforts to reduce dependence on imports.

**4.** Use **"expansionism"** to describe a historical or contemporary situation where a nation expanded beyond its borders. Was it justified?

**5.** Use **"protectorate"** in a sentence that explains the difference between formal colonialism and this softer form of control.

**6.** Use **"geopolitics"** to explain why the location and size of a country affects its foreign policy choices.

**7.** Use **"mandate"** to discuss who should have the authority to govern a territory whose population did not choose that governance.

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** The text argues that Japan's resource dependence made expansionism feel like a logical necessity to its leaders. Does economic need ever make territorial aggression justifiable?

**2.** Japan's industrialization made it stronger militarily but more vulnerable economically. Can rapid development create more instability than it resolves?

**3.** The Meiji reformers modelled Japan on Western powers — and then used Western imperial methods themselves. What does this suggest about the relationship between power and the norms used to legitimize it?

**4.** The text says each new territory "consumed resources rather than simply producing them." Why do empires so often find that expansion increases costs faster than it increases revenues?

**5.** Japan's strategic planners saw imported resources as a threat to sovereignty. To what extent is economic interdependence a source of peace, and to what extent is it a source of vulnerability?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** Many countries today — including South Korea — depend heavily on imported energy and raw materials. How do they manage this vulnerability differently from Japan in the 1930s?

**2.** The text says Japan's leaders concluded that "economic security required territorial control." How do modern states pursue resource security without territorial expansion?

**3.** Is it possible for a small, resource-poor island nation to become a genuine great power in today's world? What would that require?

**4.** The Meiji transformation is often cited as a model of successful modernization. What aspects of that story do you find admirable, and what aspects do you find troubling?

**5.** China today is investing heavily in African resources and global infrastructure through the Belt and Road Initiative. Do you see parallels with Japan's pre-war strategy, or is the situation fundamentally different?
