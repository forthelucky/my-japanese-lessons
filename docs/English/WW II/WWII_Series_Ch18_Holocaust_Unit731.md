### Example Sentences

**1. genocide**
> The Holocaust was not a spontaneous eruption of violence but a systematically planned genocide, bureaucratically organized and industrially executed over more than four years.

**2. bureaucracy**
> Hannah Arendt's phrase "the banality of evil" described how ordinary bureaucracy — filing forms, following procedures, meeting quotas — enabled the Holocaust: each participant could tell themselves they were simply doing their job.

**3. dehumanize**
> Before a state can organize mass murder, it must first dehumanize the victims in language and law — reducing them to categories, stripping their legal status, and making their suffering invisible to bystanders.

**4. complicity**
> Complicity in the Holocaust extended far beyond the SS and the concentration camp guards — it involved railway workers, local police forces, government ministries, and business corporations that supplied materials and labour.

**5. perpetrator**
> Studies of Holocaust perpetrators have consistently found that the majority were not psychopaths or fanatics but ordinary people who had been placed in institutional contexts that made refusal difficult and participation expected.

**6. atrocity**
> Unit 731's atrocities — vivisection, deliberate infection with plague and cholera, exposure experiments — were conducted under the guise of scientific research, a framing that allowed their perpetrators to maintain a professional self-image.

**7. accountability**
> The absence of accountability for Unit 731's crimes — its leaders received immunity in exchange for data — created a historical grievance that continues to affect Japan's relationships with China and Korea today.

### When Systems Become Instruments of Murder
#### The Holocaust and Unit 731: The Industrialization of Atrocity

The Second World War produced unprecedented military destruction. It also produced something that the world had not previously seen at such scale: the systematic, industrialized killing of human beings treated not as enemies to be defeated but as categories of person to be eliminated.

In Germany, the Holocaust was the culmination of a process that began with legal discrimination, moved through forced emigration and expropriation, and arrived at mass murder through a combination of wartime radicalization and bureaucratic efficiency. The six major death camps — Auschwitz, Treblinka, Sobibor, Belzec, Chelmno, Majdanek — were not improvised. They were engineered facilities, each with an administrative structure, a transport logistics system connected to the Reich's railway network, and a workforce that included not only SS guards but German civilian technicians, accountants, and managers. Approximately six million Jews, alongside hundreds of thousands of Roma, disabled people, and political prisoners, were systematically murdered.

What made the Holocaust possible was not the presence of unusually sadistic individuals — though such individuals existed — but the organization of mass murder as a bureaucratic task. Hannah Arendt, covering the trial of Adolf Eichmann, described the concept she called "the banality of evil": the observation that many of the most consequential perpetrators were not monsters but ordinary men, executing orders within institutional frameworks that normalized what they were doing. The horror was not that evil required exceptional people — it was that it required only ordinary ones, placed in the right institutional context.

In Japan, Unit 731 was a secret military medical unit based in occupied Manchuria that conducted lethal experiments on human subjects — primarily Chinese, Korean, and Soviet prisoners — under the direction of General Shiro Ishii. Subjects were deliberately infected with plague, cholera, and typhus. Experiments were conducted on live, conscious patients. Estimates of the death toll range from three thousand to over twelve thousand. Unlike the Nuremberg trials, which prosecuted Nazi war criminals, Unit 731's leadership received immunity from prosecution in exchange for sharing their research data with the United States Army.

Both the Holocaust and Unit 731 illustrate the same terrible principle: when a modern state's bureaucratic and technological capacity is directed against a group that has been stripped of legal protection and moral status, the scale of destruction it can achieve is effectively unlimited.

## ✏️ Part 3 — Vocabulary in Use

**1.** Use **"genocide"** to discuss what legal and moral obligations the international community has to prevent or respond to it.

**2.** Use **"bureaucracy"** to explain how administrative systems can enable wrongdoing by distributing responsibility across many participants.

**3.** Use **"dehumanize"** to describe the process by which language and law can strip a group of their moral and legal standing before physical violence begins.

**4.** Use **"complicity"** to discuss where responsibility lies in a situation where many people contribute to a harmful outcome without any single individual doing everything.

**5.** Use **"perpetrator"** to discuss what research on ordinary people who commit atrocities tells us about human nature and institutional design.

**6.** Use **"atrocity"** to argue for or against the proposition that some acts are beyond any possible justification, regardless of context.

**7.** Use **"accountability"** to discuss whether justice for historical crimes is achievable or necessary when the perpetrators are dead.

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** The text describes Holocaust perpetrators as "ordinary people placed in institutional contexts that made refusal difficult." Does this explanation make the Holocaust more or less disturbing than an explanation based on individual evil?

**2.** The Holocaust required railway workers, accountants, and technicians who were not directly involved in killing. At what point does professional participation in a system become moral responsibility for what that system does?

**3.** Unit 731's leaders received immunity in exchange for research data. What does this decision reveal about how the United States government weighed scientific gain against justice in 1945? Do you think it was the right decision?

**4.** The text says the Holocaust began with legal discrimination and moved through forced emigration before arriving at mass murder. What does this gradualism tell us about how mass atrocities develop, and what opportunities existed to interrupt the process?

**5.** Both atrocities were conducted under institutional framings — one as a state security operation, one as scientific research — that allowed participants to maintain a professional self-image. How does institutional framing affect individual moral perception?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** The concept of "the banality of evil" — that ordinary people in the wrong institutional context can commit extraordinary crimes — has been both celebrated and criticized. Do you find it convincing? What are its implications for how we design institutions?

**2.** The Holocaust produced the Nuremberg trials and ultimately the Genocide Convention and the International Criminal Court. Have these institutions been effective at preventing atrocities? What are their limitations?

**3.** Unit 731 is far less well-known internationally than the Holocaust. Why do some atrocities receive sustained global attention while others remain relatively obscure? Does this difference have consequences?

**4.** Several corporations — including major German industrial companies — used forced labour from concentration camps. Some of these companies still exist today. What do you think their current obligations to acknowledge and address this history are?

**5.** The text argues that mass atrocity requires the prior dehumanization of the victims in law and language. Can you identify contemporary examples of dehumanizing language directed at specific groups? What responsibilities do politicians, media, and ordinary citizens have in response?
