### Example Sentences

**1. supply chain**
> Japan's island garrisons depended entirely on supply chains stretching thousands of kilometres across open ocean; once American submarines and aircraft began interdicting those chains, the garrisons slowly starved regardless of how hard they fought.

**2. bypass**
> The genius of island-hopping was the willingness to bypass heavily defended Japanese positions, leaving them isolated and irrelevant rather than wasting lives on frontal assaults against prepared defences.

**3. fortify**
> Japan had heavily fortified its island outposts, constructing elaborate tunnel networks and underground positions that direct bombardment could not destroy — but fortifications are only useful if they can be reinforced and supplied.

**4. leapfrog**
> MacArthur's leapfrogging strategy advanced the Allied front line hundreds of kilometres in a matter of months, at a fraction of the cost that a sequential island-by-island assault would have required.

**5. perimeter**
> Japan's strategic perimeter — the ring of island bases intended to defend the home islands — was designed for a short war followed by negotiation; against America's industrial output, it simply could not be maintained.

**6. interdiction**
> American submarine interdiction of Japanese merchant shipping became one of the most strategically decisive campaigns of the Pacific war, destroying the supply lifeline connecting Japan to the resources it had gone to war to secure.

**7. isolate**
> The large Japanese garrison at Rabaul — one hundred thousand troops — was isolated by the island-hopping advance and simply left in place, consuming Japanese resources to supply while contributing nothing to the defence of the perimeter.

### Skipping to Victory
#### Island-Hopping and the Supply Chain War in the Pacific

After Midway, the United States faced a strategic problem of daunting scale: Japan occupied an enormous arc of island territories stretching from the Aleutians in the north to New Guinea in the south, thousands of kilometres from any American base of operations. The obvious approach — a sequential assault, capturing each Japanese-held island in turn while fighting through its fortified garrison — would have required years and hundreds of thousands of casualties. The American commanders chose a different approach.

The island-hopping strategy, developed primarily by Admiral Chester Nimitz and General Douglas MacArthur, rested on a simple but powerful insight: the goal was not to defeat every Japanese soldier in the Pacific — it was to destroy Japan's ability to sustain the war. Japanese island garrisons, however determined, were only dangerous if they could be supplied and reinforced. An island that could be isolated — cut off from the supply chain that connected it to Japan — would wither on its own, without the cost of a frontal assault.

This meant identifying which islands were strategically necessary — those with harbours, airfields, or command of key sea lanes — and bypassing the rest. Strongly defended Japanese positions that controlled nothing essential were simply surrounded, cut off, and left to become irrelevant. Rabaul, the massive Japanese base in the Southwest Pacific with over a hundred thousand troops, was bypassed entirely and left isolated for the remainder of the war. MacArthur advanced up the coast of New Guinea in a series of rapid amphibious landings, each one placing American air power within range of the next target, leapfrogging hundreds of kilometres at a time.

Simultaneously, American submarines conducted a devastating campaign against Japanese merchant shipping. Japan had gone to war to secure the oil, rubber, and metals of Southeast Asia. Those resources could only reach Japan's factories and fuel Japan's war machine by ship. By 1944, American submarines were sinking Japanese merchant vessels faster than they could be replaced. The oil that Japan had invaded Southeast Asia to obtain was sitting in storage depots it could no longer ship home.

The island-hopping campaign demonstrated a principle that applied far beyond the Pacific: in any extended conflict, the ability to sustain your forces — to maintain the supply chain of men, fuel, ammunition, and morale — ultimately determines who wins, regardless of how individual battles are fought.

## ✏️ Part 3 — Vocabulary in Use

**1.** Use **"supply chain"** to explain why disrupting an opponent's logistics is often more effective than defeating them in direct combat.

**2.** Use **"bypass"** to describe a situation — military, professional, or personal — where avoiding a difficult obstacle directly, rather than confronting it, proved to be the better strategy.

**3.** Use **"fortify"** to discuss the difference between building strong defences and building defences that can actually be sustained.

**4.** Use **"leapfrog"** to describe how a competitor, technology, or country can skip intermediate stages of development to reach a more advanced position.

**5.** Use **"perimeter"** (in a strategic or metaphorical sense) to describe the limits of a position — what it can and cannot defend.

**6.** Use **"interdiction"** to explain how cutting off an enemy's resources can be more decisive than defeating their forces directly.

**7.** Use **"isolate"** to describe a strategy of removing an opponent from their support network rather than engaging them directly.

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** The island-hopping strategy accepted that some Japanese garrisons would never be defeated — just isolated. Is it ever strategically sound to leave an undefeated enemy force in your rear?

**2.** The text says the goal was not to defeat every Japanese soldier but to destroy Japan's ability to sustain the war. How does identifying the correct strategic objective change the methods available to achieve it?

**3.** Japan's island defences were formidable against direct assault but useless once isolated. What does this reveal about the limitations of purely defensive strategies?

**4.** American submarines made the resources Japan had gone to war to secure effectively unreachable. Is there a bitter irony in this outcome? What does it suggest about the relationship between strategic goals and the ability to actually exploit them?

**5.** MacArthur and Nimitz had somewhat different approaches to the campaign and significant personal rivalry. How do competing command structures and personal ambitions affect the execution of a joint strategy?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** The island-hopping strategy is studied in business schools as an example of resource-efficient competitive strategy — achieving strategic objectives while avoiding costly direct confrontation. Can you describe a business equivalent?

**2.** Modern supply chain vulnerabilities — revealed dramatically by the COVID-19 pandemic — share some structural characteristics with Japan's wartime dependence on maritime shipping. What lessons from the Pacific war apply to modern supply chain resilience?

**3.** The decision to bypass Rabaul's hundred thousand troops rather than assault it was controversial at the time. How do decision-makers overcome the psychological resistance to leaving a visible, undefeated threat behind?

**4.** American submarine warfare against Japanese merchant shipping was extraordinarily effective but largely invisible in popular histories of the Pacific war. Why do indirect, logistical campaigns tend to receive less attention than dramatic battles?

**5.** Japan built its strategic perimeter assuming a short war that would end in negotiation. When did Japanese leadership realize this assumption was wrong, and why did they continue fighting despite that realization?
