### Example Sentences

**1. blitzkrieg**
> Blitzkrieg was not merely speed — it was the systematic targeting of the enemy's command and communication infrastructure, designed to paralyse decision-making before resistance could be organized.

**2. armour**
> France had more tanks than Germany in 1940 and in many respects better ones, yet French armour was dispersed along the front line in the First World War manner, while German armour was concentrated for a single devastating thrust.

**3. encirclement**
> The encirclement of the Allied armies at Dunkirk in May 1940 — trapping three hundred thousand troops against the sea — was the direct result of Germany's armoured breakthrough at Sedan, which the French high command had declared impossible.

**4. disruption**
> The lesson of blitzkrieg applies beyond military history: a disruptive approach does not need to be stronger than the incumbent system — it needs only to be faster than the incumbent's ability to adapt.

**5. doctrine**
> France's military doctrine in 1940 was rooted in the experience of 1914–18, when static defence had eventually prevailed; the result was an army perfectly optimized for the last war rather than the current one.

**6. initiative**
> Once Germany seized the initiative at Sedan, the French command could only react — and each reaction came too late, because the situation had already changed by the time orders reached the front.

**7. coordination**
> The unprecedented coordination between tanks, aircraft, and radio-equipped infantry was what made blitzkrieg work; each element was available before, but no one had combined them in this particular way.

### Speed as a Weapon
#### How Blitzkrieg Paralysed a Well-Equipped Enemy

On May 10, 1940, Germany launched its offensive against France and the Low Countries. France had an army widely considered the finest in the world. Its defences were fortified. Its equipment was plentiful, and in terms of tank quality, arguably superior to Germany's. The British Expeditionary Force stood alongside it. By June 22, France had surrendered — in six weeks.

The result shocked the world. It still requires explanation.

Germany's victory was not primarily a matter of superior numbers or superior equipment. It was a matter of superior doctrine — a method of warfare so different from anything France's generals had trained for that they could not adapt quickly enough to survive it. The concept, which journalists labelled *Blitzkrieg* or "lightning war," combined three existing technologies in a new operational philosophy: armoured tanks concentrated into independent panzer divisions, close air support from dive-bombers that served as mobile artillery, and radio communications linking all elements in real time.

The French army, drawing its strategic lessons from the First World War, had designed a system for slow, methodical defence. Tanks were distributed along the front to support infantry. Command decisions flowed upward through hierarchy before flowing back down as orders — a process that took hours. The Maginot Line, an elaborate fortification system, protected the border with Germany. France was prepared for the war of 1918.

Germany attacked through the Ardennes forest, which French planners had declared impassable to armour. They were wrong. A massive armoured column broke through in the centre, raced to the English Channel, and cut the Allied armies in two — all before the French high command had issued its first coherent response. When General Gamelin received the news of the breakthrough at Sedan, his staff officer reportedly said: "There is no reserve." The battle was effectively over before most of the fighting had occurred.

The lesson the German victory illustrated was not that blitzkrieg always wins, but that a system that cannot adapt faster than its adversary moves will be defeated by the movement itself, regardless of its overall strength.

## ✏️ Part 3 — Vocabulary in Use

**1.** Use **"blitzkrieg"** metaphorically to describe a rapid, unexpected strategy — in business, politics, or sport — that overwhelmed a stronger competitor.

**2.** Use **"armour"** (or its metaphorical equivalent) to describe the defensive structures or protections that an individual, organization, or country relies on.

**3.** Use **"encirclement"** to describe a situation — not necessarily military — where someone found themselves trapped with no good options.

**4.** Use **"disruption"** to discuss a technological or strategic innovation that made an established industry or approach obsolete.

**5.** Use **"doctrine"** to describe a governing philosophy — military, business, or personal — that shapes how decisions are made.

**6.** Use **"initiative"** to describe a situation where acting first, rather than reacting, determined the outcome.

**7.** Use **"coordination"** to explain why combining several ordinary elements effectively can be more powerful than any single exceptional element.

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** France had superior tanks in some respects yet lost in six weeks. The text attributes this to doctrine. Is doctrine — the philosophy behind how you use your resources — more important than the resources themselves?

**2.** France's military thinking was optimized for the last war. How do organizations — military or otherwise — avoid the trap of preparing perfectly for a threat that has already passed?

**3.** The text says blitzkrieg paralysed French decision-making by moving faster than orders could travel. How does speed of action compare to speed of decision as a military or competitive advantage?

**4.** The Ardennes was declared "impassable to armour" and the French relied on that assumption. What are the risks of building a strategic plan around a key assumption that has not been adequately tested?

**5.** The text draws a parallel between military disruption and competitive disruption more broadly. How useful do you find this comparison? What are its limits?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** The blitzkrieg story is often cited in business strategy as an example of how a less powerful competitor can defeat a larger one through innovation. Can you think of a business example where this pattern played out?

**2.** France's army was, by most measures, excellent at what it was designed to do — it was just designed for the wrong problem. How should organizations assess whether they are solving the right problem?

**3.** The decisive factor at Sedan was speed of decision — Germany's commanders could respond and adapt faster than France's. In today's organizations, what slows down decision-making, and how do the fastest decision-makers maintain their edge?

**4.** Blitzkrieg required deep coordination between three different military arms. What are the biggest obstacles to cross-functional coordination in large organizations, and how are they typically overcome?

**5.** Germany's generals had been sceptical about blitzkrieg tactics but were proven spectacularly wrong. What should organizations do when internal sceptics challenge a new approach? How do you distinguish genuine insight from institutional resistance to change?
