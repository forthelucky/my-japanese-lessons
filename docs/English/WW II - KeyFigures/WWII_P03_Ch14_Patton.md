### Example Sentences

**1. armoured warfare**
> Patton's mastery of armoured warfare was not theoretical — he had driven the US Army's first tank into battle in WWI and had spent the following two decades thinking about how to use tanks at speed in a way that prevented the enemy from establishing defensive positions.

**2. maverick**
> Patton was the quintessential military maverick: he wore ivory-handled revolvers, quoted ancient generals as personal acquaintances, used profanity as a motivational tool, and achieved results that would have been impossible for more conventional commanders.

**3. momentum**
> After the breakout at Saint-Lô in July 1944, Patton's Third Army advanced so fast that its momentum became the defining feature of Allied operations — outrunning not just the retreating Germans but also its own fuel supplies.

**4. relieved of command**
> Patton was twice relieved of command — after the slapping incidents in Sicily and after a post-war speech comparing Nazis to Democrats — demonstrating that brilliant results did not provide unlimited immunity from institutional consequences.

**5. exploitation**
> The exploitation of the Normandy breakout — turning a tactical success into a strategic transformation — was precisely the type of operation Patton was designed for: unlimited advance, no concern for flanks, maximum speed.

**6. deception operation**
> Patton's participation in Operation Fortitude — the deception that convinced Germany the main Allied invasion would be at Pas-de-Calais rather than Normandy — was based on German belief in his offensive capabilities. His fake "army" pinned multiple German divisions in place during the real invasion.

**7. eccentricity**
> Patton's eccentricity — the speeches, the uniforms, the declarations that he had been a warrior in past lives — was partly genuine and partly calculated theatre, designed to project the image of an unstoppable warrior that he wanted both his troops and his enemies to believe.

## 📝 Part 2 — Reading Passage

---

### The God of War and His Limits
#### George S. Patton and the Price of Genius Without Restraint

George Patton believed he was the reincarnation of ancient warriors — Hannibal, Napoleon, Scipio Africanus. He spoke of his military instincts as the product of a soul that had been a soldier in every lifetime. Whether this was genuine belief or carefully cultivated persona, it produced a commander whose absolute conviction in the inevitability of victory generated an offensive energy in his forces that was qualitatively different from anything his contemporaries could produce.

Patton's Third Army, activated in August 1944, covered more ground more quickly than any army in the history of warfare. In eleven days, it advanced 400 miles across France. It consumed fuel so fast that the Allied logistics system could not keep up — leading to the famous episode in which Eisenhower's chief logistics officer reportedly complained that if Patton had been given the fuel he demanded, he would have been in Germany in two weeks.

The problem with Patton was not his tactical or operational gifts, which were unquestionable. It was his complete inability to inhabit the institutional role that came with his authority. He struck a hospitalised soldier in Sicily, calling him a coward — and was nearly fired. He made public speeches that contradicted official Allied policy. He got into arguments with Soviet officers that risked diplomatic incidents. He had to be managed, by Eisenhower, at enormous cost in time and institutional energy.

The fundamental question Patton poses is whether exceptional performance provides unlimited institutional license — whether results so extraordinary that they cannot be replicated by anyone else justify the costs of the behaviour that comes with them. Eisenhower's answer, ultimately, was no: there is a point beyond which the institutional damage exceeds the operational value, even for genius.

Patton died in a car accident in December 1945, never having had to answer for himself in peacetime.

## ✏️ Part 3 — Vocabulary in Use

**1.** Use **"armoured warfare"** to discuss Patton's specific insight about how tanks should be used — what was different about his approach compared to more conventional commanders?

**2.** Use **"maverick"** to discuss how organisations can retain mavericks who produce extraordinary results without allowing their disruptive behaviour to destroy the institution.

**3.** Use **"momentum"** to explain why Patton consistently argued that stopping an advance was more dangerous than continuing it, even when flanks were exposed.

**4.** Use **"relieved of command"** to discuss whether Eisenhower's management of Patton — tolerating him, disciplining him, keeping him — was optimal. What would you have done differently?

**5.** Use **"exploitation"** to explain why the breakthrough phase is more important in mobile warfare than the initial attack, and why Patton specialised in it.

**6.** Use **"deception operation"** to evaluate Patton's role in Fortitude. He contributed more to D-Day without commanding a real army than most generals with real armies.

**7.** Use **"eccentricity"** to discuss whether Patton's behaviour was genuinely eccentric or strategically calculated. Does it matter?

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** Patton believed he was a reincarnated warrior. Regardless of whether this was literally true, how did this belief affect his behaviour and performance? Can false beliefs be strategically useful?

**2.** Patton struck a hospitalised soldier he considered a coward. How should institutional leaders handle individuals who produce extraordinary results but engage in clearly unacceptable behaviour?

**3.** The text asks whether "exceptional performance provides unlimited institutional license." How do you answer this question, and does it depend on the organisation or the behaviour?

**4.** Eisenhower spent enormous time managing Patton. The text describes this as a "cost." But Patton produced results no one else could. How do we calculate this trade-off?

**5.** How do organisations manage brilliant "mavericks" who deliver extraordinary results but constantly break the rules?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** Patton is the subject of an Academy Award-winning film that presents him as a romantic hero. How does popular culture shape our evaluation of morally complex historical figures?

**2.** The image of the warrior — aggressive, rule-breaking, driven by passion rather than calculation — has a powerful appeal in military mythology. How does this image distort the reality of what effective military leadership requires?

**3.** Patton's fuel was diverted to Montgomery's Market Garden operation rather than his Rhine advance. Market Garden failed; Patton's drive continued after being re-fuelled. How should the Allied command have allocated limited fuel in autumn 1944?

**4.** Mavericks exist in organisations of all types — corporations, sports teams, research laboratories. What conditions produce them? What conditions allow them to be productive rather than destructive?

**5.** Patton died before he could be held accountable for his post-war behaviour — his admiration for German officers, his publicly racist statements, his apparent willingness to restart conflict with the Soviet Union. How does his death before accountability change the evaluation of his legacy?
