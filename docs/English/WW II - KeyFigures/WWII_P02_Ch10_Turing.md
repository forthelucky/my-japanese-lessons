### Example Sentences

**1. cryptography**
> Turing's wartime contribution was the application of mathematics and systematic cryptography to break a cipher that German military planners believed was computationally unbreakable.

**2. Enigma**
> The Enigma machine's design created an almost incomprehensibly large number of possible settings — over 150 quintillion — which is why the Germans were confident it could not be broken by any human team working without mechanical assistance.

**3. algorithm**
> Turing's breakthrough was to design an algorithm that exploited known weaknesses in German operator habits and Enigma's own structural limitations, allowing the Bombe machine to eliminate incorrect settings systematically.

**4. information asymmetry**
> Ultra — the intelligence product of breaking Enigma — created a profound information asymmetry: Allied commanders knew German orders and positions while the Germans believed their communications were secure. This asymmetry shaped the outcome of every major campaign after 1941.

**5. Turing machine**
> The Turing machine — a thought experiment about the theoretical limits of computation, published in 1936 — was not intended as a practical invention but as a mathematical proof; yet it described the essential architecture of every digital computer ever built.

**6. persecution**
> Turing's persecution for homosexuality — then a criminal offence in Britain — began in 1952, seven years after the war he had helped to win ended. He was subjected to chemical castration as an alternative to prison. He died in 1954.

**7. posthumous pardon**
> The British government issued Turing a posthumous pardon in 2013 — sixty years after his conviction. In 2017, the "Turing Law" extended the pardon to all men historically convicted under the same statutes, acknowledging a systematic injustice.

## 📝 Part 2 — Reading Passage

### The Man Who Shortened the War
#### Alan Turing, Information Superiority, and the Price of Genius

Winston Churchill is supposed to have called Alan Turing "the man who contributed most to the Allied victory in the Second World War." Whether Churchill actually said this is debated; that the sentiment is defensible is not. Historians have estimated that the intelligence produced at Bletchley Park — based on the work Turing did to break the Enigma cipher — shortened the war in Europe by two to four years and saved between fourteen and twenty-one million lives.

Turing came to Bletchley Park as a Cambridge mathematician who had already published the most important theoretical paper in computer science — his 1936 description of the "Turing machine" — before the war began. His approach to cryptanalysis was different from that of the linguists and crossword-solvers who had previously dominated British code-breaking: it was mathematical, systematic, and mechanised. He understood that human minds could not check 150 quintillion possible Enigma settings in useful time, but machines could.

The Bombe — the electro-mechanical computer Turing designed — worked by exploiting structural weaknesses in the Enigma system: German operators' habits, mandatory message formats, and the machine's inability to encrypt a letter as itself. These constraints sharply reduced the effective search space. By 1943, Bletchley Park was breaking German naval, army, and air force codes with near-real-time speed. Allied commanders knew German orders almost as quickly as German subordinates did.

The consequences were enormous. At the Battle of the Atlantic, Ultra intelligence directed Allied convoys away from U-boat wolfpacks, ending the tonnage crisis that had threatened to starve Britain. In North Africa, Rommel's supply convoys — whose routes and timings were visible to Allied intelligence — were decimated before they reached port. Before D-Day, the deception operation that convinced Hitler that Normandy was a feint succeeded partly because German commanders' communications about their uncertainty were visible to Eisenhower.

After the war, Turing was prosecuted for "gross indecency" — being gay — and subjected to chemical castration. He died in 1954, almost certainly by suicide, aged 41. The government that prosecuted him had classified his wartime contributions so completely that many of his contemporaries never knew what he had done. He was not publicly acknowledged for decades.

## ✏️ Part 3 — Vocabulary in Use

**1.** Use **"cryptography"** to discuss the relationship between mathematics and warfare. How has this relationship evolved in the cyber age?

**2.** Use **"Enigma"** to explain why German planners believed it was unbreakable, and what assumptions proved incorrect.

**3.** Use **"algorithm"** to explain Turing's core insight: that a systematic procedure could do what human intelligence could not — search enormous solution spaces quickly.

**4.** Use **"information asymmetry"** to discuss how knowing your enemy's plans changes the entire nature of a conflict, even if military resources remain unchanged.

**5.** Use **"Turing machine"** to discuss the gap between theoretical ideas and practical applications. Turing's thought experiment became the architecture of all digital computers. Can you think of other examples of abstract theory producing unexpected practical consequences?

**6.** Use **"persecution"** to discuss the injustice of Turing's treatment, and what it reveals about how societies treat the individuals who save them.

**7.** Use **"posthumous pardon"** to discuss what such a pardon actually achieves — and what it cannot.

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** The text says Turing's Ultra intelligence "shaped the outcome of every major campaign after 1941." If this is true, why did no government publicly acknowledge his contribution until decades after his death?

**2.** Turing's contribution was "information and algorithms" rather than "force or resources." How does this type of contribution challenge our conventional understanding of what determines military outcomes?

**3.** The men who prosecuted Turing for homosexuality were — by the logic of the intelligence classification system — unaware of what he had done for the country. Does this context change the moral evaluation of their actions?

**4.** Turing received a posthumous pardon in 2013. Is a pardon appropriate for someone who was guilty under the law that existed at the time? What does a posthumous pardon actually accomplish?

**5.** In modern conflicts or business, how has "information superiority" become more lethal than physical resources?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** Intelligence work — spying, code-breaking, surveillance — is invisible by nature. How should democracies provide oversight for activities that cannot be publicly disclosed without destroying their effectiveness?

**2.** Turing's contributions were classified for decades. What was lost — scientifically, culturally, historically — by this secrecy? How should governments balance secrecy and historical transparency?

**3.** The "Turing test" — the question of whether a machine can simulate human intelligence convincingly — is more relevant today than it has ever been. What do you think of current AI systems in relation to this standard?

**4.** Turing was gay in a country where this was illegal. He was also the man who saved that country. How should we think about the relationship between individual dignity and national belonging when the state itself has been unjust?

**5.** Contemporary cyber warfare — attacking infrastructure, stealing secrets, disrupting communications — is the direct descendant of Turing's work. How do you evaluate the moral and legal frameworks governing it today?
