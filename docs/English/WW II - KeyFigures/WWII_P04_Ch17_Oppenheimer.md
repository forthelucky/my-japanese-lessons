### Example Sentences

**1. Manhattan Project**
> The Manhattan Project brought together the greatest concentration of scientific talent in history — refugee physicists from Europe, American theoretical giants, engineers and chemists — under Oppenheimer's direction at Los Alamos, New Mexico.

**2. nuclear fission**
> The discovery that nuclear fission could release energy in the quantities required for a weapon had been made in Germany in 1938; the terrifying possibility that Hitler might develop an atomic bomb first was the primary motivation for the Allied programme.

**3. ethical dilemma**
> The ethical dilemma Oppenheimer faced was not whether to develop the bomb — by the time of Trinity, Germany had already surrendered — but whether to advocate for its use against Japanese cities, knowing what the consequences would be.

**4. project management**
> Oppenheimer's achievement as a project manager was arguably more remarkable than his scientific contributions: managing two thousand scientists with enormous egos, competing research approaches, and military security requirements simultaneously, while maintaining the morale and focus to achieve a result in three years.

**5. scientific responsibility**
> Oppenheimer's post-war advocacy for international control of nuclear weapons was a direct expression of scientific responsibility: he believed that scientists who had created weapons of mass destruction had an obligation to argue for their controlled use.

**6. security clearance**
> The 1954 hearing that stripped Oppenheimer of his security clearance was driven partly by McCarthyite anti-communist paranoia and partly by genuine security concerns about his pre-war associations — producing one of the most famous miscarriages of institutional justice in American history.

**7. nuclear deterrence**
> Oppenheimer was deeply sceptical of nuclear deterrence as a long-term strategy — he could not believe that a balance of terror between superpowers was stable indefinitely — and his scepticism made him politically dangerous to those who had committed to deterrence as policy.

## 📝 Part 2 — Reading Passage

---

### I Am Become Death
#### J. Robert Oppenheimer and the Burden of the Bomb

At 5:29 AM on July 16, 1945, in the New Mexico desert, the first nuclear device ever detonated produced a flash visible 160 miles away and a mushroom cloud that rose ten miles into the sky. J. Robert Oppenheimer, watching from the observation bunker, quoted the Bhagavad Gita: "Now I am become Death, the destroyer of worlds."

This moment is one of the most laden in human history — not just the beginning of the nuclear age, but the first instant in which a human being held in their hands the demonstrated capacity to destroy civilisation. Oppenheimer, the man most responsible for creating this capability, experienced it with appropriate gravity.

Oppenheimer was a theoretical physicist of genuine distinction, but his greatest achievement at Los Alamos was organisational. The Manhattan Project assembled two thousand scientists from multiple nations, multiple disciplines, and multiple theoretical traditions — many of them brilliant, all of them independent-minded, several of them constitutionally incapable of taking orders. Managing this ensemble, keeping it focused on a technically unprecedented goal, under wartime secrecy and military oversight, was an achievement that no contemporary commentator fully appreciated. Oppenheimer did it in three years.

The moral question — whether the bombs should have been dropped on populated Japanese cities, whether alternatives (demonstration detonation, modified surrender terms) were adequately explored — haunted Oppenheimer for the rest of his life. He did not oppose the decision in 1945, but he spent the following decade opposing the development of the hydrogen bomb and advocating for international controls on nuclear weapons. These positions made him enemies in a government increasingly shaped by Cold War logic.

In 1954, he was called before the Atomic Energy Commission, stripped of his security clearance, and publicly humiliated in a hearing that amounted to a political trial. He was rehabilitated, partially, in 1963 when President Johnson awarded him the Fermi Prize. He died in 1967.

In 2022, the US government formally vacated the 1954 finding against him. He had been, in the most literal sense, both the nation's greatest scientific asset and its most celebrated victim.

**1.** Use **"Manhattan Project"** to discuss what specifically made it feasible — the combination of talent, resources, urgency, and leadership that produced results in three years.

**2.** Use **"nuclear fission"** to explain why the discovery of nuclear fission created an immediate and urgent military research programme. What was the nature of the threat?

**3.** Use **"ethical dilemma"** to reconstruct the moral calculations facing scientists who had created the bomb by the time Germany surrendered. What arguments existed on each side?

**4.** Use **"project management"** to argue that Oppenheimer's management achievement was as important as his scientific contribution. What specific management skills did it require?

**5.** Use **"scientific responsibility"** to discuss whether scientists bear ongoing responsibility for how their innovations are used, decades after they are created.

**6.** Use **"security clearance"** to discuss the 1954 hearing as an act of political persecution. Who was served by destroying Oppenheimer's public credibility?

**7.** Use **"nuclear deterrence"** to discuss Oppenheimer's scepticism about whether deterrence was stable. Has history supported or refuted his scepticism?

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** The text says Oppenheimer did not oppose the bombing in 1945 but spent the following decade opposing the hydrogen bomb. What changed? Was he consistent or contradictory?

**2.** Oppenheimer's security clearance was revoked partly because of pre-war communist associations. At what point do prior political associations become relevant to current security assessment?

**3.** The Trinity test produced the first nuclear detonation. Oppenheimer quoted the Bhagavad Gita: "I am become Death, the destroyer of worlds." What does this quote reveal about his state of mind?

**4.** The hydrogen bomb Oppenheimer opposed was eventually built by Edward Teller. Teller's testimony contributed to Oppenheimer's downfall. How do we evaluate the personal relationship between these two men in light of subsequent history?

**5.** Should scientists be held morally responsible for how political leaders use their technological innovations?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** Hiroshima and Nagasaki are cities with which Korea has a complex relationship — Korean forced labourers were present in both cities and died in the bombings. How does this history shape the Korean perspective on the atomic bombings?

**2.** The debate about whether to use the bomb — the "was it necessary?" question — continues to divide historians. What factors do you find most significant in evaluating this question?

**3.** Oppenheimer's rehabilitation by the Biden administration in 2022 — formally vacating the 1954 finding — came 55 years after his death. What does this very late formal justice accomplish?

**4.** The nuclear weapons Oppenheimer created now number in the tens of thousands, held by nine nations. How do we evaluate the long-term consequences of the Manhattan Project?

**5.** AI researchers today face a version of Oppenheimer's dilemma: they are developing technologies whose consequences they cannot fully predict. What obligations do they have, and what lessons from Oppenheimer's experience are relevant?
