### Example Sentences

**1. isolationism**
> American isolationism in 1940 was not based on indifference to European suffering — polls showed widespread sympathy for Britain — but on the deeply held conviction that US involvement in European wars was inherently against American interest.

**2. Lend-Lease**
> Lend-Lease was Roosevelt's solution to a legal and political problem: neutrality law prohibited selling weapons to belligerents, so he proposed lending or leasing them instead — in the analogy he used, lending a garden hose to a neighbour whose house is on fire.

**3. belligerent**
> By providing weapons, intelligence, and logistical support to Britain while Germany's U-boats were sinking Allied ships, the United States was behaving as a de facto belligerent in all but the formal legal sense.

**4. arsenal**
> Roosevelt's phrase "arsenal of democracy" captured his strategic vision precisely: the United States would contribute its industrial production to the defeat of fascism without yet committing its soldiers — using economic power as a substitute for military engagement.

**5. embargo**
> The American oil embargo on Japan in August 1941 was the most consequential trade sanction in twentieth-century history: it forced Japan's military leadership to choose between abandoning its China ambitions or going to war against the United States.

**6. proxy**
> The Lend-Lease programme made Britain and the Soviet Union, in effect, proxies for American strategic interests — fighting Germany on behalf of the United States' security while Americans debated whether to enter the war directly.

**7. strategic interest**
> Roosevelt argued that Britain's survival was a direct American strategic interest — that a German-dominated Europe would be an existential threat to the United States regardless of the Atlantic Ocean's width.

### Arming the World from Afar
#### Lend-Lease, the Arsenal of Democracy, and American Strategy Before Pearl Harbor

In June 1940, France fell to Germany in six weeks. Britain stood alone, its army evacuated from Dunkirk with its equipment lost. Roosevelt looked at this situation and drew a conclusion that most of his public had not yet reached: Britain's survival was an American vital interest. A German-controlled Europe, commanding the resources of the continent and the French and British navies, would represent a strategic threat that the Atlantic Ocean could not neutralize.

The problem was political. The American public, by large majorities, opposed entering the war. Congress had passed Neutrality Acts explicitly prohibiting aid to belligerents. Roosevelt was constrained by both public opinion and law in what he could do. He worked around the constraints with characteristic creativity.

The destroyers-for-bases agreement of September 1940 transferred fifty surplus American destroyers to Britain in exchange for rights to British naval bases — a deal structured to avoid the letter of the neutrality law. The Lend-Lease Act of March 1941 authorized the president to lend or lease war materials to any nation whose defence the president deemed vital to American security. Over the course of the war, the United States would provide approximately fifty billion dollars in assistance under Lend-Lease — including to the Soviet Union after Germany invaded it in June 1941.

Roosevelt called this the policy of the "Arsenal of Democracy" — the United States would provide the weapons while others provided the soldiers. It was a coherent strategic vision that also had the political advantage of keeping American boys out of the fighting. But it had a logical endpoint: you cannot arm one side of a war while remaining genuinely neutral. By the autumn of 1941, the US Navy was effectively engaged in an undeclared naval war with Germany in the Atlantic.

The Japanese attack on Pearl Harbor ended the debate. But it is worth noting that Roosevelt had already committed the United States to the Allied cause before December 7 — Pearl Harbor simply made the commitment explicit.

## ✏️ Part 3 — Vocabulary in Use

**1.** Use **"isolationism"** to discuss whether the desire to avoid foreign wars is ever a reasonable basis for national policy or always a failure of responsibility.

**2.** Use **"Lend-Lease"** to discuss the ethics of providing weapons to one side in a conflict. Does the arms supplier share moral responsibility for how those weapons are used?

**3.** Use **"belligerent"** to discuss the distinction between formal and de facto participation in a conflict. At what point does support for one side make you a participant?

**4.** Use **"arsenal"** to discuss how economic and industrial power can be deployed as a strategic tool without direct military engagement.

**5.** Use **"embargo"** to discuss the strategic risks of imposing economic sanctions on a country that considers its vital interests at stake.

**6.** Use **"proxy"** to discuss the strategy of using allied nations to advance strategic interests while avoiding direct military engagement.

**7.** Use **"strategic interest"** to argue whether a nation is ever morally obligated to act when its interests do not require it.

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** Roosevelt concluded that Britain's survival was an American vital interest, but most of the public had not reached that conclusion. Should foreign policy leaders act on their strategic judgements even when ahead of public opinion?

**2.** The text says "you cannot arm one side of a war while remaining genuinely neutral." Is there a morally coherent position between full neutrality and full belligerence?

**3.** Roosevelt committed the United States to the Allied cause before Pearl Harbor through Lend-Lease and naval deployments. Was this democratic — making a major foreign policy commitment without public or congressional endorsement?

**4.** Lend-Lease provided $50 billion in assistance to the Allies. Did this make the United States a participant in the war morally, even before formal entry?

**5.** The American oil embargo on Japan was designed to constrain Japanese expansion, but it contributed to the conditions that produced Pearl Harbor. How should policymakers account for unintended consequences of economic coercion?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** The Lend-Lease programme involved providing weapons to countries fighting for their survival. Modern arms sales serve different purposes. How should democracies decide to whom they sell weapons?

**2.** The United States provided enormous assistance to the Soviet Union under Lend-Lease, despite Stalin's regime being as repressive as Hitler's. What ethical constraints, if any, should govern alliances of convenience with authoritarian governments?

**3.** The "Arsenal of Democracy" concept suggests that economic power can substitute for military power. How true is this in contemporary great-power competition?

**4.** Roosevelt used executive creativity — the destroyers deal, Lend-Lease — to work around constraints that Congress had deliberately established. Is this admirable improvisation or an abuse of executive power?

**5.** Korea has significant domestic arms manufacturing capacity and exports weapons to various countries. What ethical frameworks should guide these decisions?
