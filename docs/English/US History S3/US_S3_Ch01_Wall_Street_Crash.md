### Example Sentences

**1. margin call**
> As prices fell on Black Thursday, margin calls cascaded across the market: investors who had borrowed to buy stocks were forced to sell immediately to cover their loans, which drove prices lower, triggering more margin calls in a self-reinforcing spiral.

**2. liquidity**
> Liquidity crises are particularly dangerous because they can transform a solvent institution into an insolvent one almost instantaneously: a bank can be fundamentally sound but still fail if too many depositors withdraw their money simultaneously.

**3. panic selling**
> The panic selling of October 24–29, 1929, was not primarily driven by new information about the economy; it was driven by fear that others were selling, which made selling rational regardless of one's fundamental view.

**4. credit contraction**
> The credit contraction that followed the crash was more economically damaging than the stock market losses themselves: as banks called in loans and refused new ones, businesses could not finance operations, investment collapsed, and unemployment soared.

**5. speculative fever**
> Speculative fever of the kind that gripped American markets in 1928–29 is characterized by a suspension of normal valuation discipline: investors stop asking "what is this worth?" and start asking only "will someone pay more for it tomorrow?"

**6. financial contagion**
> The financial contagion spread from Wall Street to European markets within weeks, then to commodity markets and real economies around the world — demonstrating that the global financial system had become far more interconnected than the regulatory frameworks of any individual nation could manage.

**7. market correction**
> What began as a market correction in early October 1929 became a crash by the end of the month; the distinction matters because a correction is self-limiting while a crash can become self-reinforcing through the mechanisms of leverage and panic.

### The Day the Music Stopped
#### Black Tuesday, the Crash, and the Beginning of the Great Depression

On October 24, 1929 — Black Thursday — the New York Stock Exchange opened to a wave of selling that intensified throughout the day. A consortium of major banks intervened to stabilize prices, and by the close, losses had been partially recovered. For a few days, it seemed like the crisis might be contained. Then, on October 29 — Black Tuesday — the selling resumed and the bottom fell out. Sixteen million shares changed hands, a record that reflected the sheer scale of the panic. The Dow Jones Industrial Average lost about twelve percent of its value in a single day.

The crash had been building for months. Stock prices had nearly tripled between 1924 and 1929, far outpacing growth in corporate earnings. Buying on margin — borrowing to purchase stocks, sometimes covering ninety percent of the purchase price — had become standard practice, amplifying both gains and losses. As long as prices rose, the leverage was profitable. When they fell, margin calls forced liquidation, which drove prices lower, which triggered more margin calls. The cascade was mechanical and inevitable once it started.

What turned a stock market crash into the Great Depression was what happened to the banking system. Banks had made loans backed by stock as collateral. As stock prices collapsed, collateral values fell below loan values. Banks that had lent too aggressively became technically insolvent. Depositors, fearing their savings were at risk, began bank runs. Banks failed — nine thousand of them between 1930 and 1933. As banks failed, credit contracted. As credit contracted, businesses could not finance payrolls or inventory. As businesses failed, unemployment rose. As unemployment rose, consumers stopped spending. The deflationary spiral had begun.

The Federal Reserve, which had been created in 1913 specifically to prevent banking panics, raised interest rates in 1931 to defend the gold standard — the opposite of what economic conditions required. The government maintained its commitment to balanced budgets even as revenues collapsed. These policy errors transformed a severe recession into a catastrophic depression.

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** The text says margin calls created a "self-reinforcing spiral." How do financial markets create feedback loops that amplify rather than correct initial disturbances?

**2.** The Federal Reserve raised interest rates during a banking crisis to defend the gold standard. What does this decision reveal about the dangers of institutional rigidity when circumstances change dramatically?

**3.** The text distinguishes between the stock market crash and the Great Depression, suggesting the latter was caused by policy errors as much as by the crash itself. How does this change our assessment of what was preventable?

**4.** Nine thousand banks failed between 1930 and 1933. What happens to an economy — and to social trust — when the institutions that hold ordinary people's savings collapse?

**5.** The text says speculative fever involves investors stopping asking "what is this worth?" How do investors maintain valuation discipline when everyone around them is ignoring it?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** Financial crises have followed a remarkably consistent pattern for centuries. Why does this knowledge not prevent them? What would it take to break the cycle?

**2.** The 2008 financial crisis had significant structural similarities to 1929 — leverage, speculative fever, contagion. Were the lessons of 1929 applied in 2008? What was different?

**3.** The gold standard constrained policy responses to the Depression in ways that worsened it. What are the modern equivalents of institutional constraints that prevent policymakers from doing what the situation requires?

**4.** Bank runs are a collective action problem: they are rational for each individual but collectively disastrous. How have modern financial systems designed around this problem?

**5.** Ordinary people who had saved responsibly lost their savings when banks failed. What obligations does a government have to protect citizens from the consequences of financial system failure?
