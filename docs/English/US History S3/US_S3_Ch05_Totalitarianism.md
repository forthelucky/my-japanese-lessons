### Example Sentences

**1. totalitarianism**
> Hannah Arendt argued that totalitarianism was something genuinely new in modern politics — not merely a dictatorship but a system that sought to eliminate the private sphere entirely, colonizing thought, family loyalty, and personal identity in the service of the state.

**2. fascism**
> Fascism emerged from the wreckage of World War I and the economic collapse of the Depression as an alternative to both liberal democracy and communism — promising national greatness, social order, and economic security through authoritarian discipline.

**3. militarism**
> Japan's militarism of the 1930s was not simply an external policy but an internal political condition: the military establishment had gained effective control over the cabinet, the budget, and foreign policy, displacing civilian governance.

**4. appeasement**
> The policy of appeasement, most notoriously expressed at Munich in 1938, reflected the genuine conviction that another world war was unthinkable — but it taught Hitler that aggression had no cost, producing the war it was designed to prevent.

**5. expansionism**
> Both Germany and Japan justified their expansionism in the language of vital national interests — Lebensraum in Germany, the Greater East Asia Co-Prosperity Sphere in Japan — but the reality was conquest, exploitation, and atrocity.

**6. propaganda**
> The Depression had a particular relationship to fascist propaganda: it discredited liberal democratic institutions in the eyes of millions who had voted for them in better times, making them receptive to authoritarian alternatives that promised order and prosperity.

**7. neutrality**
> American neutrality in the late 1930s reflected genuine public reluctance to repeat the experience of World War I, but it also reflected a failure of imagination about what fascism actually meant for the countries that lived under it.

### Democracy's Darkest Hour
#### Fascism, Militarism, and the Global Crisis of the 1930s

From an American perspective, the Depression was primarily a domestic crisis. From a global perspective, it was the trigger for something far more dangerous: the rise of fascism in Europe and militarism in Asia, the simultaneous collapse of the liberal international order that World War I had supposedly secured.

The mechanism was straightforward. The Depression destroyed the economic credibility of moderate, democratic governments. In Germany, the Weimar Republic — already psychologically damaged by hyperinflation and the Versailles settlement — could not survive mass unemployment and political deadlock. Hitler's Nazi Party, which had received less than three percent of the vote in 1928, received nearly thirty-eight percent in 1932 as unemployment reached six million. In Italy, Mussolini had already established his fascist dictatorship in the more favourable conditions of the 1920s. In Japan, economic crisis radicalized the military establishment, which increasingly operated independently of civilian control.

What all three regimes shared was the conviction that liberal democracy was decadent — incapable of defending the national interest — and that national greatness required the concentration of power, the elimination of dissent, and aggressive territorial expansion. The Depression had created the mass constituency for these ideas; the political failures of democratic governments had made those ideas credible.

The American response was complicated by its own domestic priorities. Roosevelt's attention was consumed by the New Deal. Public opinion was strongly isolationist; memories of World War I casualties remained vivid. Congress passed a series of Neutrality Acts designed to prevent American involvement in foreign wars. While Germany reoccupied the Rhineland, swallowed Austria and Czechoslovakia, and invaded Poland, most Americans watched from a distance — and hoped the Atlantic Ocean was wide enough.

It was not.

## ✏️ Part 3 — Vocabulary in Use

**1.** Use **"totalitarianism"** to discuss what distinguishes a totalitarian regime from an ordinary dictatorship.

**2.** Use **"fascism"** to explain the specific economic and political conditions in which fascism tends to emerge. Are those conditions present anywhere in the world today?

**3.** Use **"militarism"** to discuss the risks that arise when military values and interests dominate a nation's civilian political life.

**4.** Use **"appeasement"** to argue when accommodation of a rival's demands is a reasonable diplomatic strategy and when it is a fatal mistake.

**5.** Use **"expansionism"** to discuss what drives territorial aggression. Is it primarily economic, ideological, security-driven, or a combination?

**6.** Use **"propaganda"** to discuss how authoritarian regimes use media to create support for policies that harm their citizens' interests.

**7.** Use **"neutrality"** to discuss whether a powerful nation can remain genuinely neutral when aggressive powers are threatening others.

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** The text describes a "mechanism" by which economic failure produces political radicalization. Is this a general law of politics, or a specific historical contingency?

**2.** The text says public opinion in America was "strongly isolationist" while fascism rose in Europe. What moral responsibilities, if any, did the United States have toward the victims of fascist aggression before Pearl Harbor?

**3.** The Depression discredited moderate democratic governments in the eyes of voters who had supported them. Is democratic legitimacy always contingent on economic performance?

**4.** The Neutrality Acts were designed to prevent American involvement in foreign wars. Was this a wise policy, a failure of moral courage, or both?

**5.** The text ends with "it was not [wide enough]" — a reference to the American assumption that the Atlantic provided security. What illusion does this phrase puncture?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** Fascism emerged from specific historical conditions — World War I's aftermath, the Depression, institutional failures. Could those conditions recur in sufficiently similar form to produce comparable political movements today?

**2.** The Depression delegitimized liberal democracy as an economic manager in the eyes of millions of voters. How should democracies build the institutional resilience to survive major economic failures?

**3.** American isolationism in the 1930s was supported by the public and opposed by most foreign policy elites. Who was right? Does popular opinion or expert judgment deserve more weight in foreign policy?

**4.** Korea was under Japanese colonial occupation during this period — a direct object of the militarism and expansionism described in this chapter. How does the experience of being occupied by an expansionist power shape a nation's subsequent attitude toward international security arrangements?

**5.** The 1930s are sometimes invoked in contemporary political debates to warn against appeasement of aggressive powers. Is this historical analogy applied appropriately, or is it overused?
