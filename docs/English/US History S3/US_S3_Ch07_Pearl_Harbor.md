### Example Sentences

**1. surprise attack**
> The surprise attack on Pearl Harbor was carefully planned by Admiral Yamamoto to neutralize the American Pacific Fleet before the US could respond to Japanese expansion in Southeast Asia; its execution was brilliant, but its strategic consequences were catastrophic for Japan.

**2. declaration of war**
> Roosevelt's declaration of war speech — "a date which will live in infamy" — was eight minutes long and carefully constructed: it built the case for war through the enumeration of Japanese attacks across the Pacific, ending with a request to Congress that was also a foregone conclusion.

**3. two-front war**
> American planners had war-gamed a two-front war — against both Germany and Japan — and had concluded that Germany was the primary threat; Pearl Harbor forced the US into exactly that scenario, requiring simultaneous engagement in the Atlantic and Pacific.

**4. Pacific theatre**
> The Pacific theatre of World War II was unlike any other in military history: vast distances between strategic points, the centrality of naval and air power, and the extreme difficulty of supplying forces operating thousands of miles from any industrial base.

**5. public outrage**
> The public outrage generated by Pearl Harbor accomplished something that years of Roosevelt's warnings about fascism had failed to do: it unified American public opinion behind the war with a speed and completeness that no democratic government could otherwise have achieved.

**6. internment**
> Executive Order 9066, signed by Roosevelt in February 1942, authorized the internment of approximately 120,000 Japanese Americans — two-thirds of them US citizens — in a decision the Supreme Court later acknowledged was driven by racial prejudice rather than military necessity.

**7. strategic blunder**
> Germany's declaration of war on the United States four days after Pearl Harbor — obligatory under the Tripartite Pact, though Germany was not required to if Japan was the aggressor — was arguably Hitler's greatest strategic blunder, converting a bilateral Pacific war into the global conflict that ensured Germany's defeat.

### The Day That Unified a Nation
#### Pearl Harbor, Outrage, and the Transformation of American Purpose

At 7:55 a.m. on December 7, 1941, 353 Japanese aircraft attacked the United States naval base at Pearl Harbor, Hawaii. In under two hours, they sank or damaged eight battleships, destroyed nearly two hundred aircraft, and killed 2,403 Americans. The Pacific Fleet was crippled. The message was clear: Japan was willing to go to war with the most powerful industrial nation on earth rather than abandon its expansion in Asia.

The strategic calculation behind the attack was, as its architect Admiral Yamamoto understood, desperate. Japan needed to neutralize American naval power long enough to seize the oil fields of the Dutch East Indies and the rubber of Malaya — to secure the resources it had been denied by the American embargo. The eighteen months of strategic freedom Yamamoto hoped to purchase would, he assumed, eventually lead to a negotiated settlement on terms favourable to Japan. What he could not afford was a long war.

The attack accomplished its immediate tactical objectives. What it catastrophically miscalculated was the political effect. Before December 7, American public opinion had been sharply divided over intervention; after, it was virtually unanimous. The day Roosevelt described as "a date which will live in infamy" ended the debate that had consumed American political life for two years. Congress declared war the next day with a single dissenting vote.

Four days later, Hitler declared war on the United States in fulfilment of his Tripartite Pact commitment. This was the decision that, more than any other, sealed Germany's fate. The United States was now at war with both Japan and Germany — the precisely the two-front scenario American planners had prepared for. Germany was the primary threat; defeating it first, then turning to Japan, was the strategic priority. The road to Berlin went through North Africa, Italy, and Normandy.

## ✏️ Part 3 — Vocabulary in Use

**1.** Use **"surprise attack"** to discuss the strategic logic behind attacking without warning. What are the short-term advantages and long-term risks?

**2.** Use **"declaration of war"** to discuss what the formal declaration of war signifies politically and legally. Is it still meaningful in modern conflicts?

**3.** Use **"two-front war"** to explain why fighting simultaneously in multiple theatres is considered a cardinal strategic error.

**4.** Use **"Pacific theatre"** to discuss how geography shaped the nature of the fighting in the Pacific War.

**5.** Use **"public outrage"** to discuss how a single dramatic event can transform public opinion on a complex political question within hours.

**6.** Use **"internment"** to discuss the conditions under which a democratic government might justify detaining its own citizens without trial.

**7.** Use **"strategic blunder"** to evaluate Germany's declaration of war on the United States. Was it avoidable, or was it an inevitable consequence of the Tripartite Pact?

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** Yamamoto hoped to purchase eighteen months of strategic freedom to negotiate a settlement. What does this reveal about the gap between military calculation and political reality?

**2.** Pearl Harbor unified American opinion "virtually unanimously." Is this kind of national unity in response to external attack always genuine, or is it sometimes a manufactured emotional response that prevents rational analysis?

**3.** The internment of Japanese Americans — two-thirds of them citizens — was driven by racial prejudice rather than military necessity. How do democratic governments justify abandoning constitutional principles during wartime?

**4.** Hitler declared war on the United States despite having no obligation to do so if Japan was the aggressor. What does this tell us about his strategic thinking?

**5.** The "Germany first" strategy — treating Germany as the primary threat despite Japan's attack — was agreed upon before Pearl Harbor. How does pre-established strategy constrain responses to unexpected events?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** Pearl Harbor is described as the event that unified America. Can external threats produce this kind of political unity in modern democratic societies? What has changed?

**2.** The internment of Japanese Americans is now recognized as a profound violation of constitutional rights. How should democracies prevent wartime fear from producing similar violations in the future?

**3.** Japan attacked the United States partly because of the oil embargo. How does economic coercion create risks of military escalation that its architects do not intend?

**4.** Korea was a Japanese colony when Pearl Harbor occurred and the Pacific War began. How did the outcome of that war — Japan's defeat and American occupation of Korea — shape the trajectory of Korean history?

**5.** The Pearl Harbor attack is often cited in discussions of intelligence failure — the signs were there, but were not correctly interpreted. What lessons does this case offer for modern intelligence analysis?
