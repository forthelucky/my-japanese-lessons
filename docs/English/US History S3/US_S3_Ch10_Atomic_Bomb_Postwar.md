### Example Sentences

**1. Manhattan Project**
> The Manhattan Project was a scientific and industrial undertaking without historical precedent — employing one hundred and thirty thousand people at thirty sites across the country and costing two billion dollars — yet it remained substantially secret from the American public until August 6, 1945.

**2. nuclear deterrence**
> Nuclear deterrence is based on a logical paradox: the weapons must never be used, yet they must be credibly capable of being used; maintaining this combination of resolve and restraint is one of the most demanding challenges in statecraft.

**3. Bretton Woods**
> The Bretton Woods conference of July 1944 established the international monetary architecture that would define the post-war economy: the US dollar pegged to gold, all other currencies pegged to the dollar, and new institutions — the IMF and World Bank — to manage international finance.

**4. hegemony**
> American hegemony after 1945 was unlike anything in previous international experience: it was based not just on military power but on economic output, technological leadership, and the provision of global public goods — security, trade, and currency stability.

**5. postwar order**
> The postwar order created at Yalta, Bretton Woods, and San Francisco was explicitly designed by its American architects to prevent a repetition of the catastrophic failures of the interwar period — the rise of fascism, the collapse of trade, and the failure of collective security.

**6. war crime**
> Whether the atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki constituted war crimes under the laws of war then in force — given their indiscriminate effects on civilians — remains one of the most debated questions in twentieth-century history.

**7. Marshall Plan**
> The Marshall Plan provided approximately thirteen billion dollars in economic assistance to rebuild Western European economies after the war — motivated partly by genuine humanitarian concern and partly by the strategic calculation that prosperity was the best defence against communism.

### The Weapon That Changed Everything
#### Hiroshima, the Postwar Order, and American Hegemony

By the summer of 1945, Japan was militarily defeated in every meaningful sense. Its navy was effectively gone. Its air force could barely contest American bombing raids. Its cities were being firebombed systematically, with March 9–10 raids on Tokyo killing an estimated one hundred thousand people in a single night. Yet Japan had not surrendered, and American military planners estimated that an invasion of the Japanese home islands would cost between two hundred fifty thousand and one million American casualties, and multiple millions of Japanese.

The Manhattan Project had been building toward this moment for three years. On August 6, 1945, a B-29 named Enola Gay dropped an atomic bomb on Hiroshima. The explosion killed approximately eighty thousand people instantly and destroyed most of the city. Three days later, a second bomb fell on Nagasaki. Between the two bombings, the Soviet Union declared war on Japan and launched a massive invasion of Manchuria — the last of Japan's strategic reserves.

On August 15, Emperor Hirohito addressed the Japanese people by radio for the first time. Japan would accept the terms of the Potsdam Declaration. The formal surrender followed on September 2, 1945.

The war's end revealed a transformed world. The United States emerged from it as the world's dominant power in every dimension: its economy was producing nearly half of global industrial output; it possessed a nuclear monopoly; and its military was deployed across the globe. American architects of the postwar order — working at Bretton Woods, San Francisco, and countless bilateral negotiations — deliberately designed a new international system to avoid the mistakes of 1919: a more inclusive United Nations, the International Monetary Fund and World Bank, the Marshall Plan for European reconstruction. This order, imperfect and contested, has nevertheless maintained great-power peace for eighty years.

## ✏️ Part 3 — Vocabulary in Use

**1.** Use **"Manhattan Project"** to discuss the ethical responsibilities of scientists who develop technologies with catastrophic applications.

**2.** Use **"nuclear deterrence"** to argue whether the threat of mutual annihilation is a stable basis for long-term international peace.

**3.** Use **"Bretton Woods"** to explain how the dollar's reserve currency status benefits the United States and what obligations that status creates.

**4.** Use **"hegemony"** to discuss what makes American post-war hegemony different from previous empires, and what its limits are.

**5.** Use **"postwar order"** to discuss how the architects of 1945 tried to learn from the failures of 1919. Were they successful?

**6.** Use **"war crime"** to discuss the moral framework for evaluating attacks that kill large numbers of civilians in pursuit of legitimate military objectives.

**7.** Use **"Marshall Plan"** to discuss whether economic assistance to former enemies is a wise policy. What motivates it — generosity or self-interest?

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** The text presents the case for the atomic bombs: an invasion would have cost more lives. Does this utilitarian calculation adequately address the moral questions raised by targeting civilians?

**2.** Japan was "militarily defeated" but had not surrendered. What explains this gap, and does it change the moral calculus of the atomic bombings?

**3.** The Soviet entry into the Pacific War between the two bombings may have been as decisive as the bombs themselves. How does this complicate the standard narrative about why Japan surrendered?

**4.** The postwar order was explicitly designed to avoid the failures of 1919. What specific lessons were the architects applying, and were they successful?

**5.** The text says the postwar order has "maintained great-power peace for eighty years." Is nuclear deterrence the cause of this peace, or are other factors more important?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** Hiroshima and Nagasaki remain the only cases of nuclear weapons use in war. How should this historical singularity affect our thinking about nuclear policy today?

**2.** The dollar's reserve currency status — established at Bretton Woods — gives the United States extraordinary financial power. Is this a reasonable reward for the role the US played in building the postwar order?

**3.** The Marshall Plan rebuilt Western Europe partly to prevent the spread of communism. Does the mixture of strategic and humanitarian motives make it less admirable, or is this simply how foreign policy works?

**4.** Korea's division at the 38th parallel — a direct consequence of the postwar settlement — shaped the trajectory of Korean history more than any other single decision. How do the decisions made in the immediate aftermath of a major war create conditions that last for generations?

**5.** The postwar order — UN, IMF, World Bank, dollar system — is under stress today. What would a new international order need to look like to maintain the peace and prosperity of the past eighty years in the changed circumstances of the twenty-first century?
