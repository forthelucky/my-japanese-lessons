### Example Sentences

**1. drought**
> The drought that struck the southern Great Plains in the early 1930s was the worst in recorded American history — but the disaster it produced was not purely natural; decades of unregulated farming had left the soil vulnerable in ways that any serious drought would expose.

**2. erosion**
> The deep-rooted prairie grasses that had anchored the Great Plains soil for millennia had been ploughed under to plant wheat; without those roots, the topsoil was simply waiting for a dry year and a strong wind to begin its journey eastward.

**3. migration**
> The migration of Dust Bowl refugees to California — estimated at three hundred thousand people between 1935 and 1940 — was the largest internal displacement in American history to that point, and California's reception was often hostile.

**4. ecological**
> The Dust Bowl was one of the first ecological catastrophes in American history to receive significant public attention — it demonstrated that the exploitation of natural resources without regard to their regenerative limits had consequences that no individual farmer could absorb alone.

**5. displacement**
> The displacement of Dust Bowl families was doubly traumatic: they were not merely losing their income but their identity, their community, and their relationship to a specific piece of land that defined who they were.

**6. sharecropper**
> Sharecroppers in the south and tenant farmers on the plains were particularly vulnerable to the combination of drought and depression because they had no accumulated assets to fall back on — when the crops failed, there was nothing.

**7. tenancy**
> Agricultural tenancy — farming land you do not own — left millions of Depression-era farmers with no legal stake in the land they worked and no protection against landlords who chose to mechanize or simply evict.

### When the Land Turned Against Its People
#### The Dust Bowl, Environmental Catastrophe, and the Great Migration West

The Great Plains of the American midwest had once been a sea of tall-grass prairie, home to enormous buffalo herds and the Plains Indian nations whose civilization depended on them. By the early twentieth century, the buffalo were gone, the indigenous nations had been confined to reservations, and the prairie had been ploughed under to plant wheat. The land had been treated as an inexhaustible resource, and no one had asked what would happen if the rains failed.

In the early 1930s, the rains failed.

The drought that struck the southern Great Plains beginning in 1930 was severe, but the catastrophe it produced was not natural — it was the result of a specific kind of land use. The deep-rooted native grasses that had anchored the topsoil through thousands of years of drought had been ploughed under. Without those roots, the soil was simply waiting for a dry year and a wind to become dust. When the wind came, it came with extraordinary force: "black blizzards" — walls of topsoil thousands of feet high — darkened the sky from Texas to Nebraska and sometimes reached the eastern seaboard. On April 14, 1935 — "Black Sunday" — a dust storm so massive that witnesses thought it was the end of the world swept across the plains.

The human consequences were devastating. Farms were buried under drifting soil. Crops failed year after year. Families who had invested everything in land found that the land had become worthless. An estimated three hundred thousand people left the southern plains between 1935 and 1940, many loading their possessions onto ageing vehicles and driving west toward California, which they had heard offered agricultural work. They were met with hostility. California posted armed men at its borders to turn back the migrants, whom they called "Okies" — a term of contempt.

John Steinbeck's novel The Grapes of Wrath documented this migration with such force that it changed the political conversation about rural poverty. But it also reflected a deeper reality: the Dust Bowl had been caused by treating ecological systems as though they had no limits. It was a warning that would not be heeded for generations.

## ✏️ Part 3 — Vocabulary in Use

**1.** Use **"drought"** to discuss the relationship between natural phenomena and human vulnerability. When does a natural event become a human disaster?

**2.** Use **"erosion"** (literally or metaphorically) to describe a process of gradual deterioration that accelerates when a protective layer is removed.

**3.** Use **"migration"** to discuss what drives internal migration within a country. How does it differ from international migration?

**4.** Use **"ecological"** to discuss the principle that economic activities must operate within the limits of natural systems. How well is this principle respected today?

**5.** Use **"displacement"** to describe the non-economic dimensions of losing one's home — the loss of identity, community, and belonging.

**6.** Use **"sharecropper"** to discuss how tenure insecurity makes people particularly vulnerable to economic shocks.

**7.** Use **"tenancy"** to argue whether ownership of the land one farms makes a material difference to how one cares for it.

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** The text says the Dust Bowl was not a natural disaster but the result of specific land-use practices. At what point do human activities that transform natural systems become unacceptable risks?

**2.** California posted armed men at its borders to keep out Dust Bowl migrants. What does this response reveal about how communities respond to mass influxes of economically distressed people?

**3.** The Dust Bowl destroyed livelihoods, but it also destroyed identities — the connection of families to specific places and ways of life. How does this kind of loss differ from purely economic loss?

**4.** Steinbeck's novel changed political conversations about rural poverty. What role can literature play in generating political attention to suffering that statistics fail to convey?

**5.** The text says the Dust Bowl's warning "would not be heeded for generations." What makes it so difficult for societies to act on clear environmental warnings?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** The Dust Bowl was caused by agricultural practices that degraded the land's capacity to sustain itself. How do you see similar dynamics in contemporary industrial agriculture or resource extraction?

**2.** The migrants from the Dust Bowl faced hostility when they arrived in California. How does economic distress change the way established communities respond to newcomers?

**3.** Korea has experienced its own environmental challenges — air quality from industrial development, soil degradation. How do these compare to the Dust Bowl experience?

**4.** The federal government eventually created conservation programmes — the Soil Conservation Service — in response to the Dust Bowl. What does this case study suggest about when government intervention in environmental management is necessary?

**5.** Climate change is producing droughts, floods, and extreme weather events that are displacing populations today. What lessons from the Dust Bowl era are most relevant to managing these contemporary challenges?
