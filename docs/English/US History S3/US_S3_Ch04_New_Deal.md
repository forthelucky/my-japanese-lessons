### Example Sentences

**1. fiscal stimulus**
> The New Deal's fiscal stimulus was significant by the standards of the time but insufficient to fully offset the Depression's deflationary force; it would take the massive spending of World War II to fully restore employment and output.

**2. public works**
> The Works Progress Administration funded public works on an extraordinary scale — building schools, bridges, airports, and post offices — while simultaneously employing artists, writers, and musicians, an investment in cultural production that the WPA's critics considered frivolous.

**3. social safety net**
> The Social Security Act of 1935 established the basic architecture of the American social safety net — old age pensions, unemployment insurance, and aid to dependent children — transforming the relationship between citizens and the federal government permanently.

**4. bank deposit insurance**
> The Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation, established in 1933, guaranteed deposits up to a specified amount — essentially ending bank runs overnight by removing the incentive to withdraw in a panic, since insured deposits were safe regardless of the bank's health.

**5. regulatory agency**
> The Securities and Exchange Commission, established in 1934, was designed to ensure that the information asymmetry and fraud that had fuelled the 1920s stock market bubble could not recur on the same scale.

**6. fireside chat**
> Roosevelt's fireside chats were a masterpiece of political communication: in a calm, reassuring voice, he explained his policies to the American public as though talking to neighbours, restoring confidence not through false promises but through honest explanation.

**7. Keynesian**
> The New Deal's implicit Keynesian logic — that government spending during a recession substitutes for collapsed private demand — was not yet a fully articulated theory in 1933; Keynes published his General Theory in 1936, by which time Roosevelt was already applying its principles.

### The Government as Last Resort
#### FDR, the New Deal, and the Redefinition of Government's Role

Franklin Roosevelt took office on March 4, 1933, with a third of the workforce unemployed, the banking system in collapse, and a public that had lost confidence in both markets and government. His inaugural address acknowledged the depth of the crisis but redirected the public's anger productively: "the only thing we have to fear is fear itself." He had no comprehensive programme. He had a disposition — to act, to experiment, to try things — and a political skill set that was unprecedented in American history.

The first hundred days of Roosevelt's presidency produced a legislative transformation that no subsequent administration has matched for speed or scale. The Emergency Banking Act stabilized the banking system in days. Federal deposit insurance, established through the Glass-Steagall Act, ended bank runs by guaranteeing that ordinary depositors' savings were safe regardless of the bank's condition. The Agricultural Adjustment Administration paid farmers to reduce production in order to raise prices. The Civilian Conservation Corps put young unemployed men to work in national forests and parks. The Public Works Administration funded construction of infrastructure on an enormous scale.

The New Deal's central innovation was institutional rather than economic. It established that the federal government had an affirmative responsibility for citizens' economic welfare — that allowing mass unemployment and starvation was not an acceptable exercise of laissez-faire principle but a failure of governance. Social Security, enacted in 1935, created the basic architecture of the American welfare state. The Securities and Exchange Commission and the Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation established permanent regulatory frameworks for financial markets.

Economically, the New Deal did not end the Depression — unemployment remained above ten percent until rearmament and war spending finally restored full employment in 1940–41. But it prevented the complete collapse of the financial system, restored public confidence, and transformed the social contract between citizens and the state. Whether the specific programmes were optimally designed mattered less than the demonstration that democratic government could respond decisively to a crisis of capitalism.

## ✏️ Part 3 — Vocabulary in Use

**1.** Use **"fiscal stimulus"** to argue for or against the proposition that government spending during a recession is necessary to prevent a self-reinforcing downward spiral.

**2.** Use **"public works"** to discuss what kinds of investment produce the greatest long-term economic return. Should public works prioritize employment creation or economic efficiency?

**3.** Use **"social safety net"** to discuss what level of support a government owes citizens who cannot support themselves. Are there limits?

**4.** Use **"bank deposit insurance"** to explain how a simple institutional guarantee can prevent the collective action problem of bank runs.

**5.** Use **"regulatory agency"** to discuss the conditions under which government regulation of an industry serves the public interest rather than the industry's interest.

**6.** Use **"fireside chat"** to discuss what effective political communication during a crisis requires. What qualities make a leader's communication reassuring rather than manipulative?

**7.** Use **"Keynesian"** to describe the ongoing debate between those who believe government should expand spending during recessions and those who believe fiscal discipline should be maintained.

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** Roosevelt's approach was to "try things." Is experimental governance — implementing programmes without complete assurance of their effectiveness — appropriate in a crisis?

**2.** The New Deal did not end the Depression but prevented its complete collapse and restored confidence. Is preventing catastrophe, even without restoring prosperity, a sufficient measure of policy success?

**3.** The text says the New Deal's central innovation was establishing that government had "an affirmative responsibility for citizens' economic welfare." Was this a change in principle or a change in the scale of existing obligations?

**4.** Bank deposit insurance ended bank runs essentially overnight. What does this tell us about the power of credible government guarantees to change economic behaviour?

**5.** The New Deal's programmes were controversial then and remain so: critics argue they prolonged the Depression by discouraging private investment; defenders argue they prevented complete collapse. How should we evaluate policies whose effects are genuinely uncertain?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** The New Deal established the principle that the federal government bears responsibility for citizens' economic security. Has this principle held? What are its limits?

**2.** Roosevelt's communication style — calm, explanatory, honest about difficulty — is often contrasted with modern political communication. What has changed, and what has been lost?

**3.** Korea's economic development model involved heavy government direction of investment and industry. How does this compare to the New Deal approach? What are the similarities and differences in philosophy?

**4.** The New Deal's legacy is contested: it has been called America's salvation and its original sin of big government. How do you evaluate it?

**5.** The New Deal created institutions — the SEC, FDIC, Social Security — that have endured for ninety years. What makes some government programmes enduring and others temporary?
