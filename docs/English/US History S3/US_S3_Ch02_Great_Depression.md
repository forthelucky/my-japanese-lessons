### Example Sentences

**1. deflation**
> Deflation during the Depression created a particularly vicious trap: falling prices made debts more burdensome in real terms, which caused more defaults, which caused more bank failures, which caused more credit contraction, which caused further deflation.

**2. unemployment**
> At the peak of the Depression in 1933, unemployment reached approximately twenty-five percent — one in four American workers had no job — but this figure understated the true distress, as many of the employed were working drastically reduced hours for drastically reduced pay.

**3. bank run**
> A bank run is a self-fulfilling prophecy: it can destroy a solvent bank because even a bank with fundamentally sound assets cannot survive if all its depositors withdraw simultaneously and liquid reserves are exhausted.

**4. breadline**
> Photographs of breadlines — long queues of men waiting for free soup or bread — became the defining images of the Depression, conveying the scale of human suffering in a way that economic statistics could not.

**5. Hooverville**
> Hoovervilles appeared on the outskirts of every major American city — communities of men, women, and children living in makeshift shelters of scavenged wood and cardboard, their name a political indictment of the president who, they believed, had done too little.

**6. austerity**
> Hoover's commitment to fiscal austerity — maintaining balanced budgets by cutting spending as revenues fell — was orthodox economic thinking at the time but proved catastrophic, removing purchasing power from an economy already in freefall.

**7. laissez-faire**
> Hoover was not the laissez-faire president of his political caricature — he actually expanded federal intervention beyond any previous peacetime president — but he believed the fundamental corrective mechanisms of the market would eventually work if given time.

### When the Economy Stopped
#### The Great Depression, Bank Failures, and the Limits of Presidential Orthodoxy

By 1933, the American economy had contracted by roughly a third from its 1929 peak. Twenty-five percent of the workforce was unemployed. Nine thousand banks had failed, wiping out the savings of millions of depositors who had done nothing more imprudent than trust the financial system. Breadlines stretched around city blocks. Families evicted from homes and farms built makeshift camps — Hoovervilles — on the edges of cities. It was the worst economic catastrophe in American history, and the policies being applied to address it were making it worse.

Herbert Hoover, president when the crash occurred, was not an indifferent man. He had organized relief efforts during and after World War I with remarkable effectiveness. But his approach to the Depression was shaped by convictions that the situation had made obsolete. He believed that the market would self-correct if given time; that government spending financed by borrowing would crowd out private investment; and that maintaining the gold standard and balanced budgets were non-negotiable commitments regardless of economic conditions.

The deflation spiral was the Depression's most insidious mechanism. Falling prices sounded beneficial, but they made debts more burdensome in real terms — a farmer who had borrowed to buy equipment at 1920s prices faced those same debts with 1930s farm prices that were sixty or seventy percent lower. More defaults meant more bank failures. More bank failures meant less credit. Less credit meant less investment. Less investment meant more unemployment. More unemployment meant less consumption. Less consumption meant more falling prices.

By 1932, with the election approaching, Hoover had lost the country politically as well as economically. His name had become a synonym for failure — Hoovervilles, Hoover blankets (newspapers used for warmth), Hoover hogs (jackrabbits caught for food). His Democratic opponent, Franklin Roosevelt, promised simply that he would try things. It was enough.

## ✏️ Part 3 — Vocabulary in Use

**1.** Use **"deflation"** to explain the deflationary spiral. Why is falling prices not simply the mirror image of rising prices?

**2.** Use **"unemployment"** to discuss what mass unemployment does to individuals, families, and communities beyond the economic dimension.

**3.** Use **"bank run"** to explain the role of confidence in the functioning of financial institutions. What restores confidence once it is lost?

**4.** Use **"breadline"** to discuss what it communicates when suffering of this scale becomes visible in public spaces.

**5.** Use **"Hooverville"** to discuss how economic catastrophe produces political consequences — and whether these consequences are proportionate.

**6.** Use **"austerity"** to argue for or against the proposition that cutting government spending during a recession makes the recession worse.

**7.** Use **"laissez-faire"** to discuss the conditions under which minimal government intervention in the economy is appropriate and the conditions under which it is not.

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** Hoover was not a do-nothing president, yet his name became a synonym for failure. What does this tell us about how political accountability operates when a president inherits, rather than creates, a catastrophe?

**2.** The deflationary spiral made rational individual decisions — paying off debt, cutting spending — collectively catastrophic. What does this suggest about the limitations of market self-correction in a severe crisis?

**3.** The gold standard constrained policy responses throughout the Depression. Should monetary commitments be treated as inviolable regardless of consequences, or adjusted when circumstances change dramatically?

**4.** The text describes Depression-era poverty through photographs of breadlines and Hoovervilles. How does visible, public suffering affect the politics of economic crises?

**5.** Roosevelt promised to "try things." Why was the willingness to experiment politically appealing in 1932, and what does this tell us about what people want from leaders in a crisis?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** The Great Depression produced lasting changes in Americans' relationship to saving and spending. Do you think major economic crises produce permanent changes in individual and social psychology?

**2.** Hoover's orthodox fiscal conservatism made the Depression worse. Yet in other contexts, fiscal discipline prevents crises. How should policymakers know when orthodoxy is appropriate and when it must be abandoned?

**3.** The Depression hit different groups differently — urban workers lost jobs, rural farmers lost prices, Black Americans faced discrimination on top of economic collapse. How does existing inequality shape the distribution of economic crises?

**4.** Roosevelt's electoral victory was based more on Hoover's failure than on Roosevelt's programme. How often is democratic politics more about accountability for failure than a genuine choice between alternatives?

**5.** Japan's economy has experienced deflation for extended periods since the 1990s. How does the Japanese experience compare to the American Depression? What has Japan learned, and is it applying those lessons effectively?
