### Example Sentences

**1. delegate**
> The delegates who gathered in Philadelphia in May 1787 had been authorized only to revise the Articles of Confederation; most of them quickly agreed, however, that revision was inadequate and that an entirely new document was needed.

**2. compromise**
> Madison later wrote that the Constitutional Convention was "a miracle" — not because divine intervention had occurred, but because fifty-five men with radically different interests and visions had been willing to compromise enough to produce a workable result.

**3. proportional representation**
> Large states like Virginia argued for proportional representation in the legislature, which would give them far more political power; small states like New Jersey rejected this outright, fearing permanent marginalization.

**4. bicameral**
> The Great Compromise resolved the representation deadlock by creating a bicameral Congress: a Senate giving each state two equal votes, satisfying small states, and a House of Representatives apportioned by population, satisfying large ones.

**5. three-fifths clause**
> The three-fifths clause is often called a compromise, but it was more accurately an arrangement that benefited slaveholders — it boosted the political representation of slave states without granting any rights to enslaved people themselves.

**6. federalism**
> Federalism was not simply a pragmatic compromise between national and state power — it was a genuine philosophical position, held by many delegates, that distributed authority was itself a safeguard against tyranny.

**7. ratification**
> The ratification debates — the public argument over whether to accept the new Constitution — produced the Federalist Papers, arguably the most sophisticated analysis of republican government ever written.

### The Art of Necessary Compromise
#### Philadelphia, 1787, and the Making of the Constitution

In May 1787, fifty-five delegates gathered in Philadelphia to fix what could not, in fact, be fixed — the Articles of Confederation. Within days, they had quietly decided to start over entirely, drafting a new constitution for a new kind of government. The sessions were held in secret, and the delegates swore an oath of confidentiality that most of them kept for decades.

The convention was a gathering of extraordinary talent. James Madison had arrived with a complete draft plan and the most sophisticated understanding of constitutional theory in the room. Benjamin Franklin, at eighty-one the oldest delegate, provided wisdom and occasional wit to deflate tensions. Alexander Hamilton, Roger Sherman, James Wilson — the intellectual resources assembled in Philadelphia have rarely been matched in a single room.

But the convention's central challenge was not intellectual — it was political. Delegates represented states with incompatible interests. Large states wanted representation based on population; small states demanded equal votes. Slave states wanted their enslaved population counted for the purposes of representation; free states objected. Commercial states wanted strong federal authority over trade; agricultural states feared northern merchants would dominate.

The solution was a series of bargains that left almost no one fully satisfied. The Great Compromise created a bicameral legislature: a Senate with two members per state (satisfying small states) and a House apportioned by population (satisfying large ones). The three-fifths compromise counted each enslaved person as three-fifths of a person for representation purposes — a morally repugnant arrangement that gave slaveholders disproportionate political power for the next seventy years. The commerce compromise gave Congress authority over interstate trade while prohibiting interference with the slave trade for twenty years.

The document that emerged in September 1787 was not what any single delegate had wanted. It was what they could all live with — which, as compromises go, is often the best available outcome.

## ✏️ Part 3 — Vocabulary in Use

**1.** Use **"delegate"** to discuss what it means to represent others — what authority comes with that role, and what constraints.

**2.** Use **"compromise"** to argue either that compromise is always a virtue in political life or that some principles should not be compromised regardless of the political cost.

**3.** Use **"proportional representation"** to discuss the advantages and disadvantages of representation systems based on population size.

**4.** Use **"bicameral"** to explain the rationale for having two legislative chambers with different compositions and different powers.

**5.** Use **"three-fifths clause"** to discuss what it means when a founding compromise is built on a morally indefensible premise. How does this affect the legitimacy of the whole?

**6.** Use **"federalism"** to discuss whether dividing power between national and regional governments makes government more or less effective.

**7.** Use **"ratification"** to explain why the process of public debate and approval — not just elite agreement — matters for constitutional legitimacy.

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** The text describes the Constitution as "what they could all live with" rather than what anyone wanted. Is this kind of imperfect consensus a strength or a weakness as a foundation for government?

**2.** The three-fifths clause gave slaveholders greater political power without giving enslaved people any rights. What does this reveal about the limits of a compromise when some parties to it have no voice?

**3.** The convention was held in secret, with no public reporting. Is secret deliberation sometimes necessary for genuine compromise? What are its dangers?

**4.** Madison arrived with a complete draft, giving him extraordinary influence over the framework of discussion. How important is agenda-setting — controlling the terms of a debate — to its outcome?

**5.** The text says federalism was "a genuine philosophical position" — that distributing authority was itself a safeguard. Do you find this argument convincing?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** Constitutional moments — when a society creates or rewrites its fundamental law — are rare. What conditions make them possible? What conditions make them likely to succeed?

**2.** The Constitution was designed for a country of three million people in thirteen coastal states. How does a founding document maintain its authority and relevance as the society it governs changes beyond recognition?

**3.** The three-fifths compromise is often cited as evidence that the Constitution was, from the beginning, a pro-slavery document. Others argue it was a necessary concession to achieve union. How do you weigh these interpretations?

**4.** Madison and Hamilton were brilliant architects of the Constitution yet had very different visions of what it meant. Does it matter that a founding document's authors disagreed about its meaning?

**5.** Korea has had multiple constitutions. What are the differences between a constitution that emerges from genuine political negotiation and one that is imposed by a dominant faction?
