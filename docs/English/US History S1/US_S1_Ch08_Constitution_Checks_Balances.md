### Example Sentences

**1. separation of powers**
> The Founders' commitment to separation of powers was not simply an abstract principle — it reflected their deep conviction, drawn from history, that concentrated power inevitably became tyrannical.

**2. checks and balances**
> The system of checks and balances was designed so that ambition would counteract ambition: each branch would have both the motive and the means to resist encroachment from the others.

**3. veto**
> The presidential veto gives the executive a role in the legislative process; but Congress can override a veto with a two-thirds majority in both chambers, ensuring that no single branch has the final word.

**4. judicial review**
> Judicial review — the power of the Supreme Court to strike down laws as unconstitutional — was not explicitly stated in the Constitution; it was established by the Court itself in the landmark case Marbury v. Madison in 1803.

**5. executive branch**
> The executive branch's power over foreign policy and military command, combined with Congress's exclusive power to declare war and control the budget, creates a permanent structural tension that each generation of presidents and legislators must navigate.

**6. override**
> When Congress overrides a presidential veto, it demonstrates that legislative authority ultimately supersedes executive preference — but the two-thirds threshold means this happens relatively rarely.

**7. ratification**
> Senate ratification of treaties and presidential appointments creates one of the most significant checks on executive power — it means the president cannot make binding international commitments or fill key positions without legislative consent.

### Nobody Gets All the Power
#### The Architecture of American Constitutional Government

The Constitution created not just a new government but a new theory of government — one built on the assumption that those who held power would inevitably abuse it, and that the only reliable safeguard was to ensure that no single person or body ever held enough power to act without constraint.

This principle, which Madison described in Federalist No. 51, was embedded in every feature of the constitutional design. The government was divided into three branches, each with distinct functions and independent sources of authority. The legislative Congress made the laws; the executive president enforced them; the judicial courts interpreted them. But the separation was never clean — each branch was given tools to constrain the others.

The president could veto legislation, but Congress could override the veto with a two-thirds majority. The Senate confirmed presidential appointments and ratified treaties, giving the legislature a direct role in executive decisions. Congress controlled the budget, meaning the president could not spend money on programs Congress hadn't approved. The courts could strike down legislation as unconstitutional, but judges were appointed by the president and confirmed by the Senate.

The federal structure added another layer of distributed authority. Powers not granted to the federal government were reserved to the states — in theory. The precise boundary between federal and state authority has been contested continuously since 1787 and remains a live question today.

The system was explicitly designed to be slow and difficult. Making law required the agreement of both chambers of Congress and the president; major changes required constitutional amendments, which needed approval by two-thirds of both chambers and three-quarters of the states. The Founders were not trying to build an efficient government. They were trying to build a government that could not easily become tyrannical — accepting inefficiency as the price of safety.

Whether that trade-off remains the right one, in a world that often requires fast collective action, is a question Americans argue about to this day.

## ✏️ Part 3 — Vocabulary in Use

**1.** Use **"separation of powers"** to discuss whether concentrating authority in a single body is ever justified — for example, during a national emergency.

**2.** Use **"checks and balances"** to describe a situation — in a business, organization, or government — where the absence of oversight led to abuse.

**3.** Use **"veto"** to discuss the strategic uses of the veto power. When is vetoing a decision useful, and when does it simply obstruct?

**4.** Use **"judicial review"** to argue for or against the idea that unelected judges should have the power to strike down laws passed by elected legislators.

**5.** Use **"executive branch"** to discuss the tension between executive efficiency and democratic accountability.

**6.** Use **"override"** to describe a situation where a subordinate body's decision was reversed by a higher authority. Was the override justified?

**7.** Use **"ratification"** to argue whether requiring legislative approval for executive appointments strengthens or paralyzes democratic governance.

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** The text says the Founders built a system that was "slow and difficult" deliberately. In the modern world, is this still a reasonable trade-off? What kinds of decisions require speed that the American system cannot easily provide?

**2.** Madison argued that "ambition must be made to counteract ambition." Is this a cynical or realistic view of human nature as a basis for institutional design?

**3.** Judicial review — the power to strike down laws — was not in the Constitution but was established by the Court itself. What does it mean for an institution to claim a power not explicitly granted to it?

**4.** The boundary between federal and state authority has been "contested continuously since 1787." Does a constitution that leaves fundamental questions unresolved provide stability or create permanent conflict?

**5.** The system of checks and balances was designed to prevent tyranny. Has it succeeded? Can you think of moments when it failed or was under serious stress?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** Many modern democracies have parliamentary systems rather than the American separation-of-powers model. What are the key differences, and which do you think produces better government?

**2.** The American system is frequently described as "designed for gridlock" — making legislation hard to pass. Is gridlock always a problem, or is it sometimes a feature?

**3.** Korea's constitutional design has evolved through multiple constitutions. What features of the American system, if any, do you think Korea's designers have incorporated or deliberately avoided?

**4.** The text says the Founders accepted inefficiency as the price of safety. How do modern societies balance the need for responsive government with the need to prevent abuse of power?

**5.** Judicial review has become controversial when courts strike down popular legislation. What is the proper role of an unelected judiciary in a democratic system?
