### Example Sentences

**1. indigenous**
> When European settlers arrived in North America, they encountered hundreds of indigenous nations, each with distinct languages, governance structures, and spiritual traditions that pre-dated European civilization by thousands of years.

**2. colonization**
> The colonization of the Americas was not a single event but a centuries-long process that permanently transformed both the people who came and the societies they encountered.

**3. religious persecution**
> For the Pilgrims who sailed on the Mayflower in 1620, religious persecution in England was not an abstract threat — several of their leaders had already been imprisoned for their beliefs.

**4. Puritan**
> The Puritans who settled Massachusetts Bay Colony did not seek religious freedom in the modern sense; they sought the freedom to establish their own strict religious community — and were often intolerant of dissent within it.

**5. charter**
> The Virginia Company received a royal charter from King James I in 1606, granting it the right to establish settlements on the eastern coast of North America in exchange for a share of any profits.

**6. subsistence**
> The first winters in the new colonies were devastatingly hard; most settlers arrived with inadequate supplies and without the agricultural knowledge needed for subsistence in a climate very different from England's.

**7. sovereignty**
> From the perspective of indigenous nations, European claims of sovereignty over North American territory were simply incomprehensible — these lands already had legitimate governing authorities.

### Why They Crossed the Ocean
#### Motives, Myths, and the First Americans

When the Mayflower dropped anchor off the coast of present-day Massachusetts in November 1620, its 102 passengers were not the first Europeans to reach North America, nor were they the first English settlers. They were, however, among the most consequential arrivals in American history — partly because of what they built, and partly because of the stories later Americans told about them.

The continent they reached was not empty. An estimated ten to twenty million indigenous people lived in North America before European contact, organized into hundreds of distinct nations with sophisticated governance, trade networks, agriculture, and spiritual traditions. The assumption, long embedded in American mythology, that the colonists arrived in a "wilderness" was a convenient fiction. The land had been shaped for millennia by human habitation.

The European settlers came for varied and sometimes contradictory reasons. The Separatists aboard the Mayflower — the people Americans later called Pilgrims — had left England because they believed the Church of England was irredeemably corrupt and wished to practice their faith without interference. The larger Puritan migration of the 1630s, which brought twenty thousand settlers to Massachusetts, was similarly driven by religious dissatisfaction, though the Puritans did not simply want freedom to worship — they wanted to build what their leader John Winthrop called "a city upon a hill," a model godly community visible to the world.

Further south, the motives were frankly commercial. The Virginia colony, established at Jamestown in 1607, was a business venture backed by the Virginia Company of London. Its early settlers were not religious refugees but investors and adventurers seeking profit. They nearly starved to death — and would have, without the agricultural knowledge and food supplies provided by the neighboring Powhatan Confederacy, whose generosity was eventually repaid with dispossession and war.

These two strands — the idealistic and the commercial, the sacred and the mercenary — ran through American colonial history from the beginning. They were never fully reconciled, and their tension echoes in American identity to the present day.

## ✏️ Part 3 — Vocabulary in Use

**1.** Use **"indigenous"** to discuss what obligations modern nations have toward the descendants of the peoples they displaced.

**2.** Use **"colonization"** to describe a historical or contemporary situation where one group established control over another's territory. How was it justified at the time?

**3.** Use **"religious persecution"** to argue whether religious freedom is more secure today than in the seventeenth century, or simply threatened by different forces.

**4.** Use **"Puritan"** (or its modern equivalent) to describe a community that holds strict moral standards and enforces them on its members. What are the strengths and weaknesses of such a community?

**5.** Use **"charter"** to discuss what it means for a government or institution to formally authorize an activity. What accountability comes with that authorization?

**6.** Use **"subsistence"** to describe what you think life was like for the earliest settlers, and what qualities of character would have been essential for survival.

**7.** Use **"sovereignty"** to discuss a contemporary situation where competing claims of sovereignty — national, indigenous, or regional — create political conflict.

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** The text describes two motivations for colonization — religious idealism and commercial ambition — and calls them "never fully reconciled." Do you see both impulses still present in American culture and foreign policy today?

**2.** The Puritans wanted to build a model community, not simply escape persecution. What is the difference between fleeing something and building something new? Which motivation tends to produce more durable societies?

**3.** The text says the Jamestown colony "nearly starved to death" without indigenous assistance, yet that assistance was eventually repaid with "dispossession and war." How should we understand gratitude and betrayal at a civilizational scale?

**4.** The description of North America as a "wilderness" was called a "convenient fiction" in the text. How does a dominant society's narrative about an empty or uncivilized land justify what would otherwise be recognized as conquest?

**5.** The Pilgrims and Puritans sought religious freedom but were intolerant of dissent within their own communities. Is this a contradiction, or is it a common feature of communities built around strong shared values?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** The text opens with the observation that the "wilderness" narrative was a fiction. How do national myths shape a country's self-understanding — and what happens when those myths are challenged?

**2.** Many of the world's most significant migrations have been driven by religious persecution. Where do you see similar dynamics operating in the modern world?

**3.** The early colonists came as communities — groups of families sharing values and goals — rather than as isolated individuals. How does collective migration differ from individual immigration in terms of cultural impact?

**4.** The Virginia colony was essentially a corporate enterprise with a royal licence. What do you think about the role of profit motive in historical colonization — and in modern development in less economically powerful regions?

**5.** The text suggests that the tension between idealism and commercial interest is fundamental to American identity. Can you identify a similar foundational tension in Korean history or another national history you know well?
