### Example Sentences

**1. militia**
> The colonial militia was an unreliable fighting force — men who enlisted for short terms, returned home at harvest time, and frequently fled when faced with British regulars in open battle — but it served an essential function by keeping resistance alive between Continental Army operations.

**2. guerrilla tactics**
> Francis Marion, known as the "Swamp Fox," used guerrilla tactics in South Carolina that were ideally suited to the terrain and impossible for British regulars to counter effectively, tying down large forces with a small band of irregular fighters.

**3. morale**
> The winter at Valley Forge in 1777–78 reduced the Continental Army to near collapse — but it also forged the core of professional soldiers whose morale and discipline would sustain the war through its final years.

**4. alliance**
> France's entry into the war as an American ally in 1778 transformed the conflict from a colonial rebellion into a global war, forcing Britain to defend interests from the Caribbean to India simultaneously.

**5. continental army**
> Washington's central achievement as commander of the Continental Army was not any single victory but rather keeping the army in existence — understanding that as long as the army survived, the revolution survived.

**6. attrition**
> Washington's strategy was fundamentally one of attrition: he could not defeat the British in pitched battle, but he could make the cost of occupation too high to sustain politically in London.

**7. surrender**
> Cornwallis's surrender at Yorktown on October 19, 1781, was accompanied by his band playing "The World Turned Upside Down" — an acknowledgment that the unthinkable had occurred.

### Against All Odds
#### How the Continental Army Won a War It Had No Right to Win

By every conventional measure, the American colonies should have lost the Revolutionary War. Britain was the most powerful military empire in the world. Its professional army was superbly trained and equipped. Its navy was unmatched. The colonies, by contrast, had no standing army, no navy, no central government capable of raising revenue, and a civilian population divided in its loyalties — historians estimate that roughly one-third of colonists were Patriots, one-third Loyalists, and one-third indifferent.

Yet the colonies won. How?

The first reason was geography. Britain was fighting a war three thousand miles from home across an ocean, with supply lines that took months to traverse. Winning the war required not just defeating the Continental Army but pacifying a vast, largely hostile interior. Every British advance created a supply problem; every extended occupation stretched thin an army that could not easily be reinforced.

The second reason was leadership. George Washington was not the most brilliant tactician of the war, but he possessed two qualities that proved decisive: an understanding of what victory actually required, and the character to sustain that understanding through years of setback and near-collapse. Washington grasped that his primary objective was not to win battles but to keep his army alive. As long as the Continental Army existed, the revolution existed. He absorbed defeats at Brooklyn, White Plains, and Brandywine, retreating when necessary and striking only when conditions favoured him.

The third reason was France. The French alliance, secured partly by Benjamin Franklin's extraordinary diplomatic skill and partly by the American victory at Saratoga in 1777, transformed the strategic situation entirely. French troops, ships, and money proved essential at Yorktown in 1781, where the combined Franco-American force trapped Cornwallis's army and compelled the surrender that effectively ended the war.

## ✏️ Part 3 — Vocabulary in Use

**1.** Use **"militia"** to discuss the trade-offs between citizen soldiers and professional armies in terms of reliability, cost, and democratic accountability.

**2.** Use **"guerrilla tactics"** to explain why irregular warfare is such an effective equalizer for a weaker force facing a militarily superior opponent.

**3.** Use **"morale"** to describe how leadership — or its absence — affects the performance of a group under sustained pressure.

**4.** Use **"alliance"** to discuss what France gained from its alliance with the American colonies. Was it purely a strategic calculation against Britain?

**5.** Use **"continental army"** to discuss what it means to build a professional military institution from scratch during an active conflict.

**6.** Use **"attrition"** to describe a situation — military or otherwise — where the winning strategy was simply to outlast the opponent rather than to defeat them directly.

**7.** Use **"surrender"** to discuss the symbolism of a formal military surrender. What does the ceremony communicate beyond the military facts?

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** The text says Washington's genius was understanding that "keeping the army alive" was the objective. How does correctly identifying the true strategic objective change what counts as success?

**2.** France entered the war against Britain for strategic reasons, not out of commitment to American ideals. Does the motivation of an ally matter, as long as their military contribution is effective?

**3.** The text describes Britain's challenge as pacifying a "vast, largely hostile interior." What does this suggest about the limits of military power in achieving political objectives?

**4.** About one-third of colonists were Loyalists — opposed to independence. How should we think about the Revolutionary War as a civil conflict within colonial society, not just a war against Britain?

**5.** The war lasted eight years, through repeated defeats and near-collapses. What sustained the revolutionary cause through such prolonged adversity — ideology, leadership, material interest, or something else?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** The American Revolution is often cited as proof that a determined people can defeat a much stronger power. Is this lesson generally applicable, or were there specific conditions that made it possible?

**2.** Washington refused to become king when offered the opportunity. What does this tell us about the relationship between military victory and democratic governance?

**3.** The French alliance was decisive, yet France was itself an absolute monarchy. What does it tell us about international relations that ideological opposites can be strategic allies?

**4.** The Revolutionary War created the myth of the "citizen soldier" — the ordinary man who sets down his plough and takes up his musket to defend liberty. How has this myth shaped American political culture?

**5.** Korea has its own history of resisting foreign occupation and fighting for independence. How do the experiences compare? What is universal about resistance movements, and what is particular to each context?
