### Example Sentences

**1. precedent**
> Washington was acutely aware that his every decision set a precedent for all future presidents; he reportedly agonized over matters as seemingly minor as what to call himself, because there was no template to follow.

**2. executive power**
> Washington's exercise of executive power was measured and deliberate: he expanded presidential authority where the Constitution seemed to require it, but resisted the temptation — and there were many — to accumulate power beyond what the republic's health required.

**3. partisan**
> Washington despised partisan politics and warned against it explicitly in his Farewell Address, arguing that political parties would divide the nation into factions that placed party loyalty above national interest — a warning that proved entirely prophetic.

**4. cabinet**
> Washington's cabinet included Thomas Jefferson as Secretary of State and Alexander Hamilton as Secretary of the Treasury — men of brilliance and incompatible visions whose conflicts defined the first decade of American governance.

**5. neutrality**
> When war broke out between France and Britain in 1793, Washington declared American neutrality despite intense pressure from both Jefferson (who favoured France) and Hamilton (who favoured Britain), establishing the principle that the young republic would avoid entanglement in European wars.

**6. farewell address**
> Washington's Farewell Address warned against "permanent alliances" with foreign nations and cautioned that the "spirit of party" was democracy's most dangerous internal enemy — advice that has been selectively invoked in American political debate ever since.

**7. two-term tradition**
> Washington's voluntary retirement after two terms established a norm so powerful that no president violated it for a hundred and fifty years; when Franklin Roosevelt broke it in 1940, Congress responded by constitutionally limiting presidents to two terms.

### The Man Who Refused to Be King
#### Washington, Precedent, and the Foundations of American Democracy

When the Electoral College unanimously chose George Washington as the first president of the United States in 1789, they were doing more than selecting an executive — they were choosing the person whose decisions would define what the presidency meant. Washington understood this with unusual clarity. He had spent his career earning the trust of men who feared concentrated power, and he was not about to squander it.

The challenges were immediate and enormous. The Constitution provided a framework, but every specific question about how the executive branch would operate had to be decided from scratch: How would the president interact with Congress? How would the cabinet function? What would happen when the interests of different states conflicted? What would American foreign policy look like? Washington answered these questions through his actions, and his answers became precedents that constrained and guided every president who followed.

His most consequential decision was the one he made in 1796, when he chose not to seek a third term. Nothing in the Constitution required him to stop after two terms. Washington was enormously popular and could almost certainly have been re-elected for life. Instead, he retired, writing a Farewell Address that warned against the "spirit of party" and the dangers of foreign entanglement, and went home to Mount Vernon. The peaceful transfer of power that followed — to John Adams, Washington's vice president and political rival — demonstrated that republican government could actually work.

This act of voluntary retirement was, arguably, Washington's most significant contribution to American democracy. It established that the presidency was an office, not a person — that the republic's continuity did not depend on any single individual's continuation in power. For a new nation still uncertain whether self-government was possible, this was not a small thing.

The founding generation built institutions. Washington built norms. Both mattered enormously.

## ✏️ Part 3 — Vocabulary in Use

**1.** Use **"precedent"** to argue whether historical precedents should constrain future leaders or whether each generation has the right to make its own choices.

**2.** Use **"executive power"** to discuss what you think the appropriate limits of presidential or executive authority are in a democracy.

**3.** Use **"partisan"** to discuss when party loyalty serves democratic governance and when it damages it.

**4.** Use **"cabinet"** to discuss the value of having advisers with opposing views. How did the Jefferson-Hamilton rivalry both strengthen and complicate Washington's administration?

**5.** Use **"neutrality"** to argue whether a powerful nation can or should remain neutral when its allies are at war.

**6.** Use **"farewell address"** to discuss whether the warnings Washington delivered in 1796 — about partisanship and foreign alliances — have aged well or been overtaken by events.

**7.** Use **"two-term tradition"** to argue for or against term limits for executive leaders. Is limiting terms a democratic safeguard or an unnecessary constraint on democratic choice?

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** The text says Washington "built norms" while others "built institutions." What is the difference, and why are norms sometimes more important than rules?

**2.** Washington retired voluntarily when he could have served for life. What does this act of restraint require of a leader psychologically and morally? Is it realistic to expect most leaders to make the same choice?

**3.** Washington warned against political parties but could not prevent their formation. Is partisan politics inevitable in a democracy, or can it be managed?

**4.** The peaceful transfer of power — from Washington to Adams — seems unremarkable in retrospect but was genuinely uncertain at the time. What conditions make peaceful transfers of power possible?

**5.** Washington's Farewell Address warned against "permanent alliances." The United States subsequently became deeply involved in permanent alliances (NATO, for example). Has history vindicated or refuted Washington's warning?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** Washington is sometimes described as the "indispensable man" — that without him, the American experiment in self-government would have failed. Do you believe that individuals can be truly indispensable to historical outcomes?

**2.** The text distinguishes between institutions (formal rules and structures) and norms (informal expectations of behaviour). In your experience, which is more important to the functioning of an organization or society?

**3.** Washington warned specifically against the "spirit of party" — the tendency of political factions to place party loyalty above national interest. Looking at American politics today, how relevant does this warning seem?

**4.** The two-term norm held for over a century and a half without being legally required. What makes unwritten norms durable? What causes them to break down?

**5.** This series has covered the founding of America — from the first settlers to the first president. Looking at the full arc, what do you consider the most important legacy of this founding period for the modern United States?
