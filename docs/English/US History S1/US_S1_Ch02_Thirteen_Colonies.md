### Example Sentences

**1. plantation**
> The plantation economy of Virginia and the Carolinas required enormous amounts of unskilled agricultural labour, which planters initially attempted to supply through indentured servants and ultimately through the transatlantic slave trade.

**2. indentured servant**
> An indentured servant typically signed a contract of four to seven years in exchange for passage, food, and lodging — but the realities of colonial labour were often far harsher than the contract implied.

**3. mercantile**
> Under the mercantile system, the colonies were expected to supply raw materials to Britain and purchase finished goods in return — a relationship that enriched British manufacturers at the expense of colonial economic development.

**4. proprietor**
> William Penn received a proprietary grant from King Charles II in 1681, making him the proprietor of Pennsylvania — a colony he designed as a refuge for Quakers and other religious minorities.

**5. assembly**
> The Virginia House of Burgesses, established in 1619, was the first elected legislative assembly in the American colonies — a precedent for self-governance that colonists would invoke repeatedly in the decades ahead.

**6. cash crop**
> Tobacco became Virginia's defining cash crop in the early seventeenth century, transforming a struggling settlement into a profitable colony but making it wholly dependent on a single commodity and the labour to grow it.

**7. dissenter**
> Roger Williams, expelled from Massachusetts Bay Colony as a dissenter, founded Rhode Island on the principle that church and state should be entirely separate — a radical idea for its time.

### Thirteen Colonies, Two Worlds
#### The Geography of Difference in Early America

By the mid-eighteenth century, Britain had established thirteen distinct colonies along the eastern seaboard of North America. They shared a language, a legal tradition, and a nominal loyalty to the British Crown. In most other respects, they were remarkably different from one another — so different that it would take a major external crisis to convince them they had common interests worth fighting for.

The differences were rooted in geography and the economies it produced. In New England — Massachusetts, Connecticut, Rhode Island, and New Hampshire — the rocky soil and cold winters made large-scale agriculture impractical. Instead, the northern colonies developed economies based on fishing, shipbuilding, timber, and small-scale trade. Towns were compact and community-oriented; education was valued for religious reasons, producing relatively high literacy rates; and dissenting religious traditions encouraged a culture of argument and independent thought.

The middle colonies — New York, New Jersey, Pennsylvania, and Delaware — were more diverse. Pennsylvania in particular attracted waves of German, Dutch, and Scots-Irish settlers, creating a pluralistic society that resembled nothing else in the colonial world. Philadelphia became the largest city in British North America, a commercial hub connecting the agrarian interior to Atlantic trade routes.

The southern colonies — Virginia, Maryland, the Carolinas, and Georgia — developed along entirely different lines. Here the climate was warm enough for tobacco, rice, and indigo, and the plantation system emerged as the dominant economic model. Planters required large amounts of agricultural labour, which they supplied first through indentured servitude and then, increasingly, through the forced importation of enslaved Africans. By 1750, enslaved people constituted roughly forty percent of Virginia's population.

These two economic worlds — the small-scale commercial north and the plantation-based south — produced different social structures, different values, and different expectations of government. The tension between them would persist not just through the colonial period but through the Revolution, the founding of the republic, and ultimately the Civil War.

## ✏️ Part 3 — Vocabulary in Use

**1.** Use **"plantation"** to discuss the relationship between an economic model and the social structures it creates or requires.

**2.** Use **"indentured servant"** to discuss the ethical difference between voluntary contract labour and enslavement. Is the distinction meaningful when the conditions are equally harsh?

**3.** Use **"mercantile"** to explain how the British economic system shaped colonial attitudes toward independence.

**4.** Use **"proprietor"** to discuss what happens when enormous tracts of land or resources are granted to private individuals. What are the long-term consequences?

**5.** Use **"assembly"** to argue why representative governance — even in limited, imperfect forms — tends to create expectations that are difficult for rulers to suppress.

**6.** Use **"cash crop"** to discuss the risks of an economy that depends on a single agricultural or natural resource commodity.

**7.** Use **"dissenter"** to describe a situation — historical or contemporary — where disagreement with authority produced a new and ultimately valuable alternative.

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** The text says geography produced "different social structures, different values, and different expectations of government" in north and south. How much do you think physical environment shapes political culture?

**2.** The northern colonies developed high literacy rates partly for religious reasons — people needed to read the Bible. What does this suggest about the relationship between cultural values and economic development?

**3.** Pennsylvania's religious pluralism made it the most diverse colony. Do you think religious and ethnic diversity makes a society stronger, more fragile, or simply different?

**4.** The southern colonies shifted from indentured servitude to slavery as their primary labour source. What conditions made this transition possible, and what does it reveal about how societies justify moral compromises for economic reasons?

**5.** The text suggests the north-south tension was present from the very beginning of colonial America. Does the Civil War seem more or less inevitable when viewed against this long backdrop?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** The thirteen colonies were very different yet eventually united. What is the minimum shared identity that makes political union possible? What makes it durable?

**2.** The mercantile system — colonies exist to benefit the mother country — was the normal economic model of the era. Do you see modern equivalents of this relationship between powerful and less powerful nations?

**3.** Roger Williams founded Rhode Island on the principle of separating church and state — considered radical in the 1630s. How has that principle fared over the following four centuries, in America and elsewhere?

**4.** The plantation system required slavery to function. When an economy becomes dependent on an arrangement that is later recognized as morally catastrophic, how should subsequent generations reckon with that legacy?

**5.** The colonial period produced the first American legislative assemblies. What conditions, in your view, make self-governance possible? Is it primarily cultural, economic, or institutional?
