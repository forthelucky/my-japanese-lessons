### Example Sentences

**1. confederation**
> The Articles of Confederation created a confederation in the original sense: a league of sovereign states that cooperated voluntarily, not a national government with authority over individuals.

**2. sovereignty**
> Under the Articles, each state jealously guarded its sovereignty — refusing to allow Congress to levy taxes, regulate commerce, or raise a national army without unanimous state consent.

**3. ratify**
> The Articles required unanimous ratification by all thirteen states, and Maryland's refusal to ratify until 1781 — demanding that states cede their western land claims — delayed their implementation for four years.

**4. insolvency**
> The Continental Congress's insolvency was genuinely dangerous: without the power to tax, it could not pay soldiers, repay war debts, or maintain the basic functions of government.

**5. amendment**
> Any amendment to the Articles required unanimous agreement from all thirteen states — a threshold so high that it made reform effectively impossible.

**6. commerce**
> The absence of federal authority to regulate commerce meant that states erected tariff barriers against each other, creating something resembling thirteen separate foreign nations competing economically rather than a unified market.

**7. unanimous**
> The unanimous consent requirement for amendments was the Articles' fatal design flaw; it gave every state a veto over national reform, ensuring that narrow interests could always block changes serving the broader good.

### Too Weak to Govern
#### The Articles of Confederation and America's First Political Crisis

The Revolutionary War had barely ended before the United States faced its first existential crisis — not from a foreign power, but from its own governing document. The Articles of Confederation, ratified in 1781, had been designed by men who feared central authority above almost everything else. They had just fought a war against a distant government that they believed had trampled their rights. The last thing they wanted to create was another one.

The result was a government that could barely function. Congress had no power to tax — it could only request money from the states, which frequently ignored the requests. It could not regulate interstate commerce, leaving each state free to impose tariffs on goods from other states. It could not raise an army directly; it could only requisition troops from state militias. Any amendment required unanimous consent of all thirteen states, making reform essentially impossible.

The consequences were immediate and severe. The federal government was essentially bankrupt, unable to pay the soldiers who had fought the Revolution or service the war debts owed to France and domestic creditors. Veterans' protests turned violent: in Shays' Rebellion of 1786–87, Massachusetts farmers who had fought in the Revolution took up arms against state courts that were seizing their farms for debt. Congress had no army to suppress the rebellion; Massachusetts had to raise its own force.

Perhaps more fundamentally, the states behaved like competing nations. New York taxed goods passing through its ports from New Jersey and Connecticut. States made separate commercial treaties with foreign powers. There was no unified currency and no reliable way to enforce contracts across state lines. The promising new republic was beginning to look less like a nation than a dysfunctional alliance.

The crisis produced a consensus, at least among the commercial and political elite, that something had to change. The question was how much.

## ✏️ Part 3 — Vocabulary in Use

**1.** Use **"confederation"** to discuss the trade-off between loose cooperation and stronger union. When does a confederation become unworkable?

**2.** Use **"sovereignty"** to discuss why states or regions resist transferring authority to a central government, even when doing so would clearly serve their interests.

**3.** Use **"ratify"** to explain why the formal process of approval matters, even when the outcome seems predetermined.

**4.** Use **"insolvency"** to describe what happens when a government cannot pay its obligations. Is government insolvency fundamentally different from private insolvency?

**5.** Use **"amendment"** to discuss the design of the Articles' amendment process. Was unanimity a reasonable safeguard or a fatal flaw?

**6.** Use **"commerce"** to explain why the inability to regulate trade consistently across a large territory creates political as well as economic problems.

**7.** Use **"unanimous"** to discuss when unanimous consent requirements serve democratic values and when they enable minority obstruction.

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** The Articles' designers feared strong central government above all else. Given what they had just experienced under British rule, was this fear reasonable? At what point does reasonable caution become dysfunctional excess?

**2.** The text says any amendment required unanimous consent, making "reform essentially impossible." How should a governing document balance stability with the ability to adapt?

**3.** Shays' Rebellion — veterans taking up arms over debt — alarmed the political class. How does economic desperation after a war of liberation reveal the gap between political ideals and material realities?

**4.** The states behaved "like competing nations." What does this tell us about whether political unity is natural or requires deliberate institutional design to sustain?

**5.** The crisis persuaded elites that change was needed, but not necessarily ordinary people. Is top-down constitutional reform legitimate if it addresses real problems, even without broad popular demand?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** The European Union resembles the Articles of Confederation in some ways — a voluntary association of sovereign states, dependent on unanimous or near-unanimous agreement for major decisions. What lessons from the American experience might apply?

**2.** The federal government's inability to pay veterans who had fought for the country led directly to armed rebellion. What obligations does a government have to those who served it?

**3.** The Articles failed because they prioritized the freedom of states from central authority over the capacity of the central government to act. Is this always the wrong balance, or are there contexts where it makes sense?

**4.** The problems with the Articles became visible very quickly — within years of ratification. What does this suggest about the difficulty of designing governing institutions in advance, before the problems they will face are known?

**5.** The failure of the Articles led directly to the Constitutional Convention. Is it possible that a failed first attempt at governance was necessary — that the experience of dysfunction was essential to producing better design?
