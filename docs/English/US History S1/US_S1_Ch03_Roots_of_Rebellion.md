### Example Sentences

**1. taxation**
> The colonists' objection to British taxation was not primarily the amount — the taxes were actually quite modest — but the principle: Parliament was taxing people who had no representatives in Parliament.

**2. representation**
> "No taxation without representation" was less a demand for immediate political change than a statement of constitutional principle: legitimate government required the consent of the governed.

**3. boycott**
> The colonial boycott of British goods following the Townshend Acts was remarkably effective — not because it caused immediate economic pain, but because it demonstrated that the colonies could organize collective action across colonial boundaries.

**4. pamphlet**
> Thomas Paine's pamphlet Common Sense, published in January 1776, sold an estimated five hundred thousand copies in a population of three million — arguably the most widely read political text in American history.

**5. grievance**
> The colonists' grievances accumulated over more than a decade before armed conflict began; each new British measure seemed to confirm that the relationship between colony and mother country had become fundamentally exploitative.

**6. provocation**
> The Boston Massacre of 1770 was as much provocation manufactured by colonial propagandists as it was a genuine act of British aggression; the reality — British soldiers surrounded by an angry mob, firing in fear — was more complicated than Paul Revere's famous engraving suggested.

**7. colonial legislature**
> When the British Parliament dissolved colonial legislatures as punishment for resistance, it inadvertently radicalized the colonial political class, who concluded that their representative institutions were only as secure as British goodwill allowed.

### Tea, Taxes, and the Breaking Point
#### How Britain Turned Loyal Subjects into Revolutionaries

For most of the colonial period, the relationship between Britain and its American colonies was, if not harmonious, at least workable. The colonists thought of themselves as English people living abroad, entitled to the traditional rights of Englishmen. Britain, preoccupied with European wars and comfortable profits from colonial trade, largely left the colonies to govern themselves. This arrangement — historians call it "salutary neglect" — worked well enough for over a century.

It ended with the Seven Years' War. Britain's decisive victory over France in 1763 removed the French threat from North America but left Britain with enormous debts and an expanded empire requiring defense. The obvious solution, from London's perspective, was to require the colonists to pay a share. The result was a decade of escalating tension that ended in revolution.

The Stamp Act of 1765 imposed a tax on virtually every piece of printed paper in the colonies — newspapers, legal documents, playing cards. It was the first direct tax Britain had levied on the colonists without the involvement of their own legislatures, and the response was explosive. Colonial assemblies passed resolutions denying Parliament's right to tax them. Sons of Liberty groups formed across the colonies, intimidating tax collectors and organizing boycotts. The Stamp Act was repealed within a year — but Parliament simultaneously passed the Declaratory Act, asserting its absolute right to legislate for the colonies "in all cases whatsoever."

The fundamental disagreement was constitutional. British politicians argued that Parliament was sovereign and represented all British subjects, including colonists. Colonial leaders argued that only their own elected assemblies could legitimately tax them. Neither side was wrong, exactly — they simply held incompatible theories of government, and the collision between them was increasingly impossible to manage.

On December 16, 1773, a group of colonists disguised as Mohawk Indians boarded three ships in Boston Harbor and dumped 342 chests of British East India Company tea into the water. The Boston Tea Party was not a spontaneous act of rage — it was a carefully planned political demonstration. Britain's response — closing Boston's port and suspending Massachusetts's self-government — transformed a local conflict into a continental one.

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** Britain repealed the Stamp Act but simultaneously asserted unlimited legislative authority through the Declaratory Act. Was this a reasonable compromise or the worst of both worlds? What would a wiser policy have looked like?

**2.** The text says the colonists and the British held "incompatible theories of government." Is it possible to resolve a constitutional conflict through negotiation, or does incompatibility at the theoretical level make conflict inevitable?

**3.** "Salutary neglect" — leaving the colonies largely alone — produced loyalty. Active management produced rebellion. What does this suggest about the relationship between governance and legitimacy?

**4.** The Boston Tea Party was carefully planned but disguised as spontaneous. How does political theatre — staged events designed to look organic — function in creating revolutionary momentum?

**5.** The text notes that the taxes were "actually quite modest" in amount. This suggests the conflict was about principle rather than practical hardship. Are principled conflicts harder or easier to resolve than material ones?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** "No taxation without representation" remains a powerful political slogan. Are there modern situations where people are taxed or affected by decisions made by bodies they have no voice in?

**2.** Britain's attempt to impose taxes after a century of benign neglect backfired dramatically. What does this suggest about how established relationships of authority can be destabilized by sudden change?

**3.** The Sons of Liberty used intimidation as well as argument. At what point does political protest cross the line into coercion? How should democratic societies handle this question?

**4.** Thomas Paine's Common Sense was enormously influential in shifting colonial opinion toward independence. What qualities make political writing genuinely persuasive rather than merely argumentative?

**5.** The Seven Years' War debt was the trigger for the tax policies that caused the Revolution. In what ways do wars create political consequences that their architects do not foresee?
