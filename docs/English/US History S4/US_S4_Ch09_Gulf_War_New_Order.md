### Example Sentences

**1. unipolarity**
> The brief moment of American unipolarity after the Cold War — when no other nation could challenge US military or economic dominance — was an extraordinary historical anomaly, and like all such moments, it did not last.

**2. coalition**
> The Gulf War coalition assembled by President Bush included not just NATO allies but Arab states — Saudi Arabia, Egypt, Syria — whose participation was essential both militarily and politically, signalling that the UN mandate was genuinely international.

**3. new world order**
> Bush's "new world order" vision was deliberately vague but suggested a post-Cold War system in which the UN Security Council — no longer paralysed by Soviet vetoes — could act collectively against aggression, establishing rules-based international behaviour.

**4. precision weapons**
> The Gulf War's televised precision strikes — laser-guided bombs hitting bunker doors with remarkable accuracy — created a public impression of near-bloodless warfare that was partly accurate for Coalition forces and profoundly misleading about the war's full effects.

**5. UN resolution**
> The Security Council passed Resolution 678 authorising member states to use "all necessary means" to expel Iraq from Kuwait — the first time since Korea that the UN had collectively authorised the use of force against a member state's aggression.

**6. sovereignty**
> The intervention in Kuwait was justified as the defence of state sovereignty — the principle that borders should not be changed by force — but subsequent American interventions complicated this principle by questioning sovereignty's inviolability when governments committed atrocities against their own people.

**7. hegemony**
> American hegemony after the Cold War was exercised not just through military force but through the control of global institutions — the IMF, the World Bank, the UN Security Council — and through the dominance of the dollar as the world's reserve currency.

### The Sole Superpower Moment
#### The Gulf War, the New World Order, and American Unipolarity

On August 2, 1990, Iraqi forces under Saddam Hussein invaded and occupied Kuwait. The invasion was swift, brutal, and, from Saddam's perspective, calculatedly timed: the Cold War had just ended, the Soviet Union was dissolving, and he believed the United States, still cautious from its Vietnam-era aversion to casualties, would not respond militarily.

He was wrong. President George H.W. Bush assembled one of the most impressive diplomatic coalitions in modern history — thirty-four nations, including Arab states that had never cooperated militarily with the United States — and secured UN Security Council authorization for the use of force. Operation Desert Storm began on January 17, 1991. In forty-two days of air campaign followed by a hundred-hour ground war, Coalition forces expelled Iraqi forces from Kuwait. American casualties were under three hundred killed; Iraqi casualties were estimated in the tens of thousands.

The victory was overwhelming, and its conduct appeared to vindicate a generation of military reform that had rebuilt the US armed forces since Vietnam. Precision weapons, air dominance, and the devastating effectiveness of combined-arms armoured operations demonstrated a military capability that no other nation could approach. Bush declared a "new world order" — a post-Cold War system in which collective security through the UN would replace the paralysis of superpower confrontation.

The decision not to pursue Iraqi forces to Baghdad and remove Saddam Hussein was deliberate and principled: the UN mandate authorized expelling Iraq from Kuwait, not regime change. Secretary of State Baker and National Security Advisor Scowcroft later argued that toppling Saddam would have produced a power vacuum, fractured the coalition, and required an open-ended American occupation — precisely the scenario that unfolded when a later Bush administration made the opposite decision in 2003.

The Gulf War marked the peak of American post-Cold War confidence. What came after was more complicated.

## ✏️ Part 3 — Vocabulary in Use

**1.** Use **"unipolarity"** to discuss whether a world with a single dominant power is more or less stable than one with multiple great powers.

**2.** Use **"coalition"** to discuss what holds a military coalition together when its members have different interests and different stakes in the outcome.

**3.** Use **"new world order"** to discuss whether the concept of collective security through international institutions has been realised or has remained an aspiration.

**4.** Use **"precision weapons"** to discuss how the visibility of precision technology affects public understanding of what modern warfare actually costs.

**5.** Use **"UN resolution"** to discuss the conditions under which UN authorization provides genuine legitimacy to military action, and the conditions under which it does not.

**6.** Use **"sovereignty"** to discuss the tension between respecting state sovereignty and intervening to prevent atrocities against civilian populations.

**7.** Use **"hegemony"** to discuss what obligations — if any — a hegemon has toward the international system it dominates.

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** Bush chose not to pursue Iraqi forces to Baghdad. His advisers argued this restraint prevented a power vacuum and open-ended occupation. The 2003 invasion produced exactly this outcome. What does the comparison suggest about the value of clear, limited objectives?

**2.** The Gulf War coalition included Arab states that had never cooperated militarily with the US. What allowed this coalition to form? Why have subsequent coalitions been harder to assemble?

**3.** The text says American unipolarity was "an extraordinary historical anomaly." What created it, and what has eroded it?

**4.** Bush's "new world order" concept was vague. Is vagueness in political vision a flaw or a feature when building coalitions?

**5.** The Gulf War appeared to vindicate military reform since Vietnam. Did the "lessons learned" from Vietnam produce the right military for the Gulf War but the wrong military for subsequent conflicts?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** With the Cold War over, what should be the global responsibility of the world's only remaining superpower? Did the United States use its unipolar moment wisely?

**2.** The Gulf War was fought to defend state sovereignty — the principle that borders should not be changed by force. Is this principle still operative in international relations today?

**3.** Korea's security depends significantly on its alliance with the United States. How did the Gulf War and the post-Cold War period affect the nature of that alliance?

**4.** The "new world order" concept suggested that the UN could finally function as its founders intended, without Cold War paralysis. How has this vision fared over the past three decades?

**5.** American military supremacy, demonstrated in the Gulf War, was so overwhelming that it deterred direct military challenge. Has this supremacy changed the nature of conflict — pushing rivals toward asymmetric strategies rather than conventional military competition?
