### Example Sentences

**1. containment**
> George Kennan's containment doctrine argued that patient, firm resistance at every point of Soviet pressure would eventually cause the Soviet system to moderate or collapse — a prediction that proved accurate, forty years later.

**2. Iron Curtain**
> Churchill's 1946 speech in Fulton, Missouri, introduced "Iron Curtain" into the political vocabulary: from Stettin in the Baltic to Trieste in the Adriatic, he said, an iron curtain had descended across the continent.

**3. Truman Doctrine**
> The Truman Doctrine, announced in response to communist pressure on Greece and Turkey, committed the United States to supporting free peoples resisting subjugation — an open-ended pledge that would draw the US into conflicts across the globe for four decades.

**4. Marshall Plan**
> The Marshall Plan provided thirteen billion dollars to rebuild Western European economies — motivated by the conviction that economic despair, not political argument, was communism's most effective recruiting tool.

**5. sphere of influence**
> The post-war settlement tacitly recognised Soviet sphere of influence in Eastern Europe while the US dominated Western Europe and Japan — an informal division of the world that both superpowers maintained through a combination of military presence and economic leverage.

**6. arms race**
> The nuclear arms race consumed enormous resources on both sides, eventually producing arsenals capable of destroying human civilisation many times over — a situation strategists called Mutually Assured Destruction, or MAD.

**7. NATO**
> NATO's founding principle — that an attack on one member is an attack on all — was designed to deter Soviet aggression by making the cost of any military move against Western Europe prohibitively high.

### The World Divided in Two
#### The Iron Curtain, Containment, and the Architecture of the Cold War

World War II ended the era of European dominance and produced two superpowers whose mutual suspicion would define international relations for the next forty-five years. The United States and the Soviet Union had fought as allies against fascism; they emerged from the war with incompatible visions of what the post-war world should look like — and with military forces large enough to enforce their respective visions across enormous areas of the globe.

The break came quickly. At Yalta in February 1945, Roosevelt, Churchill, and Stalin had made agreements about the post-war settlement that each side interpreted differently. The Soviet Union, which had lost over twenty million people in the war, was determined to create a buffer zone of friendly governments in Eastern Europe. The United States interpreted the same agreements as committing the Soviets to democratic elections in liberated countries. Neither side was clearly wrong in its reading; the agreements were ambiguous enough to sustain both interpretations until the ambiguity ran out.

By 1947, the outlines of the Cold War were clear. Soviet-backed governments had consolidated control across Eastern Europe. Communist insurgencies threatened Greece and Turkey. George Marshall's State Department produced a plan for massive economic assistance to Western Europe. President Truman articulated what became the Truman Doctrine: the United States would support free peoples resisting subjugation by armed minorities or outside pressures.

The architecture that followed — NATO, the Berlin Airlift, the nuclear arms race — was built on a fundamental assessment: the Soviet Union was an expansionist power whose ambitions could only be checked by a credible American commitment to resist them. Whether this assessment was entirely accurate, historians still debate. What is not debated is that the commitment shaped the world for half a century.

## ✏️ Part 3 — Vocabulary in Use

**1.** Use **"containment"** to discuss whether preventing expansion, rather than rolling it back, is a coherent long-term strategy.

**2.** Use **"Iron Curtain"** to discuss how a single metaphor can shape political reality by giving a complex situation a memorable frame.

**3.** Use **"Truman Doctrine"** to argue whether an open-ended commitment to support "free peoples" everywhere is sustainable foreign policy.

**4.** Use **"Marshall Plan"** to discuss whether economic investment is a more effective tool against political radicalism than military force.

**5.** Use **"sphere of influence"** to discuss whether great powers are entitled to privileged positions in neighbouring regions.

**6.** Use **"arms race"** to explain how competitive escalation can leave both parties less secure despite spending more on defence.

**7.** Use **"NATO"** to discuss the principle of collective defence. Does the obligation to defend all allies make a nation more or less secure?

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** Both the US and USSR could justify their interpretation of the Yalta agreements. When both sides believe they are acting legitimately, how can such disputes be resolved?

**2.** The Marshall Plan was motivated by both humanitarian concern and strategic calculation. Does mixed motivation affect a policy's value?

**3.** The Truman Doctrine was an open-ended commitment. What were the long-term costs of this commitment, and were they worth it?

**4.** The Soviet Union demanded a buffer zone because of its wartime losses. Is this a reasonable security demand, or an unacceptable imposition on neighbouring peoples?

**5.** The Cold War divided the world into two blocs, leaving developing nations to be contested by proxy. How did this binary framework shape — and distort — international relations in Asia, Africa, and Latin America?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** The Cold War was described as a contest between capitalism and communism. Looking back, how much was genuinely ideological and how much was traditional great-power competition?

**2.** NATO's Article 5 collective defence commitment has been the cornerstone of European security for seventy-five years. Is this commitment still credible today?

**3.** Korea experienced the Cold War as a hot war — fought directly on its territory. How does this shape the Korean perspective on Cold War history?

**4.** The Marshall Plan is often cited as enlightened foreign policy — investing in the prosperity of potential adversaries. Are there contemporary situations where a similar approach might work?

**5.** The Cold War produced a stable — if dangerous — world order based on mutual deterrence. The post-Cold War world has been less stable in some respects. What did the Cold War framework provide that has been difficult to replace?
