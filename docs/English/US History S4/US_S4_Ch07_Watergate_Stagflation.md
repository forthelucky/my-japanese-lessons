### Example Sentences

**1. Watergate**
> Watergate demonstrated that it was not the original crime — a clumsy break-in that Nixon may not have personally ordered — but the cover-up that destroyed his presidency: the abuse of executive power to obstruct justice was a far more serious constitutional offence.

**2. impeachment**
> The House Judiciary Committee had approved articles of impeachment against Nixon in July 1974; faced with their near-certain passage in the full House and conviction in the Senate, Nixon became the only president in American history to resign from office.

**3. stagflation**
> Stagflation confounded the economic models of the early 1970s: the standard tools for fighting inflation — raising interest rates, cutting spending — made the unemployment problem worse, while the standard tools for fighting recession — cutting rates, increasing spending — made inflation worse.

**4. oil shock**
> The 1973 oil shock — OPEC's embargo in response to American support for Israel in the Yom Kippur War — quadrupled oil prices overnight, triggering inflation across all sectors of an economy built on cheap energy.

**5. cover-up**
> The cover-up of Watergate involved the payment of hush money, the destruction of evidence, the firing of the special prosecutor, and the use of the CIA and FBI to obstruct the investigation — abuses that went far beyond the original break-in.

**6. accountability**
> Nixon's resignation, and the subsequent pardon by President Ford, raised a question that has never been satisfactorily answered: does accountability require that even a president face criminal prosecution for crimes committed in office?

**7. constitutional crisis**
> The Saturday Night Massacre — Nixon's firing of the special prosecutor and the resulting resignations of the attorney general and deputy attorney general — was a genuine constitutional crisis, a direct challenge by the executive to the independence of the justice system.

### When the System Failed Itself
#### Watergate, Stagflation, and the Collapse of 1970s Confidence

The 1970s were, by almost any measure, the worst decade for American institutional confidence since the Great Depression. The decade began with the continuation of the Vietnam War, the credibility gap already well established. It added the first forced resignation of an American president, two oil shocks that destroyed the post-war economic model, and an unemployment and inflation crisis that neither mainstream Keynesian nor monetarist economics could explain or manage.

Watergate began with a relatively minor crime — the June 1972 break-in at the Democratic National Committee headquarters at the Watergate complex in Washington, carried out by operatives connected to the Nixon re-election campaign. Nixon himself had won re-election by a landslide that autumn. What destroyed his presidency was not the break-in but the cover-up: the payment of hush money, the obstruction of investigations, and — most fatally — the tape-recorded conversations that revealed Nixon had known about the cover-up within days of the break-in and actively directed it.

The constitutional drama played out over two years, culminating in Nixon's resignation in August 1974. For all its ugliness, the system worked in one sense: the press, the courts, the special prosecutor, and ultimately Congress forced the accountability that the executive branch had tried to prevent. But the cost was enormous. Trust in government, already damaged by Vietnam, fell to levels from which it has never fully recovered.

The economic crisis was less dramatic but more structurally damaging. The 1973 OPEC oil embargo quadrupled energy prices and exposed the deep dependence of the American economy on cheap oil. The resulting combination of high inflation and rising unemployment — stagflation — defied the economic models that had guided policy for thirty years. The Federal Reserve raised interest rates to crushing levels to break the inflationary psychology, producing a severe recession in 1980–82. The Keynesian consensus that had shaped economic policy since the New Deal was broken.

## ✏️ Part 3 — Vocabulary in Use

**1.** Use **"Watergate"** to discuss why cover-ups of relatively minor crimes so often produce more severe political consequences than the original offence.

**2.** Use **"impeachment"** to discuss what the impeachment process is designed to achieve. Is it primarily a legal mechanism, a political one, or both?

**3.** Use **"stagflation"** to explain why an economic problem that violates standard theoretical models is particularly difficult for policymakers to address.

**4.** Use **"oil shock"** to discuss how a nation's dependence on imported energy creates both economic and geopolitical vulnerability.

**5.** Use **"cover-up"** to discuss the psychology of cover-ups. Why do people in positions of power so often make the original problem worse by attempting to conceal it?

**6.** Use **"accountability"** to argue whether Gerald Ford's pardon of Nixon served or damaged the principle of equal accountability under the law.

**7.** Use **"constitutional crisis"** to discuss what conditions transform a political scandal into a crisis for the constitutional system itself.

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** The text says the system "worked in one sense" during Watergate — Nixon was held accountable — but at enormous cost to institutional trust. How should we evaluate a system that functions correctly but is severely damaged in the process?

**2.** Nixon won re-election by a landslide the same year as the Watergate break-in. What does this tell us about the limits of electoral accountability for executive misconduct?

**3.** Stagflation defied the economic models that had shaped policy for thirty years. What does this reveal about the relationship between economic theory and economic reality?

**4.** The 1973 oil embargo was a response to US foreign policy in the Middle East. How does dependence on imported resources create vulnerabilities that extend far beyond economics?

**5.** Nixon's tapes — recordings of Oval Office conversations — provided the decisive evidence against him. Did the tapes' existence reflect democratic transparency or an arrogant assumption of impunity?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** The Watergate crisis produced major reforms in campaign finance, government ethics, and congressional oversight. Were these reforms adequate? How have they held up?

**2.** "Watergate" has become a suffix attached to almost every subsequent political scandal. Does this usage trivialise the original, or does it reflect genuine structural similarities?

**3.** Korea experienced its own presidential corruption scandals leading to impeachment and removal. How does Korea's constitutional response to executive misconduct compare to the American experience?

**4.** The oil shock of 1973 demonstrated how energy dependence becomes a geopolitical vulnerability. How have countries' energy strategies changed since then, and how effectively have they managed this vulnerability?

**5.** How vital is the moral integrity and transparency of a leader to the stability of a nation? Is this primarily a moral question or a practical one?
