### Example Sentences

**1. civil rights**
> The civil rights movement sought not new rights but the enforcement of rights already guaranteed by the Constitution — the Fourteenth Amendment's equal protection clause had been ratified in 1868, yet nearly a century later, Black Americans in the South could not eat at the same lunch counter as white Americans.

**2. segregation**
> The Supreme Court's 1954 ruling in Brown v. Board of Education declared school segregation unconstitutional, overturning the "separate but equal" doctrine of Plessy v. Ferguson — but the decision required years of pressure to implement and was bitterly resisted across the South.

**3. nonviolent resistance**
> Martin Luther King's commitment to nonviolent resistance was not merely tactical — it was deeply theological, rooted in his understanding of Christian love and Gandhi's philosophy — and it was strategically sophisticated: it exposed the violence of the system it challenged.

**4. civil disobedience**
> Rosa Parks's refusal to give up her bus seat in Montgomery, Alabama, in December 1955 was an act of civil disobedience that triggered a year-long bus boycott — demonstrating that sustained economic pressure could force institutional change.

**5. legislation**
> The Civil Rights Act of 1964 and the Voting Rights Act of 1965 were landmark legislation, but they were the product of years of street-level pressure, television images of police brutality, and political calculation by President Lyndon Johnson.

**6. momentum**
> After the March on Washington in August 1963, when two hundred and fifty thousand people gathered to hear King deliver "I Have a Dream," the movement had achieved a moral momentum that made it difficult for Congress to resist legislation.

**7. march**
> The Selma to Montgomery marches of 1965 — during which state troopers attacked peaceful marchers with clubs and tear gas on the Edmund Pettus Bridge — were broadcast on national television and galvanised public support for voting rights legislation.

### The Long March to Equality
#### The Civil Rights Movement and the Second American Revolution

Nearly a century after the Civil War and the abolition of slavery, Black Americans in the South lived under a comprehensive system of legal discrimination — segregated schools, buses, restaurants, and water fountains; barriers to voting through poll taxes and literacy tests; and the constant threat of racial violence, largely unpunished by white-dominated law enforcement. The civil rights movement of the 1950s and 1960s was the organised effort to dismantle this system through nonviolent protest, legal challenge, and political pressure.

The movement's strategic genius lay in choosing tactics that exposed the contradiction between American democratic ideals and the reality of Southern apartheid. When police in Birmingham, Alabama, turned fire hoses and attack dogs on peaceful child protesters in 1963, the images were broadcast around the world. When state troopers beat marchers on the Edmund Pettus Bridge in Selma in 1965, the footage reached living rooms across America that evening. The violence of the system was made visible — and visibility was itself a form of political power.

Martin Luther King's "I Have a Dream" speech at the March on Washington in August 1963 was the movement's moral high point — an extraordinary act of political rhetoric that reached beyond the specific demands of the moment to articulate a vision of America's unfulfilled promise. But the movement was not built on speeches alone. The Montgomery Bus Boycott had demonstrated sustained economic pressure. Sit-ins at lunch counters had forced desegregation through personal risk and dignified endurance. Freedom Riders had challenged interstate segregation by riding buses into violent confrontation in Alabama and Mississippi.

The Civil Rights Act of 1964 outlawed discrimination based on race, colour, religion, sex, or national origin. The Voting Rights Act of 1965 dismantled the systematic mechanisms of Black disenfranchisement in the South. These were legislative victories of the first order — but they were the product of a decade of organised effort, accepted suffering, and moral clarity that changed the country more profoundly than any legislation alone could achieve.

## ✏️ Part 3 — Vocabulary in Use

**1.** Use **"civil rights"** to distinguish between rights that exist on paper and rights that are effectively enforced. What bridges this gap?

**2.** Use **"segregation"** to discuss how a legal system of separation creates psychological as well as material harm.

**3.** Use **"nonviolent resistance"** to argue for or against the proposition that nonviolent tactics are always more effective than violent ones in achieving social change.

**4.** Use **"civil disobedience"** to discuss when breaking the law is morally justified. What conditions does an unjust law need to meet for civil disobedience to be appropriate?

**5.** Use **"legislation"** to discuss the relationship between law and social change. Does legislation cause cultural change, or must culture change first?

**6.** Use **"momentum"** to describe how social movements build and maintain energy. What sustains a movement through its inevitable setbacks?

**7.** Use **"march"** to discuss the political function of public demonstrations. What does marching accomplish that petitions, lobbying, or voting cannot?

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** The text says making violence visible was "itself a form of political power." How does the presence of cameras and media change the dynamics of political protest?

**2.** King's nonviolent strategy was both principled and tactical — it exposed the system's violence. Do you think it would have been as effective without the brutality of the white Southern response?

**3.** The Civil Rights Act of 1964 was a legislative landmark but was built on years of street-level pressure. What does this suggest about the relationship between social movements and formal politics?

**4.** The movement succeeded in dismantling legal segregation but did not end racial inequality. How should we evaluate a movement that achieves its formal goals but leaves deeper structural problems intact?

**5.** The text describes several forms of protest — boycotts, sit-ins, marches, Freedom Rides. Which do you think was most strategically effective, and why?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** The civil rights movement used nonviolent resistance to achieve profound change. Can you think of a contemporary social movement that uses similar tactics? How effective have they been?

**2.** King argued that unjust laws must be openly broken and the consequences accepted. Does this principle apply to civil disobedience in modern democratic societies?

**3.** The movement's power came partly from moral clarity — the obvious injustice of what it opposed. Are contemporary social justice movements similarly clear about what they oppose? Does clarity of opposition help?

**4.** Korea's democracy movement in the 1980s used mass protest and civil disobedience to achieve democratic transition. How do you compare this to the American civil rights movement?

**5.** The civil rights legislation of 1964–65 is fifty years old. What racial inequalities persist in America today that law has not been able to address? What tools — legal, social, economic — might be more effective?
