### Example Sentences

**1. Reaganomics**
> Reaganomics produced the longest peacetime economic expansion in American history to that point, but also tripled the national debt — a contradiction that supporters attributed to insufficient spending cuts and critics attributed to the fundamental flaw in supply-side theory.

**2. supply-side economics**
> Supply-side economics — the argument that tax cuts for corporations and the wealthy would generate growth that would "trickle down" to workers — was accepted enthusiastically by those who stood to benefit most directly from the tax reductions.

**3. deficit**
> Reagan's combination of tax cuts and defence spending increases produced enormous deficits that contradicted his stated commitment to fiscal conservatism — suggesting that the deficit was less a problem to be solved than a weapon to be used against social spending.

**4. deregulation**
> The deregulation of the savings and loan industry in the early 1980s, combined with federal deposit insurance, created a moral hazard that produced a wave of reckless lending and ultimately the most expensive financial bailout in American history to that point.

**5. Strategic Defence Initiative**
> Whether the Strategic Defence Initiative was technically feasible mattered less than its strategic effect: it forced Soviet military planners to consider the possibility of a missile shield, adding a dimension of uncertainty to nuclear calculations that the Soviets could not afford to ignore.

**6. glasnost**
> Gorbachev's glasnost policy released forces he could not control: once Soviet citizens were permitted to discuss their government's failures openly, the accumulated grievances of seventy years of communist rule overwhelmed the system's capacity to manage them.

**7. Berlin Wall**
> The fall of the Berlin Wall on November 9, 1989 — not ordered by any authority, simply not stopped — was the most powerful symbol of the Cold War's end: millions of people pouring through checkpoints that, hours earlier, guards had been authorised to defend with lethal force.

### The Wall Comes Down
#### Reagan, the Cold War's Final Chapter, and the Soviet Collapse

Ronald Reagan entered the White House in January 1981 with three convictions: that the federal government was too large and spent too much; that the Soviet Union was an "evil empire" that needed to be confronted rather than accommodated; and that American confidence, battered by Vietnam and Watergate, needed to be restored. Over eight years, he pursued all three with varying success.

Economically, Reagan cut the top marginal income tax rate from seventy percent to twenty-eight percent, reduced domestic spending, and dramatically increased defence spending. The resulting combination produced both a genuine economic expansion — unemployment fell substantially from its 1982 recession peak — and a tripling of the national debt. The promised "trickle-down" effects of tax cuts for the wealthy produced measurable gains in corporate profits and stock markets; their effects on working-class wages were more modest.

In foreign policy, Reagan's approach to the Soviet Union combined confrontation with selective engagement. The Reagan Doctrine provided support to anti-communist guerrillas fighting Soviet-backed governments in Afghanistan, Angola, and Nicaragua. The Strategic Defence Initiative — the "Star Wars" missile defence system — added technological uncertainty to Soviet strategic calculations. Military spending increased dramatically.

But the Cold War ended for reasons that went beyond American policy. Mikhail Gorbachev, who became Soviet General Secretary in 1985, recognised that the Soviet system was failing economically and sought to reform it through glasnost (openness) and perestroika (restructuring). He released forces he could not control. Once Soviet citizens could discuss their government's failures openly, the accumulated grievances proved irresistible. Eastern European communist governments collapsed in rapid succession through 1989. On November 9, the Berlin Wall fell — not ordered down by anyone, simply not defended.

The Soviet Union itself dissolved on December 25, 1991. The Cold War was over.

## ✏️ Part 3 — Vocabulary in Use

**1.** Use **"Reaganomics"** to discuss whether economic growth that benefits primarily higher income groups represents a policy success or failure.

**2.** Use **"supply-side economics"** to explain the "trickle-down" theory. What evidence exists for and against this proposition?

**3.** Use **"deficit"** to discuss whether government deficit spending is ever fiscally responsible.

**4.** Use **"deregulation"** to explain the mechanism by which removing regulations in one context can produce systemic risks elsewhere.

**5.** Use **"Strategic Defence Initiative"** to discuss how the announcement of a technology that may not work can nonetheless have real strategic effects.

**6.** Use **"glasnost"** to discuss how greater transparency can unleash forces that the system introducing it is unable to manage.

**7.** Use **"Berlin Wall"** to discuss what it means for a symbol to fall — the political significance beyond the physical event.

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** Reagan tripled the national debt while reducing domestic spending. How do you evaluate a fiscal philosophy that claims to oppose deficits but produces them?

**2.** The text argues the Cold War ended for reasons "beyond American policy." Did Reagan win the Cold War, or did the Soviet Union simply collapse from within?

**3.** Gorbachev intended to reform the Soviet system, not destroy it. What does it tell us about political systems that reforms intended to save them can accelerate their collapse?

**4.** The Berlin Wall fell "not ordered down by anyone, simply not defended." What does this suggest about how authoritarian systems can collapse suddenly after appearing stable?

**5.** Reagan's foreign policy included support for anti-communist groups in Afghanistan. Among those supported were figures who later became part of the Taliban. How should we evaluate Cold War-era decisions in light of their long-term consequences?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** Reaganomics reduced top marginal tax rates dramatically. The debate about tax policy — how much to tax, whom to tax, and what for — continues today. What principles should guide this debate?

**2.** The Soviet Union collapsed suddenly despite appearing stable. What does this pattern — sudden collapse after apparent stability — tell us about how authoritarian systems hide their vulnerabilities?

**3.** Korea's relations with the US were shaped by the Reagan era and the Cold War's final decade. How did the Cold War's end change Korea's strategic situation?

**4.** The fall of the Berlin Wall is one of the twentieth century's most powerful images of popular liberation. Can you think of comparable moments — when a physical symbol of oppression was removed by popular action?

**5.** Reagan is celebrated by American conservatives as a transformative figure who restored American confidence and won the Cold War. How do you evaluate this historical assessment?
