### Example Sentences

**1. globalization**
> The globalization of the 1990s was American-driven in both its institutional framework — NAFTA, the WTO, the IMF's structural adjustment programmes — and its cultural content — Hollywood, McDonald's, Microsoft — creating what critics called a form of soft imperialism.

**2. dot-com bubble**
> The dot-com bubble of the late 1990s was fuelled by a genuine insight — that internet commerce would transform the economy — but executed with the speculative excess that typically accompanies transformative technologies: companies with no revenue were valued in the billions based on projected future dominance.

**3. free trade**
> The Clinton administration's enthusiastic promotion of free trade — through NAFTA, the Uruguay Round, and China's WTO accession — was based on the economist's consensus that free trade raised aggregate welfare, which was correct; it was less attentive to how the gains and losses were distributed within countries.

**4. venture capital**
> Silicon Valley's venture capital ecosystem was the engine of the dot-com era: it funded thousands of experiments, most of which failed, but a small number of which — Amazon, Google, eBay — generated returns that justified the entire portfolio.

**5. network effect**
> The network effect that benefited early internet platforms was extraordinarily powerful: each new user of eBay made the platform more valuable to all existing users, creating barriers to competition that conventional economics had not adequately theorised.

**6. NAFTA**
> NAFTA eliminated most trade barriers between the United States, Canada, and Mexico, producing significant economic gains for consumers and export-oriented industries while accelerating the movement of manufacturing jobs to lower-wage Mexico — a distributional consequence that became politically explosive twenty years later.

**7. Pax Americana**
> The 1990s represented the high point of Pax Americana: American military dominance was unchallengeable, the global economy was integrating rapidly on terms favourable to American corporations and financial institutions, and American cultural products dominated global media.

### When America Wired the World
#### The 1990s, Globalization, and the Dot-Com Revolution

The 1990s were, for the United States, a decade of extraordinary confidence. The economy expanded for 120 consecutive months — the longest peacetime expansion in American history. The budget deficit that had ballooned under Reagan was converted to a surplus under Clinton. Unemployment fell below four percent. The NASDAQ, home to technology companies, tripled in value between 1997 and 2000. American culture — films, music, fashion, business models — spread globally with a reach that no previous civilization had achieved.

The twin engines of this prosperity were globalization and the internet. Under Clinton, the United States actively promoted a rules-based international trading system through NAFTA, the completion of the Uruguay Round of trade negotiations, and ultimately China's accession to the World Trade Organization. The theory was straightforward: free trade raises aggregate economic output; countries specialise in what they do best; everyone benefits. The practice was more complicated. Manufacturing jobs moved to lower-wage countries. Communities built around specific industries hollowed out. The aggregate gains were real, but their distribution was deeply unequal.

The internet transformed more than commerce. The World Wide Web, developed from a tool for academic communication into a mass medium within the space of five years, created new industries, destroyed old ones, and connected the world in ways that no one had fully anticipated. Companies that had not existed in 1994 — Amazon, Google, eBay, Yahoo — were worth billions of dollars by 1999. Venture capital flooded into anything that ended in ".com," and the distinction between genuine business models and speculative fantasy was temporarily obscured.

The NASDAQ peak of March 2000 was followed by a crash that erased five trillion dollars in market value over two years. Many dot-com companies vanished entirely; the survivors — Amazon, Google — emerged stronger, having outlasted less-focused competitors. The economic and social transformations the internet produced survived the bubble: the way Americans shopped, communicated, consumed information, and conducted business had been permanently altered.

## ✏️ Part 3 — Vocabulary in Use

**1.** Use **"globalization"** to argue whether the 1990s version of globalization was genuinely beneficial for all participants or primarily designed to serve American and Western interests.

**2.** Use **"dot-com bubble"** to discuss the recurring pattern by which genuine technological transformations generate speculative excess.

**3.** Use **"free trade"** to discuss the tension between aggregate economic gains from trade and the concentrated losses experienced by specific workers and communities.

**4.** Use **"venture capital"** to discuss the role of risk capital in driving technological innovation. Is this system replicable outside the US context?

**5.** Use **"network effect"** to explain why early internet platforms achieved near-monopoly positions and why it has been difficult for competitors to displace them.

**6.** Use **"NAFTA"** to discuss the political backlash against free trade agreements that built over twenty years. Was this backlash justified?

**7.** Use **"Pax Americana"** to discuss whether American global dominance was benign, coercive, or somewhere in between for the non-American world.

## 💬 Part 4 — Discussion: Text-Related Questions

**1.** The text says free trade raised aggregate welfare but its gains were "deeply unequally distributed." Should a policy be implemented if it benefits some people substantially while harming others significantly?

**2.** Many dot-com companies with no revenue were valued in the billions. How does genuine technological transformation generate speculative excess, and how should investors distinguish between the two?

**3.** The internet was "developed from a tool for academic communication into a mass medium within five years." What made such rapid diffusion possible, and what social conditions enable or impede technological adoption at that speed?

**4.** The 1990s prosperity was partly built on a bubble. How should policymakers evaluate economic expansions — by their duration and magnitude, or also by their sustainability?

**5.** The text describes the 1990s as the "high point of Pax Americana." What has changed since then, and how did the decade's decisions contribute to subsequent challenges to American dominance?

## 🗣️ Part 5 — Free Discussion: Broader Themes

**1.** The internet was invented with public money through DARPA and the NSF, then commercialised by private companies. How should society manage the transition from public investment to private exploitation of technological platforms?

**2.** The 1990s globalisation model is now under significant political stress. What went wrong, and how should a more durable form of international economic integration be designed?

**3.** Korea benefited significantly from globalisation in the 1990s — its exports grew dramatically and Samsung, Hyundai, and LG became global brands. How does Korea's experience of globalisation compare to the American experience?

**4.** The dot-com bubble collapsed in 2000, destroying enormous paper wealth but leaving behind real infrastructure — fibre networks, e-commerce platforms, search engines — that transformed the subsequent economy. Is the bubble retrospectively justifiable?

**5.** The 1990s internet transformed how Americans consumed information. Looking at the political and social consequences of this transformation — misinformation, polarization, loss of shared reality — do you think the internet has been a net positive or net negative for democracy?
